Matter is all around us. It can be a thing we see, touch, or feel. Matter is made up of tiny bits called particles. These particles are too small to see, but they make up everything. Matter comes in three main forms: solid, liquid, and gas.

Solids: Solids are things that keep their shape. Think of a rock. It is hard and does not flow. A toy car is a solid too. You can hold it, and it keeps its shape. The particles in a solid are packed tightly together. They do not move much. This is why solids keep their shape.

Liquids: Liquids do not keep their shape. They take the shape of the cup or bowl they are in. Water is a liquid. Juice is a liquid too. You can pour them, and they will flow. The particles in a liquid are close but can move around each other. This is why liquids can flow.

Gases: Gases are all around us. Air is a gas. We cannot see it, but we can feel it when the wind blows. Gases can spread out to fill a space. If you open a soda can, the bubbles are gas. The particles in a gas are far apart and move fast. This is why gases can fill up a room quickly.

Changing States: Matter can change its form. This is called a state change.

  1. Melting: When a solid gets warm, it can melt. Ice is a solid. When it gets warm, it turns to water, a liquid. The particles move faster and spread out.

  2. Freezing: When a liquid gets very cold, it can freeze. Water in the freezer turns to ice. Ice is the solid form of water. The particles slow down and pack close.

  3. Condensation: When gas cools down, it can turn into a liquid. Think of a cold glass of water. Drops of water form on the outside. That is gas turning to liquid. The particles slow down and come closer.

  4. Evaporation: When a liquid gets warm, it can turn into gas. If you leave a wet shirt in the sun, the water will dry. The water turns to gas and goes into the air. The particles move faster and spread out.

  5. Sublimation: Some solids can turn right into gas. Dry ice is one. It skips the liquid step and turns into gas. The particles speed up a lot and spread out fast.

Physical vs. Chemical Changes:

A physical change is when the look or feel of something changes, but it is still the same thing. If you cut paper, it is still paper. If you melt ice, it is still water. The particles stay the same, but they move in new ways.

A chemical change is when a new thing is made. If you burn paper, it turns to ash. Ash is not paper. It is something new. The particles break apart and join in new ways. Other examples are rusting metal and baking a cake.

In summary, matter is all around us in three states: solid, liquid, and gas. It can change states by melting, freezing, condensing, evaporating, or sublimating. Physical changes keep the same stuff. Chemical changes make new stuff.
