A Monk's Guide to Happiness by Gelong Thubten

You’re like a house, Leave the front door and back door open, Allow your thoughts to come and go. Just don’t serve them tea.

We could focus on our body, our breathing, listening to sounds, or looking at a visual object. This helps us to be present, and it teaches us how not to get too entangled in our thoughts. We train in returning to our chosen focus when we realize our minds have wandered.

Where to seat? An ideal position would be with a wall or the corner of the room behind you, providing a sense of security and stability. Your feet are parallel, flat on the floor, and your hands can be in your lap, resting with the palms up—right hand on top of the left, the tips of the thumbs just touching—or place your hands on your legs, with the palms down on your knees or thighs.

It’s better if your eyes are open, not looking around the room, but gazing into the space in front of you, perhaps at an angle downward, and of course you should blink whenever you need to. You’re not particularly focusing on anything, you’re just looking into space. Meditating with the eyes open is a statement that everything is fine just as it is, that there is no need to get rid of anything.

Guidance is not so great, however, when it becomes a “crutch”; after some time you could grow dependent on the person’s voice, which means you’re not actually doing the meditation for yourself—your mind wanders and it is the voice, not you, that brings you back to the focus. So it is best to then go “solo,” like taking the training wheels off a bicycle.

Cortisol levels are highest in the morning—we experience a spike in the hormone to help our body move from the sleep state to the waking state—so it’s good to bring the levels back to normal with meditation before starting the day.

The mind drifts away, and all you need to do is gently capture your attention and bring it back, using your body as the anchor.
