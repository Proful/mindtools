### What is matter?

The different things which we see around us is matter. It exists in different physical forms. Each state can be interconverted. This is the physical change in the state of matter. Chemical change alters the composition of matter.

Look at the things around you. You see a large variety of things with different shapes, sizes and appearances (Fig. 1). Of these, some are living like plants and animals while some are non-living and are natural like air, water, sun or man-made like plastic, rubber, steel, etc. They are all made of different kinds of materials. They have mass and occupy space. *Anything that occupies space and has mass is called matter.* Thus all the things in our surrounding are made up of matter.

### COMPOSITION OF MATTER

Matter is made up of very small particles. These particles are **atoms**, **ions** or **molecules**. Atom is the smallest particle of matter and has all its properties. Usually, atoms of most of the materials do not have independent existence, thus they combine with one another to form molecules. Thus, molecules are the smallest distinguishable unit into which a matter can be divided. It exhibits all the properties of that kind of matter.

Two or more than two, like or unlike atoms combine to form a molecule. For example, molecule of oxygen, carbon dioxide and water. The oxygen molecule is made up of two atoms of oxygen; carbon dioxide molecule is made up of one atom of carbon and two atoms of oxygen; and water molecule is made up of two atoms of hydrogen and one atom of oxygen.

# CHARACTERISTICS OF MATTER

1. The particles of matter are in constant motion. In 1827, Robert Brown was the one who first gave the evidence for such movement of particles. The constant hitting of the pollen by particles of water produced a characteristic zigzag path which was named as **Brownian motion** after the name of the scientist.

Besides liquids, particles of gases also show Brownian motion. With the increase in temperature, the Brownian motion of particles increases in the liquids or gases.

2. The particles of matter are held together by a force called **intermolecular force of attraction**. This force is different for different types of matter.

3. The particles of matter have space in between them called **intermolecular space**.

**Fig. 6: Brownian motion**

Robert Brown suspended a pollen grain in the water and then observed the movement of pollen under the microscope. He observed that pollen grain rapidly moved to the surface of water. Then he explained that water is composed of tiny particles which are moving very fast. These fast moving particles continuously strike the pollen grain and cause it to move to the water surface.

# STATES OF MATTER

On the basis of physical properties, like temperature, matter is classified into solids, liquids, and gases. These are known as **states of matter**.

## Solid State

The molecules in solids are tightly packed. They have the least intermolecular space. The intermolecular force of attraction between solid molecules is the strongest. Thus molecules hardly move about, rather they vibrate at their positions. Also, solids have a fixed shape and a fixed volume. Ice, wood, sugar, coal, diamond, glass, etc. are some solids.

**Properties of solids:** Solids have the following properties.

- The molecules are tightly packed. Thus they have a fixed shape and a fixed volume.
- The intermolecular force of attraction in solid molecules is the strongest.
- Solids do not flow.
- Solids do not fill their container completely.
- Solids cannot be compressed much. Some soft solids like rubber and sponge can be compressed.
- Solids do not diffuse. The process of mixing two substances is called **diffusion**.

## Liquid State

Liquid is the state of matter that is characterised by the change in its shape but resistance to change in its volume. The intermolecular force of attraction is less compared to solids. So, the molecules in liquids are less tightly packed than solids. The molecules of liquids can move from one position to another within the liquid. That is why liquids can flow easily. The intermolecular space in liquids is more than in solids. Water, oil, milk, lemonade, alcohol, tea, benzene, etc. are liquids.

**Fig. 11: Water is liquid**

### Properties of liquids:

- Liquids have loosely packed particles.
- Liquids have a fixed volume but no fixed shape. They take the shape of the container in which they are placed.
- Liquids flow easily.
- Liquids can be compressed much more than solids.
- Liquids can diffuse with each other. Liquids like acetone-water or glycerine-water can mix with each other and are called **miscible liquids**. Some liquids like petrol and water or oil and water do not mix with each other and are called **immiscible liquids**.

## Gaseous State

Gas is the state of matter that is characterised by its capacity to change its shape and volume. The molecules in gases are very loosely packed compared to liquids and solids. The molecules are far apart as the intermolecular force of attraction between them is the weakest. They are free to move about in the space available to them. The intermolecular space between them is large. Carbon dioxide, oxygen, hydrogen, methane, nitrogen, helium, etc. are some gases.

### Properties of gases:

- Gases have very loosely packed particles.
- Gases have neither a fixed volume nor a fixed shape. The gas particles are free to move and have no fixed positions so they lack any definite shape. Also, as there are no fixed spaces between the particles, gases do not have fixed volume.
- Gases can easily flow.
- Gases fill the container completely in which they are placed.
- Gases can be compressed easily.
- Gases have the highest rate of diffusion.

# ARRANGEMENT OF MOLECULES IN SOLIDS, LIQUIDS AND GASES

You have now seen that matter exists in three states—solid, liquid and gas. For example, water exists as ice (solid), water (liquid) and water vapour (gas). The molecules in all the three states are the same. Why does this difference in the states occur? This is due to the difference in the arrangement of the molecules in solids, liquids and gases.

In solids, the molecules are closely packed. Their positions are almost fixed. That is why solids retain their shape and have a fixed volume. For example, a brick or a piece of chalk or a metallic pot does not change its shape or volume on its own.

In liquids, the molecules are not packed as closely as in solids. Liquids, therefore, change their shape easily but not their volume.

In gases, the molecules are far apart from one another as compared to solids and liquids (Fig. 15). They are free to move. Gases have, therefore, neither a fixed shape nor a fixed volume. They take the shape and the volume of the closed container they are kept in.

---

### Effect of Arrangement of Molecules on the Properties of Solids, Liquids and Gases

The properties of solids, liquids and gases can be explained on the basis of arrangement of particles or molecules from which they are formed.

**Solids:** In solids, the molecules are closely packed due to strong intermolecular force of attraction (or **cohesive force**, i.e. intermolecular force of attraction between the like particles of a substance) between them (Fig. 16). However, the space between molecules is very small. Thus, the positions of solid molecules are almost fixed. Therefore, solids have a definite shape and a fixed volume.

When a solid is heated, the molecules get extra energy, and they begin to vibrate at their positions and gradually move away from their positions. The distance between the molecules increases resulting in the conversion of the solid state into liquid state.

**Liquids:** In liquids, the intermolecular forces of attraction (cohesive forces) are less strong as compared to the solids. Also, the distance between the molecules, called intermolecular space, is more in liquids than in solids. Hence, liquids are free to move about but continue to interact with one another. Thus liquids do not possess a definite shape but a definite volume.

**Fig. 15: Arrangement of molecules in different states of matter** (Solid, Liquid, Gas)

**Fig. 16:** The arrangement of molecules in solids

On heating a liquid, the molecules acquire more energy, and start moving with higher speed. Those molecules which are near the surface after acquiring more energy escape from the liquid as vapour, a gaseous state of matter.

**Gases:** In a gas, the molecules move very fast. Also, the molecules are separated from one another by huge intermolecular spaces (Fig. 18). The interacting force between the molecules is, therefore, very weak, and molecules can move freely. They occupy the whole space available to them. Hence, a gas has neither a definite volume nor a definite shape.

When a gas is cooled, the speed of the molecules decreases. On cooling, the intermolecular space decreases. The molecules come closer to one another. The gas, then, changes into the liquid state.

|--------|--------|---------|-------|
| Intermolecular Attraction | Maximum | Less | Minimum |
| Intermolecular Space | Minimum | More | Maximum |
| Kinetic Energy (of Molecules) | Minimum | In between | Maximum |

### EFFECT OF HEAT ON MATTER

The effect of heat on matter can be explained in terms of – expansion in matter; change of state of matter and chemical change in matter.

#### Expansion in Matter

When a substance is heated, it increases in its size. The increase in size of a substance on heating is called **expansion** of that substance. During expansion, the particles of the substance remain the same but it is the intermolecular space between the particles that changes.

All the three states of matter show **expansion**. Solid particles vibrate more when it is heated and occupy more space; liquid particles move around each other more when heated and take up more space; and gas molecules move even more quickly in all directions when it is heated and occupy more space.

Different substances expand by different amounts depending on the given temperature change. Usually, gases expand the most when heated and solids expand the least.

# Interconversion of States of Matter

Now we know that matter exists in three physical states—solid, liquid, and gas. The physical state of matter can be changed by changing the temperature, i.e., by heating it or cooling it or by changing the pressure or both.

When a solid melts to form a liquid or a liquid evaporates to form a gas, we say that there is a change of state. Also, there is a change of state when a gas is cooled, and this change is from gas to a liquid. This is called **interconversion of states of matter**.

**Fig. 23: States of matter** (Solid → Liquid → Gas)

**Interconversion of states of matter by heating:** When a change takes place from one state of matter to another, some processes take place as mentioned below.

- **Melting (fusion) and melting point:** The process of changing a solid substance into liquid by heating is called **melting**. For example, when ice (solid) changes into water (liquid), it is called **melting of ice**. A solid substance can be melted only by applying heat. The melting of a solid substance takes place at a fixed temperature.

The temperature at which a solid substance melts and changes into a liquid is called **melting point of the substance**. The melting point of ice is 0°C.

- **Boiling (vaporisation) and boiling point:** When a liquid is heated continuously, then its temperature goes on rising and its evaporation (changing into vapour) takes place faster and faster. At a certain temperature, the liquid changes into vapour (a gas) very rapidly and we say that the liquid has started boiling. *The rapid changing of liquid into vapour (or gas) on heating is called boiling.*

The boiling of a liquid takes place at a fixed temperature. The temperature at which a liquid boils and changes into vapour (or gas) very rapidly is called **boiling point of the liquid**. For example, water boils at a temperature of 100°C to form a gas called steam, so the boiling point of water is 100°C.

- **Evaporation:** The gaseous state of a liquid is also called its **vapour state**. Most of the liquids keep on changing into vapours slowly even at room temperature. *The changing of a liquid into vapour (or gas) at any temperature below its boiling point is called evaporation*. The evaporation of a liquid can take place at all temperatures.

---

### FUN FACT

The gaseous state of water is called vapour. Is it then oxygen gas or oxygen vapour?

Water can be in solid or liquid state under normal condition but changes into the gaseous state on heating. So, we say water vapour and not water gas (it is a mixture of two gases which you will learn in higher classes).

Substances like oxygen, hydrogen, chlorine, nitrogen dioxide, carbon dioxide remain in this state under normal conditions. So, they are called gas and not vapour.

---

- **Condensation (liquefaction):** We have just seen that when water is heated, it changes into steam (or water vapour). The reverse of this is also true. That is, if we cool the steam, it changes back into water (liquid state). *The process of changing a gas (or vapour) into a liquid by cooling is called condensation.* Thus, when steam (or water vapour) is cooled at 100°C, it condenses to form liquid water.

- **Freezing (solidification) and freezing point:** We have seen that when ice is heated, it melts to form water. The reverse of this is also true. That is, when water is cooled, it changes into ice. *The process of changing a liquid into solid by cooling is called freezing.* For example, when water is cooled at 0°C, it freezes to form ice. The change of state from liquid to solid is also called **solidification**.

The freezing of a liquid takes place at a particular temperature. The temperature at which a liquid freezes and changes into solid is called **freezing point of the liquid**. The freezing point of water is 0°C.

- **Sublimation:** Some substances change directly from solid to gas on heating without passing through the liquid state. This process is called **sublimation** and such substances are called **sublimable substances.** For example, iodine, ammonium chloride, camphor, benzoic acid, solid carbon dioxide (dry ice) and naphthalene.

### By changing pressure

Change in the state of a substance can also be brought by changing the pressure. Cylinders used at home contain the gas in liquid form under high pressure. When the pressure is released it changes to gaseous form and the gas burns. Oxygen cylinders used in hospitals store the gas in liquid form under high pressure. On releasing the pressure it changes into gaseous form.

Thus we see that change in the state of matter can be brought by changing the temperature and pressure. You can also see that these changes are reversible. Please remember when matter changes its state by heating, the temperature will remain unchanged until the state has been changed completely.

### Chemical Change in Matter

Besides expansion of matter and interconversion of states of matter, another important effect of heat on matter is the **chemical change in matter**.

The burning of candle is one such example which involves the chemical change in matter. When a candle burns, it is not wax that burns but the wax vapours that burn. The liquid wax moves up the wick and the heat of the flame then changes the liquid wax into wax vapours. When these wax vapours burn, it gives off heat and light. Thus a chemical change takes place.

In addition to the chemical change, physical changes involving shortening of candle; solidification of melted, dripped wax, also takes place in this process.
