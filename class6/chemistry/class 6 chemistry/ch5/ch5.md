### COMPOSITION OF AIR

Air is a mixture of gases. Until the eighteenth century, people thought that air was just one substance. Experiments have proved that it is really not one substance but a mixture of several gases. Nearly four-fifths of the air is nitrogen. About one-fifth of the air is oxygen. The air also has small amount of carbon dioxide, argon, helium, water vapour and dust particles.

**Aim:** To demonstrate that air is a mixture of gases

**Materials Required:** A glass trough, water, caustic soda, ink, a metallic block, candle, and large gas jar

**Procedure:**

1. Take a glass trough and fill it about one-third with water.
2. Dissolve one teaspoonful of caustic soda in water.
3. Now, add a few drops of ink.
4. Now, place the metallic block in the water and fix a candle on it.
5. Light the candle.
6. Cover the lighted candle with the large gas jar.
7. Record your observations.

**Fig. 6:** Air is a mixture of gases (diagram: inverted gas jar over burning candle, water rises)

**Observation:** The candle continues burning as long as there was air in the gas jar. The coloured water rises up in the bottle when the candle goes off.

**Inference:** The candle continues to burn because the oxygen of air is continuously available to the candle for burning. The water rises up in the bottle because oxygen of air is used up in the burning process. The carbon dioxide formed as a result of burning is absorbed by the caustic soda solution. It creates a vacuum in the gas jar that causes water to rise up. Nitrogen is the major part of air which is not used up by a burning candle as it does not support burning. Therefore, it is still present in the bottle.

It is also used in cold drinks and soda water.

**Procedure:**

Calcium hydroxide + Carbon dioxide → Calcium carbonate

This is a confirmatory test for carbon dioxide gas.

### Nitrogen

It is the major part of air. It takes up a four-fifths (78.09 per cent) of the space that air fills. It does not support combustion but controls the rate of burning. It is important for living things. All living things need nitrogen compounds for their growth. It is an essential part of proteins and enzymes, which are necessary for the growth and development of plants and animals.

Most plants cannot use atmospheric nitrogen directly. The nitrogen combines with oxygen under special conditions to form oxides of nitrogen, i.e., the usable form of nitrogen for living organisms. The process of conversion of atmospheric nitrogen to nitrogen compounds is called **nitrogen fixation**. We will learn nitrogen fixation in detail in higher classes.

### Oxygen

Oxygen is another major constituent of air. It forms about 21 per cent of air. It supports combustion and, therefore, essential for the burning of fuel. It is also very important for living beings. They use oxygen for respiration. The respired oxygen burns food in the living body to release energy.

### Inert Gases

Inert gases such as helium, neon, argon, krypton and xenon are present in traces in the air. These gases have a number of applications in our daily life. Helium is used in air balloons and in obtaining very low temperature. Argon is used in electric bulbs. Xenon and Krypton are used in photography.

### Water Vapour

Variable amount of water vapour is present in air. The amount of water vapour varies at different periods of time. During the rainy season, the amount of water vapour is highest and it is lowest during the summer season. Have you ever thought where the water vapour comes from into the air? It has come from the water which has evaporated from the surface of the earth. The presence of water vapour in the atmosphere is essential for the rains and rains are a natural source of water for plants and animals.

## AIR IS A MIXTURE

Several evidences prove air to be a mixture of various gases and not just a single substance.

1. Air cannot be represented by a definite formula as the constituents of air are not present in a fixed proportion. A compound is always represented by a definite formula.
2. The constituents of air retain their individual properties. In a compound, the elements do not retain their own properties.
3. The composition of air is not fixed. It varies with place and time. For example, in the coastal areas the amount of water vapour in the air is always higher than the interiors. The composition of a compound does not vary with time and place.
4. There is no change in energy when the constituents of air are mixed together. During the formation of a compound there is change in energy.
5. The constituents of air can be separated by physical methods. For example, liquid nitrogen and liquid oxygen are obtained on fractional distillation of liquid air. The constituents in a compound can only be separated by chemical methods.
6. Liquid air does not have a fixed boiling point. The boiling point of a compound is fixed.

# IMPORTANCE OF AIR FOR LIVING BEINGS

All the constituents of air are important for living beings in one way or the other. Some of the important uses of air are given below.

## Importance of Air in Respiration

Respiration is an essential process both in plants and animals. Living beings take oxygen and use it to produce energy from food during this process. Energy so produced is used by the organisms to carry out their physical activities.

Plants use the oxygen they produce during the process of photosynthesis. Animals take oxygen required for respiration by breathing in air. Thus we breathe in oxygen-rich air. It is called inhaled air. We breathe out carbon dioxide-rich air. It is called exhaled air. At higher altitudes, air is thin and breathing becomes difficult.

Mountaineers and miners carry with them cylinders containing oxygen. Divers also carry oxygen cylinders when they go deep into the sea as they cannot breathe there. Oxygen gas is also supplied to patients who have difficulty in breathing. You have already seen how much the oxygen cylinders were in demand during the Covid-19 pandemic.

# RUSTING

Iron reacts with oxygen and moisture present in the atmosphere to form a brown, flaky substance called rust. Rusting of iron is an undesirable change because the layer of rust formed falls off, exposing the metal to further rusting. As a result, iron objects become weak with the passage of time. Rusting is defined as the process of slow eating away of iron due to the attack of oxygen and moisture on its surface.

## Conditions Necessary for Rusting

The conditions necessary for rusting are air and moisture.

When rusting occurs, iron reacts with water and oxygen in its environment. This reaction forms an oxide, also called **rust**. Thus rusting is a chemical reaction in which iron is changed into a new substance, i.e., rust. The process of rusting may be represented by the following word equation:

\[ \text{Iron} + \text{Oxygen} + \text{Water} \rightarrow \text{Iron oxide (hydrated), i.e., rust} \]

**Aim:** To demonstrate that air and moisture are necessary for rusting

**Materials Required:** Three test-tubes, three iron nails, calcium chloride, water, test-tube stoppers

## Procedure:

1. Take three test-tubes with one iron nail in each and mark them as A, B, and C.
2. Place a small amount of calcium chloride in the test-tube A to dry the air.
3. Fill the test-tube B with boiled water. Boiling of water drives out all the dissolved oxygen from water.
4. Pour some tap water in the test-tube C.
5. Close the mouth of all the three test-tubes with stoppers.
6. Leave the set-up undisturbed for 3 to 4 days.
7. Record your observations.

**Diagram:** Test-tube A (Nail free from rust, Calcium chloride), Test-tube B (Boiled water), Test-tube C (Rusted iron nail, Water)

**Observation:** The nails in A and B test-tubes are free from rust while that in the C test-tube has rusted.

**Inference:** Oxygen and moisture are two necessary requirements for rusting. Test-tube A lacks moisture while test-tube B lacks air, thus nails in these test-tubes are not rusted. In the test-tube C, both oxygen and moisture are present that make the iron nail rust.

### Methods of Preventing Rusting

Rusting can be prevented by not allowing the iron to come in contact with air and water. The most important methods of rust prevention are as follows:

**Oiling or greasing:** The oil or grease forms a layer over the surface of iron. This prevents oxygen and water coming in contact with iron. This method of rust prevention is usually used for moving parts of machines such as in bicycle chains and car engines.

**Painting:** This prevents oxygen and water coming in contact with iron. The paint forms a layer over the iron object. This method is used to prevent rusting of bridges, ships, gates, cars and bicycles.

**Electroplating:** Coating iron with a thin layer of zinc is called galvanising. Coating the surface of iron with metals like tin or chromium is called electroplating. Buckets, dustbins, sheets of iron and wire for fencing are often covered with zinc. Iron coated with zinc is called galvanised iron.

### AIR POLLUTION

Air is an important natural resource required for survival. Various human activities and even natural disasters like volcanic eruption release harmful gases into the air. *Releasing harmful and undesirable substances into the air is known as air pollution*. These harmful substances make the air unfit for breathing. These substances are called air pollutants. Pollutants can be gases or solid particles such as ash and dust. In the modern world pollution is increasing day by day.

---

### Causes of Air Pollution

How does air get polluted? Sometimes, air pollutants come from natural sources like dust and smoke from forest fires or volcanic eruptions. Besides natural causes of pollution, some human activities also add pollutants to the atmosphere. Such man-made sources of air pollution are factories; power plants; burning of firewood and dung cakes, and emissions from automobile exhausts, etc. Petroleum refineries are a major source of gaseous pollutants like oxides of sulphur and nitrogen.

Burning diesel and petrol also produce micro particles which are suspended in air. They reduce visibility and when inhaled cause dangerous respiratory diseases. Among industries, steel-making and mining industries also give out fine ash particles which also pollute the atmosphere.

## Carbon Dioxide and Greenhouse Effect

You know that the rays coming from the sun warm the earth’s surface. But only a part of radiation that falls on the earth is absorbed by it and the remaining part of radiation is reflected into the space. This reflected part of radiation gets trapped by the atmosphere. This trapped radiation further warms the earth. This is called the **greenhouse effect**.

In fact, life would not have been possible on the earth without the greenhouse effect. But excess of carbon dioxide (CO₂) in the air acts as a pollutant. It increases the average temperature of the earth. This is called **global warming**.

Due to global warming sea levels will rise dramatically and many coastal areas will be flooded. Global warming could affect weather and temperature patterns, plants, forests, agriculture and animals. It is estimated that the temperature may rise by more than 2 degrees Celsius by the end of the century. This level is considered to be dangerous for animal life.
