## PURE SUBSTANCE
You have often come across the term pure water, pure milk or pure air. In general by pure we mean a substance free of impurities. In chemistry, by pure substance we mean a substance having same chemical composition throughout even if the substance is sourced from different places. They are made of one type of particle. Elements and compounds are pure substances for example, salt, gold, silver, diamond, sugar, ethanol, etc.

# Characteristics of a Pure Substance

1. A pure substance is made up of one type of atom or molecule.
2. A pure substance always has the same composition (homogeneous) throughout.
3. A pure substance has a fixed melting and boiling point. For example, pure water freezes at 0°C and boils at 100°C. If we add salt to it, its freezing point decreases and boiling point increases.
4. A pure substance has a uniform structure.

Let us study in details about these pure substances.

### Properties of Compounds

- The compounds consist of two or more elements.
- The compound is made up of the same constituent elements which are combined together in a definite proportion or fixed ratio. For example, carbon dioxide which the plants use to prepare their food is made of 1 atom of carbon and 2 atoms of oxygen.
- Compounds are always pure and homogeneous. It has the same property throughout.
- The properties of compounds are different from those of their constituent elements. For example, oxygen is a gas and helps in burning. The element carbon exists in solid form and can burn in oxygen. Carbon dioxide does not support burning and is used in fire extinguishers to extinguish fires.
- The constituent elements of the compounds cannot be separated by physical means. The compound water cannot be separated into hydrogen and oxygen by boiling. It can be broken down into its constituent elements by passing electricity through acidified water. This is called electrical decomposition of water or electrolysis.

### ATOMS AND MOLECULES

An **atom** is the smallest unit of an element which possesses all the properties of that element. It may or may not have independent existence. The atoms are characterised by the following properties:

- The atoms of most elements are reactive (except noble gases) and combine with the atoms of the same element or the other elements. Thus they do not exist freely.
- All the atoms of an element are similar.
- The atoms of an element are different from the atoms of other elements.
- Atom does not change its properties irrespective of the physical or chemical changes.

## FUN FACT
The word atom is derived from an ancient Greek word *atomos* which means indivisible.

*A molecule is the smallest unit of an element or a compound that has independent existence and exhibits all the properties of the element or compound.* The molecule is characterised by the following properties:

- They are formed by the combination of the atoms of the same element or different elements.
- Molecule is the smallest particle of a compound that has independence existence.
- It has atoms of different elements combined in a fixed ratio.
- The combining force of atoms in molecules is **chemical bond**.
- The molecules of compounds can be broken down into their constituent elements.
- Like elements, the molecule is denoted by a molecular formula.

---

### Molecules of elements:
The molecules of elements are formed when two or more atoms of the same element combine together. For example, two atoms of hydrogen combine to form one molecule of hydrogen. Similarly, 8 atoms of sulphur combine to form one molecule of sulphur.

---

### Molecules of compounds:
Molecule of a compound is formed when atoms of two or more elements combine in a fixed proportion. For example, the water molecule is formed by two atoms of hydrogen and one atom of oxygen. Similarly, the molecule of carbon dioxide is formed by the combination of one atom of carbon and two atoms of oxygen.

# MOLECULAR FORMULA
A **formula** represents one molecule of an element or a compound. It tells us the number of atoms of each element present in one molecule of a substance. Example, molecular formula of water is H₂O.

# Formulae of Elements
The molecular formula of an element represents the number of atoms of that element present in its one molecule. *The number of atoms in a molecule of an element is called its **atomicity**.* The molecule may be monoatomic, diatomic, triatomic or polyatomic depending upon the number of atoms present in one molecule of the element. The molecular formula of the element can be represented by writing the symbol of the element, followed by the number of atoms as subscript. For example, the molecular formula of oxygen is O₂.

# Formulae of Compounds

The compounds are formed by the combination of the atoms of two or more than two elements. Thus, the formula of a compound shows the number of atoms of different elements present in one molecule of that compound. For example, the molecular formula of water is H₂O. It means water is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen. Two atoms of hydrogen combine with one atom of oxygen to form water.

To write the chemical formula of a compound, two things are important. One, the symbols of the elements taking part in the formation of the compound. The second is the combining capacity of the elements. Atoms of different elements combine in whole numbers to form compounds. For example, in the compound ammonia, nitrogen and hydrogen combine in the ratio 1:3. While writing the chemical formula of a compound, we apply the following steps:

1. To begin with, write the symbols of all the elements that are present in a molecule of the compound side by side.
2. Indicate the number of atoms of each element present in the molecule by a subscript to that element.
3. If only one atom of an element is present in a molecule, no number is to be shown as subscript. In such molecules, the symbol itself represents one atom of the element.
4. To represent more than one molecule of a compound or atoms of an element, the number is put as a prefix to the formula or the symbol. For example, 2NaCl represents two molecules of sodium chloride. Similarly, 2H₂ means two molecules of hydrogen, 2Ca means two atoms of calcium and 3C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ means three molecules of sugar.

# MIXTURES

Most substances around us are not pure. They are mixtures of two or more pure substances—elements and compounds. The pure substances can be mixed in different ways and in any proportion. The air we breathe in is not a pure substance rather it is a mixture of different gases. It is a mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide and water vapour along with dust particles. River water consists of solid particles. Similarly, the tea we prepare at home contains milk, water, sugar, tea extract and other things.
A **mixture** is a substance which consists of two or more elements or compounds not chemically combined together. All the mixtures are impure substances because they contain more than one kind of particles.

---

[Diagram] SUGAR + TEA → WATER + MILK → TEA

# Characteristics of Mixtures

1. The constituents of a mixture may be present in any proportion. For example, in a mixture of sugar and water, the quantities of sugar and water are not fixed. A glass of sugar solution (sharbat) may contain 1, 2 or 3 teaspoons of sugar, but it is called mixture of sugar and water.
2. The constituents of a mixture can be separated by simple physical methods. For example, a mixture of iron filings and sulphur powder can be separated by using a magnet. A magnet attracts the iron filings and thus iron filings are separated by magnet from the mixture.
3. A mixture does not have any specific property. In a mixture, each constituent exhibits its original properties. For example, the taste of sugar is sweet and when it is mixed with water, it makes the water sweet.
4. A mixture does not have a fixed melting or boiling point. For example, the sugar solutions prepared with different quantities of water and same amount of sugar will have different boiling points.
5. The constituents in a mixture are not chemically bonded to each other and thus retain their individual property.
6. A mixture does not have a formula.

---

# Types of Mixtures

We have studied above that mixtures are composed of different substances. These substances are called **components** or **constituents** of the mixture. These constituents may be completely mixed together, or may remain separate, or may diffuse through another substance. In some cases, the constituents of the mixture are completely mixed in a way that they are not distinguishable from one another. In other cases, the constituents are not completely mixed and are distinguishable from each other. Based on this criterion, mixtures are of two types—homogeneous mixtures and heterogeneous mixtures.

## Homogeneous mixtures:

The mixtures containing its constituents completely mixed together in a way not distinguishable from one another are called **homogeneous mixtures**. They are also called **solutions**. The constituents of such mixtures are uniformly mixed and have the same properties throughout. For example, sugar solution in water is a homogeneous mixture. The molecules of water and sugar are uniformly mixed and cannot be seen separately. Air, blood, brass, steel, fruit juice, soda water, etc. are homogeneous mixtures.

## Heterogeneous mixtures:

The mixtures whose constituents are not completely mixed and distinguishable from each other are known as **heterogeneous mixtures**. The constituents of such mixtures are not uniformly mixed and do not have the same properties throughout. For example, the mixture of sugar and sand is a heterogeneous mixture. The particles of sugar and sand can be seen separately. Soup, oil in water, mist, smoke, chalk in water, muddy water are some examples of heterogeneous mixtures.

# COMPOUNDS AND MIXTURES
Compounds are the substances made of chemical combinations of their constituents. The properties of the compounds are entirely different from the properties of their constituents. Mixtures are the substances made up of physical mixing of their constituents. The properties of the mixtures are same as those of their constituents. Thus the properties of compounds are different from those of the mixtures.

# NEED FOR SEPARATING THE COMPONENTS OF A MIXTURE

We know that a mixture is made up of two or more components. The components of a mixture retain their properties. The eatables and other materials, which we get from the nature, are all mixtures. Some mixtures, such as, vegetables and fruits can be used as such because they are useful for us. But materials, such as, rice, wheat, pulses, gram and black pepper, obtained from the nature cannot be used as such. They may contain impurities, such as, dust particles, husk, insects, small pieces of stones and other unwanted or harmful substances. Before using them in our homes, we must separate the unwanted and harmful components present in them.

We need air for breathing that is a mixture of several substances, such as, gases, water vapours and dust particles. Our body does not require all of these components of air. Hence, our body separates the useful components, such as, oxygen from air and the other constituents are breathed out. Similarly, suspended impurities present in water are usually removed before we drink it because they may be harmful to us. Thus, for obtaining useful components, we need to separate unwanted and harmful components present in a mixture. Therefore, the separation of the components of a mixture is essential for the following reasons:

- To remove unwanted components
- To remove harmful components
- To obtain pure and useful components

# Separation of Insoluble Solid-Liquid Mixture

**Sedimentation and decantation:** Solids, which are insoluble in liquids, can be separated by sedimentation-decantation method. For example, sand and water can be separated from their mixture by this method. To do this, allow the mixture to stand in a container for some time. You will see that the sand will settle at the bottom of the container. This is called **sedimentation**. Pour the top layer of water gently into another container. Be careful so that the sand may not be disturbed. This process is called **decantation**. This method is suitable where the solid is insoluble and heavier than the liquid.

**Evaporation:** It is the simplest way of separating a soluble solid from a liquid. It involves changing a liquid into a gas. This method is used on large scale to obtain salt from seawater. ### Separating Mixtures by Using Separating Funnel

A separating funnel is used to separate two or more **immiscible** liquids having different densities. The mixture is taken in a separating funnel and allowed to stand for a while. This separates the liquids into layers. The liquid with the lower density floats on the top and the one with the higher density settles below it. After this, the funnel tap is opened and the liquid at the bottom of the funnel is transferred into a container. For example, oil and water are immiscible liquids and can be separated by using a separating funnel.

# BUZZWORDS

1. **Compound:** a substance composed of two or more elements, chemically combined in a definite proportion
2. **Element:** a substance made up of identical atoms
3. **Homogeneous mixture:** a mixture with uniform composition
4. **Heterogeneous mixture:** a mixture with non-uniform composition
5. **Molecules of compounds:** contain atoms of different elements
6. **Molecules of elements:** contain atoms of same elements
7. **Sieving:** method used to separate the components of a mixture which are of different sizes
8. **Symbol:** shorthand notations of writing the names of elements
9. **Crystallisation:** method of separating a pure solid in the form of its crystals from its solution
10. **Sublimation:** a change of a solid state directly to the vapour state
11. **Winnowing:** a method used to separate undesirable constituents from a mixture by using wind
12. **Decantation:** method of separating a liquid without disturbing the sediment
13. **Distillation:** method used to separate a soluble solid in a liquid or two miscible liquids
14. **Sedimentation:** the settling of insoluble heavy solid particles in a mixture of solid and liquid
15. **Filtration:** the process used to separate an insoluble solid from a liquid

# TO SUM-UP

- Matter is classified into elements, compounds and mixtures.
- Elements and compounds are pure substances.
- A pure substance is made up of only one kind of molecules.
- The smallest unit of an element is atom and the smallest unit of a compound is a molecule.
- Elements and compounds are represented in shorthand using a formula.
- Many of the substances found in nature, that we use, are mixtures of one or more substances.
- The differences in the properties of substances are used to separate them from a mixture.
- Selection of a separation method depends upon the nature of the components to be separated.
- Separation of the components of a mixture is done to remove an undesirable component or to get a desirable component.
- The components of a mixture are separated by one method or a combination of various methods.
- Various methods such as decantation, sedimentation, sieving, handpicking, filtration, magnetic separation, loading, winnowing, evaporation, crystallisation, sublimation and distillation are used for separation of various components present in a mixture.
- Some substance mix with each and are called miscible substances while some do not mix with each other and is said to be immiscible.
