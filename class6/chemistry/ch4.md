## SOURCES OF WATER

About three-fourths of the earth’s surface is covered with water. That is why, the earth is also called the **blue planet** or the **watery planet**. About 97 per cent of water is found in the seas and oceans. However, it is not suitable for drinking and irrigation. The remaining, about 3 per cent of the earth’s water, is available to us as freshwater in glaciers, rivers, lakes, ponds, springs, wells, etc. Less than 1 per cent of water is suitable for drinking purpose. Some of the water sources are on the surface of the earth while others are underground. Thus, the sources of natural water can be divided into two groups, i.e., surface water and underground water.

## Surface Water

Surface water is present on the surface of earth. There are five main sources of surface water.

- **Rainwater**: It is the purest form of natural water because the rain is produced by the condensation of water vapour. However, as the rain falls, the water sticks to dust particles and dissolves a number of gases present in the atmosphere. Usually, it contains 0.005 per cent solid impurities besides the dissolved atmospheric gases like sulphur dioxide (SO₂), carbon dioxide (CO₂), nitrogen dioxide (NO₂), ammonia (NH₃), etc.

- **Ocean and seawater**: The largest amount of water on the earth is found in the ocean. The oceans cover more than two-thirds surface of the earth. It is the most impure water. Rivers flow into the seas where due to continuous evaporation of water, the dissolved impurities begin to accumulate. Seawater contains nearly 3.5 per cent of dissolved matter, out of which about 2.7 per cent is common salt (sodium chloride). The seawater is salty and cannot be used at home and in agriculture.

- **River water**: River from the rains and from the glaciers that melts flow along the surface of the earth forming river. Besides this, spring water also flows into the river. River water, therefore, contains both soluble and suspended impurities. Some of the minerals and organic matters like clay, sand, straw, etc., remain suspended in it and make it muddy. Besides gases, the nature of the dissolved matter depends upon the type of the beds over which the river water passes.

- **Lake**: There are many deep and large hollows of water on the earth which are called lakes. The lakes get water from rains, rivers and springs.

- **Pond water**: Sometimes, rainwater is collected in small and shallow hollows on the surface of the earth. These are called ponds. This water also contains both dissolved and suspended impurities.

---

## Underground Water

The rainwater or water from different waterbodies which percolate through the upper layer of the earth’s surface and is collected on the solid rocks underground is called **underground water**. Wells, tube wells, boreholes and hand pumps are used to draw underground water. In some places the underground water often comes up to the earth’s surface in the form of a spring. Water obtained from these sources is not always fit for drinking and cooking purposes. Many impurities and germs may be present in it. Various methods are used to make the water fit for drinking.

- **Spring water**: This water is free from suspended and organic impurities but contains lot of minerals such as soluble salts of Na, K, Fe, Ca, etc. dissolved in it. It also contains soluble gases. It, thus, develops a good taste and is used for drinking purposes. Spring water also has medicinal properties.

- **Well water**: Well water also contains CO₂ and mineral salts dissolved in it. This water has been used for drinking and irrigation purposes since a long time. Today, tube wells are very common and are made by boring pipes in the ground.

## STATES OF WATER

Water exists in all the three states—solid (ice), liquid (water) and gas (vapour). The Arctic and Antarctic oceans and the top of high mountains are full of snow (ice). Oceans, seas, lakes and rivers have water in liquid form. Water in the vapour form is found in the atmosphere. In our houses too, we see all the three states of water. Refrigerator has a special cabin for making ice, liquid water is used by all of us every day. We observe steam (gas) when water is boiled.

### Interchangeability of Three States of Water

The three states of water are interchangeable. It can be changed from one state to another by changing the temperature. When temperature is raised, solid state of water changes to liquid state and liquid state changes to gaseous state. For example, ice melts on heating and changes to water. Similarly, when water is boiled, it changes to vapour. On the other hand, when temperature is reduced, the gaseous state of water changes into liquid state and liquid state changes into solid state. For example, when water vapours are cooled, they condense and change back to water. Water on further cooling changes back to ice.

## THE WATER CYCLE

You have already learnt that on heating, water turns into water vapour. Water vapour on cooling, forms water again. *The movement of water from the earth to the atmosphere and back to the earth is called the water cycle.*

Let’s take a look at water cycle in detail.

The sun warms the water in oceans, ponds, lakes and rivers. The water from these sources continuously changes into water vapour which escape into the air. *The process of changing water into water vapour is known as evaporation*. Plants also convert water into water vapour through the process of transpiration.

The heat of the sun warms up the air near the surface of the earth. This warm air, containing water vapour rises up. In the atmosphere, temperature goes down with altitude. When water vapour cools at higher altitudes, minute water droplets are formed. *The process of cooling of water vapour into minute water droplets is known as condensation*.

The tiny water droplets merge to form clouds. When the water droplets in the clouds come together, they form larger droplets of water. The larger droplets become heavy and fall as rain back to the earth. This process of forming rain from small droplets is known as **precipitation**.

A part of the rainwater evaporates while the remaining rainwater is absorbed by the soil and goes underground. The water on land is utilised by living objects including human beings, which also comes back to nature through various life processes.

When air cools to a greater extent, the water droplets freeze into snow particles. These particles combine to form snow flakes which fall as snow in the colder regions. When the snow melts, water flows into streams and rivers. Many of these rivers fall into the ocean. Most of the water falling in the form of rain also reaches the sea through streams and rivers.

Thus, the water cycle occurs in nature all the time. It makes water available in the form of rain. It plays an important role in regulating weather and global climate. By absorbing large quantities of heat, the oceans help in controlling global warming. We know that heat is absorbed when water evaporates and is released when it condenses. This absorption and release of heat, which is a form of energy, helps drive weather patterns in the short term and regulate the climate over longer periods of time.

## PHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF WATER

Water has several important physical properties.

1. Water is a tasteless, colourless transparent liquid. The presence of dissolved minerals and salts, and dissolved gases like carbon dioxide give water its natural taste.
2. Pure water freezes at 0°C and boils at 100°C under normal atmospheric pressure.
3. Water has a high specific heat capacity of 1 cal/gm. **Specific heat capacity** is defined as the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of 1 gm of the substance by 1°C. Due to the high specific heat capacity of water, it is used as a coolant.
4. The maximum density of water is 1 gm/cc at 4°C. On reducing the temperature, the volume of water decreases till the temperature reaches 4°C. On further reducing the temperature to 0°C till it becomes ice, water starts to expand and its volume increases. This is called **anomalous expansion** of water. Due to this property, aquatic life can survive in the cold regions where water freezes on the surface but remains liquid below the icy layer.
5. Water has the ability to dissolve many substances and hence is called a universal solvent.

Water is the habitat for a large number of marine or aquatic animals and plants living in oceans, lakes, rivers and ponds. They take dissolved nutrients and oxygen from water for their survival. Even the animals and plants on land need water to sustain their life.

It plays a variety of roles in our body. Firstly, it helps the digestion of food in our stomach. Secondly, it facilitates the absorption of nutrients in the blood and their distribution to various parts of our body. Thirdly, it helps in maintaining body temperature. We sweat a lot when the weather is warm. We drink water to ensure that body temperature is maintained within the reasonable limits. - Water acts as a coolant to keep the engines cool.

## THE NEED FOR POTABLE WATER

Although the earth has a huge stock of water, the amount of water that can be used by human beings and other animals is very small. Of the total water on earth, 97.4 per cent is in the oceans. It is salty and hence cannot be used by human beings. Most of the freshwater is frozen in glaciers and in the polar areas of the earth. Only a small fraction (0.01 per cent) is available for human use. This small amount of accessible water is repeatedly recycled for use by animals and human beings.

The water suitable for drinking by human beings is called **potable water**. Water that we get from various sources may not be fit for drinking. It may have dissolved salts, along with suspended particles and microorganisms. You know that some of the dissolved salts are essential for our body. However, if some salts are present in excess, they need to be removed. Suspended particles are also to be filtered out and microorganisms killed before the water reaches the municipal water supply, otherwise it may cause several diseases, especially **gastroenteritis** and **diarrhoea**.

## WATERBORNE DISEASES

The water obtained from different sources is not always potable, i.e., not fit for drinking. It may contain harmful chemicals and microorganisms. The chemicals and microorganisms may cause diseases. Such diseases are known as **waterborne diseases**. Some waterborne diseases are as follows:

- **Diarrhoea**: It is caused by an infection in the intestine and is characterised by frequent loose motions. It results in dehydration, i.e., loss of water in the body.
- **Typhoid**: It is caused by a bacterium present in the drinking water. The bacterium affects the intestine leading to high fever for a number of days.
- **Jaundice (hepatitis)**: It affects the liver of a person and is caused by the Hepatitis A virus. In this, the person suffers from disorder of the liver, nausea, weight loss and stomach pain.
- **Cholera**: It causes frequent vomiting. It may even cause death if medical treatment is delayed.
- **Gastroenteritis**: It is caused by an infection in the intestine. It is characterised by pain in the stomach with loose motions.
- **Dysentery**: It is caused by bacteria or amoebae. This infects the walls of intestine resulting in secretion of mucus during motion. In severe cases, acute stomach pain and bleeding also occur.

## PURIFICATION OF NATURAL WATER

Pure water is required for drinking, industrial and scientific purposes. The water obtained from natural sources is not always potable. It may contain suspended and dissolved impurities and microorganisms. Therefore, water needs to be purified before its use. This can be done by killing the germs and removing impurities from it. The purification of water involves sedimentation and decantation, loading, filtration, and destroying germs.

**Sedimentation and decantation:** Impure natural water obtained from rivers and lakes is first passed through sedimentation tank. Here the water is allowed to stand for some time. Most of the suspended impurities called sediments settle and water becomes clear. This water is then sent into the loading tank.

**Loading:** The fine suspended particles in water may not settle in the sedimentation and decantation tank. To increase the rate of their sedimentation, certain chemical known as alum is added. Alum makes the suspended particles heavier so that they settle. Thus, the water is treated with potash alum in the loading tank and allowed to stand still for some time.

**Filtration:** Thereafter, the water is passed through a filtration tank which contains layers of fine sand, gravel and charcoal. These layers act as filters. Water that passes through these filter layers is totally free from suspended impurities.

**Destroying germs (boiling or chlorination):** The addition of chlorine to the water is called chlorination. The filtered water is sterilised with bleaching powder. The bleaching powder releases chlorine which dissolves in water and kills germs and other microorganisms. The boiling of water also kills germs present in it.

**Storage:** The water purified through the above processes is then stored in a storage tank from where it is supplied to the homes through pipes.

## WATER—THE UNIVERSAL SOLVENT

Water has the capacity to dissolve a large number of substances, thus it is known as the **universal solvent**. A solvent is a substance in which other substance gets dissolved completely and evenly. Rainwater is pure water, but, after falling on the ground, it comes in contact with several minerals and dissolves some of them. It also dissolves certain substances from the atmosphere as it comes down. The substances that dissolve in a solvent are called **solutes**. When solute dissolves in a solvent, we get a **solution**. In a solution, a solvent is present in larger quantity than the solute. The formation of a solution depends on the nature of the solute and solvent, size of the solute particles and temperature of the solution and increases on stirring. *Solution of any substance in water is known as its aqueous solution*. Let us try to understand how the process of dissolution takes place.

The quantity of a substance, which dissolves in water, increases with temperature. Similarly, water dissolves liquids like alcohol and gases like oxygen, which is essential for animals and plants living in water.

In our body, water dissolves minerals, gases and chemicals, and carries them around wherever needed. It also carries the wastes and removes them from the body.

Thus, it can be said that dissolution is a process of mixing. No chemical reaction takes place when a solute dissolves in a solvent.

### Saturated and Unsaturated Solutions

A solution which cannot dissolve any more solute into it at a particular temperature is called a **saturated solution** at that temperature. A solution which can take up more of the solute at a given temperature is called an **unsaturated solution**. The quantity of solute that dissolves in a given quantity of a solvent at a particular temperature is called the **solubility** of the solute. It is expressed in grams of solute per 100 gram of solvent. It has been experimentally observed that solubility of salts like potassium nitrate, potassium bromide, sodium chloride, sodium nitrate, etc. increases with temperature. Solubility of salts like calcium sulphate, sodium carbonate decreases with increase in temperature.

## WATER POLLUTION

The presence of unwanted material makes water polluted. Inland water is often polluted by several chemical substances and tiny living organisms. Such substances and living organisms are known as water pollutants and this condition of water is known as water pollution. Thus water is called polluted when it is unfit for drinking or bathing. Polluted water is also harmful to plants and animals.

## Causes of Water Pollution

The main causes of water pollution are as follows:
- **Industrial waste:** The waste chemicals from industries are called **effluents** which dissolve in water causing toxicity to aquatic plants and animal life. This water is unfit for drinking.
- **Domestic sewage:** The water coming from our homes contains various organic and inorganic wastes like waste from toilet, waste from washing clothes, food and vegetable waste. It is called **sewage**. This sewage is drained into the lakes, rivers and seas. It contains germs and microorganisms which cause various waterborne diseases in human beings.

---

### FUN FACT

You must have noticed a green coloured growth on some lakes or ponds. This is due to the overgrowth of algae in the waterbody. This is called **eutrophication**. This happens due to excessive nutrients being added to the waterbody which results in the depletion of dissolved oxygen in water and results in the loss of aquatic life.

---

### Methods of Preventing/Reducing Pollution

The following are the methods of preventing/reducing pollution.

1. The sewage should be treated in sewage treatment plant rather than directly released into rivers.
2. The polluted water from industries should be passed through effluent treatment plants so that all the harmful chemicals present may be removed.
3. Organic farming should be encouraged so that excessive use of pesticides and insecticides may be reduced.
4. Trees and plants may be planted along the banks of the rivers. Trees act as natural filters and help in removing the pollutants before water flows into the rivers.
5. Bathing and cleaning of animals near the source of water like lakes, rivers and ponds must be avoided.
6. The waste products from factories should be recycled and used before being released.
7. In many areas, people defecate in open fields. The rain washes away human excreta and animal dung into the nearby water sources. The dung contains several harmful microorganisms that can cause diseases such as diarrhoea, dysentery and jaundice. Human and animal excreta should be prevented from mixing with water sources. This can be achieved by building pit latrines and treating the sewage properly before releasing in rivers.

## FLOODS AND DROUGHTS

There is uneven distribution of rainfall in our country. Some places in our country receive very heavy rainfall whereas, some places are extremely dry. Excessive rainfall in certain places may lead to a condition called **flood**. Due to flood the water level in rivers, lakes and ponds may rise which may submerge the adjoining areas.

Flood also leads to waterlogging in several places which disrupts the flow of traffic. Fields get submerged under water which lead to damaged crops. Flood also leads to loss of animal life. Organisms living in soil do not get air to breathe and die.

When rainfall does not happen at a place for a prolonged time it results in **drought**. The groundwater level falls and there is scarcity of water everywhere. Ponds and lakes dry up. Animals living in water may die. The plants do not get adequate water and do not grow well. This leads to severe food shortage.

## CONSERVING WATER

The total amount of potable water for living organisms is not sufficient. Therefore, it is very important and essential to conserve water. Some of the reasons to conserve water are as follows:

- The overuse of ground water has led to scarcity of water.
- The output of crops is severely affected by the scarcity of water.
- Agriculture and industrialisation demand a large quantity of water.

---

## How can Water be Conserved?

Following are the methods of conserving water:

- Rainwater harvesting must be done both at domestic and community levels. In this process collection, storage and use of rainwater is done.
- More and more plantation is to be done as plants help in bringing rain.
- Use of drip irrigation or sprinkler irrigation system for agricultural crops must be encouraged.
- Maintaining the water cycle.
- Methods of water pollution prevention must be adopted.

At the individual level, avoid wasting of water. Do not leave the tap open while brushing your teeth or washing hands and repair leakage taps.
Organising mass awareness programmes through private and public sectors to conserve water.
