### WHAT IS MATTER?

Physics is the subject that studies laws of Nature. Before we understand the laws by which Nature works, it is better to classify the things we see before ourselves. There are various objects around us such as tables, chairs, doors, houses, shops, bread, butter, water, bottles, friends, parents and relatives. Although these objects are almost different from each other, there is one commonality in these objects. All these substances are made of matter.

Now that we know a few examples of matter, we should straight away define what matter is?

### A thing that occupies space and has mass is known as matter.

Vacuum is not matter. Love, hatred and suspicion are also not matter, these are emotions. Victory and defeat are not matter. Gold, silver and bronze are matter. So, now you know why certain things are considered matter and why others are not.

# INVESTIGATION 1.1

Take an old plastic bottle. Cut it into two parts. Make a beaker shaped pot from the lower part. Tie it to a strong thread in such a way that it makes a suspension loop. Suspend this beaker from the hook of a spring balance and note the weight of the beaker. Now fill the beaker with sugar and note the weight. You will notice that the spring balance shows more weight.

Thus, we conclude that sugar (a solid) has weight and hence, has a mass.

Repeat the activity by using milk or water. You notice that the spring balance shows less weight as compared to the previous instance when you used sugar.

Thus, from this investigation, we conclude that solids and liquids have weight and hence have a mass.

# INVESTIGATION 1.2

Make a simple balance with a ruler and string. Tie one end of the piece of string to the centre of the ruler at the 6 cm mark. Tie the other end to a hanger so that ruler hangs horizontally below it.

Blow up two large and similar balloons to the same size. Tie one balloon to the ruler at the 1 cm mark and other at the 11 cm. Adjust the string so that it makes the ruler level and the balloons balanced.

The filled air causes the balloons to stretch and grow bigger. The air takes up space and exerts pressure on the surface of the balloon.

Prick a pin or needle on the balloon to allow the air to escape slowly and observe what happens. The ruler will tilt down towards the balloon that still contains air. This is because the balloon containing the air has more mass than the empty balloon. Thus, the difference in mass of the balloons is equal to the mass of air contained in the balloon.

Thus, we conclude that air has weight and hence has mass.

---

Similarly, we can prove the above investigation by taking some dry ice (solid carbon dioxide) of noticeable mass. Weigh a chunk of dry ice. Put the chunk in a plastic bag and tie it off. It will sublimate and turn into gas. The bag will expand noticeably. A solid, 44 g chunk of dry ice will expand to around 22.4 l of gas. Thus, we can again say that gases have mass and volume.

# INVESTIGATION 1.3

Take a glass stopper and tie it with a fine thread. Place an overflow jar on a wooden block and slowly pour water into it, till the water just starts overflowing through its spout. Stop pouring water at this moment. In this situation, the water in the overflow jar is at the verge of overflowing. Place a clean and dry beaker under the spout of the overflow jar.

Now, gently lower the glass stopper into the water in the overflow jar till it gets completely immersed. At the same time, the water overflows through the spout and collects in the beaker.

Remove the glass stopper from the overflow jar and pour the water collected in the beaker into the overflow jar. You notice that overflow jar is filled till the level of its spout.

Thus, we can conclude that solids and liquids occupy space. The glass stopper occupies space, and thus, pushes the water out of the overflow jar. The water also occupies space, as the water collected in the beaker fills the overflow jar till the level of its spout.

# INVESTIGATION 1.4

Take two empty glasses and a bucket of water. Turn a glass upside down and then push it into the water in the bucket. About 2/3 of the glass remains unfilled (without water) due to the air trapped in the glass, which does not allow water to rise up in the glass. Thus, we can say, air occupies space. Now bring the second glass in the water in a similar manner and keep it near the first one. Tilt the first glass gently and let the air from that escape into the second glass.

It is noticed that bubbles of air rush out from the first glass and to take its place water flows into the glass. Similarly, bubbles of air from the first glass take their place in the second glass.

Thus, it is observed that the first glass gets filled with water while in the second one, the volume of air increases.

From above investigations, we can conclude that:
(a) All material bodies have weight and hence, have mass.
(b) All material bodies occupy space.

The above two properties are necessary conditions for a substance to be a matter.

Hence, the matter of which the material bodies are made can be defined as:

A material which has mass and occupies space is called matter. What is matter comprised of?

The word matter is used to cover all the materials and substances, i.e., living and non-living of which universe is composed of.

You have seen a lot of objects around you. Do you know what the building blocks of all these objects are?

Let us take an example. Every building is made of bricks, mud, cement, sand, steel and wood. Whatever be the type of building, using these components, a building can be built.

Just like in the case of a building, every object is also thus, made up of tiny particles called atoms. Two or more atoms combine together to form molecules. A large number of atoms or molecules bind together to form the matter which we can touch, see or feel. Therefore, the building block of all matter is the atom.

STATES OF MATTER

Matter exists in three states:
(a) Solids: Substances like bottle, chair, table, bus and books, are solid states of matter.
(b) Liquids: Substances like water, milk, fruit juice and alcohol, are liquid states of matter.
(c) Gases: Substances like oxygen, nitrogen, carbon dioxide and steam, are gaseous states of matter.

Let us now study the characteristics of solids, liquids and gases.

CHARACTERISTICS OF A SOLID

(a) It has a definite shape.
Observe the things like cups, saucers, spoons, tables and chairs. Do they change their shapes? No. So, solids have a definite shape.

(b) It has a definite volume.
Ask your friend to sit on the wooden stool. After he sits, does the volume of the stool change? No, it does not.

(c) It may get heaped.
Take a bowl of common salt. Empty it on a plate. What do you observe?

The common salt does not flow. It gets heaped.  
From the previous examples, it can be concluded that:  
(i) Solids have definite shape.  
(ii) Solids have definite volume.  
(iii) Solids do not flow. They get heaped.  
(iv) Solids do not need a container to hold them.  

From the above characteristics, a solid can be defined as:  
*A material that has a definite shape and definite volume, such that it can have any number of free surfaces, is called solid.*

### CHARACTERISTICS OF A LIQUID  

Let us perform the following investigations to find out the characteristics of a liquid.  

---

### INVESTIGATION 1.5  

Take a small conical cup and fill it with water. Observe the shape of water. It resembles the shape of the conical cup.  

Its top surface is free and flat, i.e., it has only one free flat surface as shown in the Fig. 1.7 (a).  

Now pour water from the conical cup into a bowl. You will notice that the water flows into the bowl and takes the shape of the bowl, while its top surface is free and flat. Pour the water from the bowl to a small bottle. You will notice that the water flows into the bottle and its top surface is free and flat.

### INVESTIGATION 1.6  

Take an unused disposable syringe. Fill half of the syringe with water by drawing the plunger outward. Close the nozzle of the syringe with a rubber cork.  

Now push the plunger inward with maximum force and record your observations. Stop applying force and record your observations.  

You observe that the plunger does not move inward on the application of force. When the force is removed, the plunger does not move backward. Thus, the volume of water does not change. So, we can conclude that liquids do not change their volume when compressive forces are applied.  

---

**Fig. 1.8** : Liquids do not get compressed  

---

From the above two investigations, we can conclude that liquids have the following characteristics:

(a) They have no definite shape.  
They take the shape of the containing vessel.  

(b) They have a definite volume.  
(c) They have only one definite free surface, which is on top of the vessel in which they are contained.  
This surface is always free and flat.  

(d) They cannot be compressed i.e., their volume does not change due to compressive force.  

(e) They always need some container, otherwise they start flowing.  
From the above characteristics, a liquid can be defined as:  

A material which has a definite volume, but no definite shape and has only one free surface, is called liquid.

### CHARACTERISTICS OF A GAS  

#### INVESTIGATION 1.7  

**Fig. 1.9 :** Gases take the shape of the container  

Take an uninflated balloon, a football and a rubber doll and inflate all of them with air. You will notice that all of them are filled with air, but are of different shapes, i.e., shapes of the containing vessels — balloon, football and doll respectively. This suggests that air (or gases) have no shape of their own.  

---

#### INVESTIGATION 1.8  

**Fig. 1.10 :** Gases fill all the space available to them  

Take out a fresh incense stick from a pack and hold it in a stand. Place the stand in the corner of a room. Light the sticks. You will be able to smell the fragrance everywhere in the room.  

Thus, we can conclude that gases have no definite volume. They expand to fill all the space available to them.  

---

#### INVESTIGATION 1.9  

**Fig. 1.11 :** Gases get compressed  

Take an unused disposable syringe. Fill half of the syringe with air by drawing its plunger outward. Close the nozzle of the syringe with a rubber cork. Now push the plunger inward with the maximum force and observe it. Stop applying force and again observe it.  

You observe that you are able to push the plunger somewhat inward. After the compressive force is removed, the plunger again returns back to its original position.  

Thus, we can conclude that gases (like air) are compressible, unlike solids and liquids.

# Did You Know?

Gases, on heating to tens of thousands of degrees Celsius, change into another state of matter called **plasma**. At such a high temperature, the atoms of gas break into positively and negatively charged particles. The matter in the Sun is in plasma state. Similarly, the gases in fluorescent tubes are in plasma state.

### (a) Gases have no definite shape.
They take the shape of the containing vessel.

### (b) Gases have no definite volume.
They have a property to fill the entire space available to them.

### (c) Gases have no free surface.

### (d) Gases can be easily compressed.

From the above characteristics we can define a gas as:

> A material which has neither definite shape nor definite volume, is easily compressed and has no free surface, is called **gas**.

# Why Matter Exists in Three Different States

Matter consists of extremely small particles, which are not visible to the unaided eye. The **smallest particle of matter which can exist independently is named molecule**. The molecules are in the state of continuous motion. This motion does not stop with time. Due to the continuous motion, all the molecules have kinetic energy. The spaces in between the molecules are called **intermolecular spaces**. The molecules attract each other with a force, called **intermolecular force**. The intermolecular force increases as the intermolecular space decreases and vice-versa.

For example, when we heat ice (a solid) its molecules begin to move faster and farther apart until water reaches a liquid state. If we continue to heat the liquid, the molecules move even faster and farther apart to become water vapour, which is a gas.

## Solid

In case of solid, the intermolecular force is very strong and the intermolecular spaces very small. So, the molecules are not free to change their positions. Thus, they are tightly packed.

So, a solid has definite shape and volume. It cannot be compressed due to its very strong intermolecular force and very small intermolecular space.

## Liquid

In case of liquid, intermolecular force is weak and intermolecular space is more as compared to solid. So, the molecules are free to change their positions within the liquid. The molecules cannot leave the liquid.

Thus, a liquid has definite volume but not a definite shape. It can flow and needs a container to hold it.

## Gas

In case of gas, intermolecular force is very small and intermolecular space is far more as compared to liquid. So, the molecules are free to move in all directions and can fill the entire space available to them. They need a container to hold them.
