# UNIT 1 – NATURAL AND ARTIFICIAL MAGNETS

## DISCOVERY OF MAGNET

There is no known exact date or year when the magnet was discovered. However, as the story goes, there used to live a shepherd boy **Manganus**, in the town of Magnesia in Asia Minor (Greece). He used to carry a wooden staff provided with an iron sole. One day while roaming on **Mount Ida**, he placed his staff on a blackish rock and its iron sole got stuck to it. He was terrified. He thought some spirit under the rock was trying to hold his wooden staff.

This blackish rock is actually an ore of iron. It is called **magnetite**. The name of this rock came from the town of Magnesia, where it was discovered. As this rock attracted pieces of iron towards itself, therefore any other substance which had similar properties to iron was called 'lodestone'.

### NOTES

Iron, cobalt, nickel and steel are called **ferromagnetic substances**. The word ferro is coined from Latin name of iron, i.e., ferrum. The substances which behave like iron as far as magnetic properties are concerned are called ferromagnetic substances.

### NOTES

A mixture of ferric oxide and barium oxide is strongly magnetic. It is commonly called **"ferrite"**. It is used for making very powerful magnets for radios and transistors.

## MAGNETIC AND NON-MAGNETIC SUBSTANCES

### Magnetic Substances

Magnetic substances are substances which are strongly attracted by magnetic fields. They are divided into two categories: natural and artificial.

### Non-magnetic Substances

Non-magnetic substances are substances which are not attracted by magnetic fields. They are divided into two categories: natural and artificial.

---

## NATURAL AND ARTIFICIAL MAGNETS

**Natural Magnet**: A material which occurs naturally and has magnetic properties is called **natural magnet**.

**Artificial Magnet**: A substance to which properties of natural magnet are imparted by artificial means, is called **artificial magnet**.

The magnets made of iron, steel, cobalt or nickel are called artificial magnets. These magnets are of different sizes and shapes as illustrated in Fig. 6.3.

## Why We Make Artificial Magnets

These days magnets are used in a number of devices such as telephones, loud-speakers, radios, televisions, etc. For making these devices, we need powerful magnets. However, natural magnets are not very powerful. Following are the reasons of using artificial magnets:

1. They can be made very powerful, which is not possible in case of natural magnets.
2. They can be made in any desired shape or size. It is not possible in case of natural magnets which break when shaped with cutting tools.

You have already seen that maximum number of common pins cling at the ends of a bar magnet.

## PROPERTIES OF A MAGNET

After performing the above eight investigations, we are in a position to sum up the properties of magnet.

1. **A magnet attracts magnetic substances** (like iron, steel, cobalt, nickel) towards itself.

2. When suspended freely, it points in **north-south direction**.

3. Like poles of magnets **repel** each other.

4. Unlike poles of magnets **attract** each other.

5. When a bar magnet is **rubbed over an unmagnetised piece of iron or steel**, it changes into a magnet.

6. When a magnet is broken, then each piece is a **complete magnet**.

7. Magnetic force can **easily pass through** non-magnetic substances.

8. If a magnet is **heated or handled roughly**, it loses its magnetism.

## MAKING OF MAGNETS

1. **Single Touch Method**: The specimen to be magnetised is placed flat on the table. A permanent bar magnet is taken and its one pole is placed on one end of the piece. The bar magnet is then drawn to the other end, keeping it in the inclined position as shown in **Fig. 6.14**. The permanent magnet is then lifted and the process is repeated several times. The specimen is then turned over and the other side is also magnetised in the same way.

   The specimen gets magnetised. Its starting end gets the same polarity as the polarity of the magnet touching it. The polarity at the other end of the specimen is opposite to that of the magnetising pole.

2. **Divided Touch Method**: The specimen to be magnetised is placed flat on a table. Opposite poles of two strong bar magnets of equal strength are placed together in the middle of the specimen. The ends of bar magnets are drawn towards the opposite ends of the specimen, keeping the bar magnets inclined as shown in **Fig. 6.15**. The magnets are then lifted. The process is repeated several times.

   The specimen is then turned over and the other side is also magnetised in the same way. The end of the specimen where the south pole of the bar magnet leaves, becomes the north pole. Similarly, the end of the specimen where the north pole of the bar magnet leaves, becomes south pole.

   For strong magnetisation, the two ends of the specimen are supported on the two poles of two other bar magnets, such that the pole of one magnet is placed on the pole of the other magnet.

3. **Double Touch Method**: This method is almost similar to the divided touch method. The only difference is that a piece of wood or cork is placed between the two opposite poles of the permanent magnets. The magnets are then moved together from the middle to the one end and then to the other end without lifting them from the specimen as shown in **Fig. 6.16**. This process is repeated several times.

The polarities on the ends of the specimen are of the opposite nature to that of the nearer poles of stroking magnets (**Fig. 6.16**). For strong magnetisation, the specimen is mounted on two permanent bar magnets as mentioned in the divided touch method.

### MAKING A MAGNET WITH THE HELP OF ELECTRIC CURRENT [ELECTROMAGNET]

**Fig. 6.17** - Electromagnet setup

Take an iron nail about 8 cm long and wind an insulated copper wire over it as shown in **Fig. 6.17**. Bare the ends of copper wire over it from insulation. Fix each end of bare copper wire on the terminals of dry cell. Hold the copper wire at terminals with adhesive tape.

Bring the above arrangement near the iron paper clips placed on the table. It is observed that iron paper clips cling to the iron nail. This proves that iron nail changes to magnet when current flows around it.

Now remove the wire from the dry cell. It is found that paper clips fall down. From this, we conclude that the iron nail behaves like a magnet only as long as the current flows around it. The strength of the electromagnet can be increased either by increasing the current in the coil or by increasing the number of turns of the coil.

A magnet which operates only when electric current flows around iron is called **electromagnet**.

---

### CHOICE OF MATERIAL FOR THE CORE OF ELECTROMAGNET [IRON V/S STEEL]

It has been found that if a current carrying copper wire is wound on iron nail (soft iron), then it gets highly magnetised. Thus, the strength of magnetic field strongly increases. In such a situation, the arrangement is called **electromagnet**. It behaves like a magnet only as long as the current flows through it. However, when current is stopped, it gets demagnetised as soft iron cannot retain magnetism.

However, if instead of soft iron, a bar of steel is used, then it changes to permanent magnet. But, the magnetic strength in steel is not as high as in soft iron.

Thus, we can say that soft iron is used as core of electromagnet, which loses its magnetism, as soon as electric current stops flowing through the coil. But, its strength can be increased by increasing current or number of turns in the coil. Its polarity can be changed also by changing the direction of current. But, when bar of steel is placed in copper coil, then it changes to permanent magnet, whose strength and polarity cannot be changed. It does not need electric current to work. But, its strength is less than in soft iron.

**Magnetic Induction**: The phenomenon due to which a piece of steel or iron behaves like a magnet when placed near a strong magnet is called **magnetic induction**.

**Induced magnetic poles**: The magnetic poles formed at ends of iron or steel when placed near strong bar magnet are called **induced magnetic poles**.

**Why do two common pins attached to the pole of a bar magnet diverge outward, whereas if attached to each end of the horse shoe magnet converge inward?**

In case of bar magnet, the pin heads acquire south polarity, whereas pin tips acquire north polarity.

As like poles repel each other, therefore pins diverge, as illustrated in **Fig. 6.18**.

In case of horse-shoe magnet, the pin head at the south pole acquires north polarity and the pin tip south polarity. The pin head at north pole acquires south polarity and the pin tip acquires north polarity as illustrated in **Fig. 6.19**. As the opposite poles attract each other, therefore the pins converge inward.

### KEY IDEAS

It has been found that the more current flows through the coil, the more powerful is the electromagnet.

### TEMPORARY AND PERMANENT MAGNETS

The magnets which lose their magnetism as soon as the cause producing them is removed, are called **temporary magnets**.

Electromagnets and the magnets made from soft iron are temporary magnets.

The magnets which do not lose their magnetism, when the cause producing them is removed, are called **permanent magnets**.

The magnets made from steel are permanent magnets.

# STORING MAGNETS

If a magnet is left to itself, it loses its power over a long period of time, i.e. it gets demagnetised. To prevent demagnetisation, the bar magnets are arranged in pairs, separated by a piece of wooden block with opposite poles facing each other. Two soft iron bars are placed at the either end of the pair of magnets. These soft iron bars are called **magnetic keepers**. This system forms a giant magnetic chain, and hence, protects them from de-magnetisation (Fig. 6.20).

---

# DEMAGNETISATION OF A MAGNET

A magnet can be demagnetised by the following ways:
- By heating the magnet to a high temperature.
- By hammering the magnet frequently.
- By flowing alternating current through a coil around the magnet and magnet is kept in East-West direction.
- By rough handling.
- By dropping the magnet frequently.

# UNIT 3 – MAGNETIC FIELD AND MAGNETIC LINES OF FORCE

## INTRODUCTION

In the previous units, we have learnt that a magnet attracts substances such as iron and steel from a distance. A magnet has two poles - north pole and south pole. When a magnet is suspended freely, it is oriented in the geographic **north-south** direction. Like magnetic poles repel each other and unlike poles attract each other, when brought close together. Also, when a bar magnet is broken into two halves, then each half is a similar magnet.

Let's understand how a magnet interacts from a distance and why the freely suspended bar magnet points in the geographic **north-south** direction.

## MAGNETIC FIELD AND MAGNETIC LINES OF FORCE

### Magnetic Field

Before we discuss magnetic field, let us perform the following investigation.

Take a drawing board and fix a smooth white sheet of paper over it. On the paper, sprinkle fine powder of iron (iron filings), such that it is spread evenly on paper.

Now take a bar magnet and place it on the table in north-south direction. Over the magnet, place the drawing board, such that magnet is in the middle of drawing board.

Tap the drawing board gently. What do you observe? It is seen that in a small space around the magnet, iron filings set themselves in the form of curves. Why does this happen? The iron filings move and reposition themselves because they are affected by the magnetic force of bar magnet.

**Fig. 6.24**

The space around the magnet where its influence can be detected, is called the **magnetic field**.

The magnetic field is filled with the magnetic lines of force. What are magnetic lines of force?

In simple words, we can say that the paths along which iron filings will re-adjust in a magnetic field will be the magnetic lines of force.

If you study Fig. 6.24 carefully, you will find that these magnetic lines of force are highly concentrated near poles. Furthermore, they are closed continuous curves. However, we cannot tell, in which direction these lines of force move. It means, we cannot tell whether they are going from magnetic north pole to magnetic south pole or from magnetic south pole to magnetic north pole. For finding their direction, we will perform the following experiment.

### Experiment to Draw Magnetic Lines of Force around a Bar Magnet

Take a drawing board and fix a white sheet of paper on it. Draw a line in the middle of paper along its length. On the line place the magnet NS and draw its boundary. Mark a point O at the edge of boundary as shown in Fig. 6.25.

Take a small plotting compass and place it in such a way, that its south pole is pointing towards point O. Mark the point 1 in direction in which north pole of plotting compass is pointing.

Now lift the plotting compass and place it in such a way that its south pole points towards point O. Mark the position of north pole with point 2. Repeat the process and obtain points 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, etc., as shown in Fig. 6.26.

Join the points 0, 1, 2, etc., by a smooth curve. The curve so obtained will be magnetic line of force and its direction will be the direction in which north pole of plotting compass needle points.

### Definition of Magnetic Line of Force

It is a closed continuous curve in a magnetic field along which the north pole will move if free to do so, and its direction is given by the direction in which free north pole will point.

### Properties of Magnetic Lines of Force

1. They are continuous curves and form a closed loop.
2. They are directed from north pole to south pole outside the magnet and from south to north inside the magnet.
3. They tend to contract longitudinally, that is, they bend along the length of magnet.
4. They tend to repel each other.
5. They never intersect with each other.
6. Their number determines the strength of magnetic field.

### Magnetic Field around a Bar Magnet

Take a drawing board and fix a white sheet of paper over it. In the middle of paper, draw a straight line along the length of paper. Place the bar magnet along the line and draw its boundary. Place the board in north-south direction. With the help of plotting compass, plot magnetic lines of force. The pattern of magnetic lines of force so obtained will be as shown in Fig. 6.27.

## EARTH AS A HUGE MAGNET

Let us perform the following investigation.

### INVESTIGATION 6.10

With the help of a magnetic compass, mark north-south direction in one corner of your lawn. Along this direction, dig a small trench about 10 cm long, 4 cm wide and 10 cm deep.

Now take a steel rod about 8 cm long and apply a little oil to it so as to prevent rusting. Wrap this steel piece in a polythene bag and bury it in the trench in north-south direction for two weeks.

After two weeks, take out the steel rod and test its ends with a freely suspended magnetic needle. It will be seen that its one end acts as a weak north pole and other end as a weak south pole.

Thus, we can conclude that steel piece has changed into a weak magnet. Why is it so? The only answer is that the Earth acts as a giant magnet, and hence, induces magnetism in the steel rod, when placed along the direction of Earth's magnetic field.

---

In Investigation 6.10, we found that the Earth is a huge magnet. It is believed (it is not true scientifically) that approximately along the geographic north and south poles of the earth is buried a giant magnet.

The south pole of this magnet is oriented towards the geographic north pole and the north pole of this magnet is towards the geographic south pole. It is because a freely suspended magnetic needle points towards the geographic north and south directions. It means that free north pole of the magnetic needle is attracted towards magnetic south pole of earth. Similarly, the south pole of the free magnetic needle is attracted towards magnetic north pole of earth.

As we have said, the earth behaves like a magnet. Therefore, it must have an area around it where its influence can be felt. This area where earth's magnetic influence is felt, is called earth's magnetic field.

Earth's magnetic field is strongest near the magnetic north and south poles and weakest near equator, much the same way as the magnetic field of a permanent bar magnet.

# MAGNETIC COMPASS

A magnetic compass is a simple device used by sailors and navigators to find the direction in which their ship or aeroplane is going.

It consists of a flat circular aluminium box at the base of which are marked directions like north, south, east, west, north-east, north-west; etc. From its centre, rises a sharp needle over which is placed a magnetic needle. The magnetic needle is completely free to move in any direction. On the top of box, a circular glass plate is fixed. By looking at the north pole of magnetic needle, we can find the given direction.

# KEYWORDS

1. **Magnetism** - The phenomenon due to which certain substances attract pieces of iron, steel, cobalt and nickel.
2. **Natural magnet** - A material which occurs naturally and has magnetic properties.
3. **Artificial magnet** - A material to which properties of natural magnet are imparted by artificial means.
4. **Temporary magnet** - A magnet which loses its magnetism, when the cause which is imparting magnetic properties to it is removed.
5. **Permanent magnet** - A magnet which does not lose its magnetism, even when the cause which is imparting magnetic properties to it is removed.
6. **Magnetic needle** - A thin strip of steel, pointed at its ends, and points in north-south direction, when freely suspended or pivoted.
7. **Poles of magnet** - The ends of a magnet where most of its magnetic strength is located.
8. **Magnetic keepers** - Soft iron bars placed at the ends of a pair of magnets to prevent demagnetisation.
9. **Electromagnet** - A magnet which operates only when electric current flows around iron.
10. **Magnetic lines of force** - A closed continuous curve in a magnetic field along which the north pole will move if free to do so.
11. **Magnetic field** - The space around a magnet where its influence can be detected.
12. **Magnetic compass** - A device used by sailors and navigators to find direction.
