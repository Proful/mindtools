## UNIT 1 – MEASUREMENT OF LENGTH

### IMPORTANCE OF MEASUREMENT IN EVERYDAY LIFE

Very often, people make guesses. We can guess how far Delhi is from Bengaluru or how warm a room is. If we think carefully about our guess and use all our knowledge and common sense, we are making an **estimate**. Estimates are often very useful, but they are not exact and can sometimes be very wrong. Sometimes our senses can mislead us.

Most of the measurements we make in physics need to be fairly accurate. In this chapter, we shall study some of the basic tools and know how they are employed to have basic or fundamental measurements.

---

### Example 1

Imagine you take a piece of cloth to a tailor for making a shirt. What will the tailor do? He will first measure the length of cloth with measuring tape. Then, he will take measurements of your body. After noting all this, he will cut the cloth.

Thus, we can say that **length** is a basic quantity and **measuring tape** is the **tool** employed to measure length.

---

### Example 2

Imagine you are going for a school picnic at 7 a.m. tomorrow. What will you do? You will set your alarm clock at 5 a.m. at night. Then you will get up in the morning and clean your teeth, take bath and get ready by 6 a.m. You will leave for your school on foot as you know that it is an hour's walk. What are you measuring all along? You are measuring time and are doing certain activities in a specified time.

Thus, we can say that **time** is a basic quantity and **clock** is the **tool** employed to measure time.

---

### Example 3

Imagine you have to buy vegetables from the market. You just cannot estimate whether the mass of vegetables required by you is one kg or two kg, unless the shopkeeper weighs it by a balance.

In this case **mass** is a basic quantity and the **balance** is the **tool** employed to find mass.

---

### Example 4

Imagine you are having fever. The doctor cannot tell your temperature, by merely putting his hand on your forehead. What will the doctor do? He will use thermometer to record your temperature.

In this case **temperature** is a basic quantity and **thermometer** is the **tool** employed to find temperature.

## MEASUREMENT OF LENGTH

**Length** is the measure of distance between two points.

Fig. 2.3(a) shows two rods, AB and CD, which are almost equal in length. However, to know which rod is longer, we can place them side by side as shown in Fig. 2.3(b). Thus, we can see that rod CD is longer than rod AB.

### Need for a Scale

A third object used for comparing the lengths of two other objects is called **scale** or **unit**. Following are the advantages of using a scale:

(i) Scale can be easily taken from one place to another place.

(ii) Scale does not change during measurements.

However, there are situations when you just cannot move objects and place them side by side. Take the example of your teacher's table and the dining table at your home. If you want to know which of the two is longer, you just can neither carry teacher's table to your house nor your dining table to school.

However, if you measure the length of teacher's table by the spans of your palm and compare it with the length of dining table by the spans of your palm, you will easily find out which one of the two tables is longer.

Now think what you did to find the answer. You did not move any of the table. Instead, to make the measurement, you used third object, that is, the palm of your hand.

---

## NEED FOR A STANDARD SCALE (OR UNIT)

In the early days, people used to measure length with the help of parts of body, such as outstretched palm, foot, arm or cubit.

**King Henry I** declared that distance from the tip of his nose to the stretched thumb of his sideways stretched arm should be used as unit of length. He called this length as **yard**.

### Body Parts Used as Units

| Body Part | Average Length Range |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Outstretched palm | 18 cm to 21 cm |
| Foot length | 20 cm to 27 cm |
| Cubit or arm length | 40 cm to 45 cm |
| Yard | 80 cm to 90 cm |

You can see that such units cannot serve as standard for measuring length. The simple reason is that they are going to be different for different people.

## KEY IDEAS ABOUT MEASUREMENT

(i) Any quantity which we study in physics is called a **physical quantity**.

(ii) Measurement of a physical quantity means comparing it with a known **standard quantity**. Each accepted standard quantity is the standard of measurement and is commonly called **unit**.

### Characteristics of a Unit

The unit of a physical quantity should have the following characteristics:
- It should be of convenient size.
- Its value (magnitude) should not change with respect to place or time.

### Parts of a Measurement

A measurement of physical quantity is made up of two parts:
1. **Magnitude** - the numerical value
2. **Unit** - the standard of comparison

For example, if the length of a park is 200 m, then:
- **200** is magnitude
- **metre** is the standard unit

---

## STANDARD UNIT

A unit which is acceptable to majority of the people as a basic unit of measurement is called **standard unit**.

The scientists all over the world have accepted a basic set of units for measuring basic physical quantities, that is, measurement of mass, length, time, temperature, etc. This set of units is called **Standard International system of units**. It is generally referred to as **SI system of units**.

---

## STANDARD UNITS OF LENGTH

According to SI system of units, the fundamental unit of length is **metre**. In short form, metre is written as **m**.

### Definition of metre

It is the length between two fine marks on a metal bar made of platinum-iridium alloy. This bar is maintained at constant temperature and is placed on two rollers. It is kept near Paris in France.

**OR**

One metre is one ten-millionth (1/10,000,000) of the distance from the Equator to the North Pole along the longitude passing through Paris in France.

---

### Multiples of Metre

The metre is not a convenient unit for expressing long lengths. For example, the distance between Amritsar (border city of India) and New Delhi is 470,000 metres or 470 thousand metres. It is far more convenient to say that the distance between Amritsar and New Delhi is 470 **kilometres**.

---

### DID YOU KNOW?

For measuring extremely large distances, such as distance of the stars from the Earth, the kilometre is too small of a unit. For measurement of such large distances, the commonly used unit is **light year**.

Light year is equal to the distance travelled by light in one year at a constant speed of $$\( 3 \times 10^5 \, \text{km} \, \text{per second} \)$$.

Its magnitude is \( 9.46 \times 10^{12} \, \text{km} \).

---

### DID YOU KNOW?

For measuring extremely small distances, such as diameter of a molecule or atom or other tiny objects, the most commonly used units of length are **micrometre** or **angstrom**.

\[ 1 \, \text{micrometre} (\mu \text{m}) = 10^{-6} \, \text{m} \]

\[ 1 \, \text{angstrom} (\text{Å}) = 10^{-10} \, \text{m} = 10^{-7} \, \text{mm} \]

---

### Submultiples of Metre

Some lengths are much smaller than a metre. For example, the length of a pencil is much smaller than a metre. Thus, it is inconvenient to express such lengths in metres. A pencil is of length \( 0.15 \, \text{m} \) or \( 3/20 \, \text{m} \).

To express such lengths, a sub-multiple of metre is used. It is called **centimetre** (in short form **cm**). The word **centi** means one hundredth part of a metre.

\[ 100 \, \text{cm} = 1 \, \text{m} \]

Some lengths are even smaller than the centimetre. For example, thickness of coins is generally far smaller than a centimetre. To express such small lengths conveniently, we use another sub-multiple of metre. It is called **millimetre** (in short form **mm**). The word **milli** means one thousandth part of a metre.

\[ 1000 \, \text{mm} = 1 \, \text{m} \]

---

### Relationship Between Units of Length

\[ 10 \, \text{millimetres} \, (\text{mm}) = 1 \, \text{centimetre} \, (\text{cm}) \]

\[ 100 \, \text{centimetres} \, (\text{cm}) = 1 \, \text{metre} \, (\text{m}) \]

\[ 1000 \, \text{metres} \, (\text{m}) = 1 \, \text{kilometre} \, (\text{km}) \]

---

### The Metre Scale

If you look at metre scale, it has 100 divisions. Each division is equal to one centimetre. Furthermore, each centimetre has ten more divisions. Each division is equal to one millimetre.

Fig. 2.5 shows a centimetre scale commonly found in your geometry boxes. It has a length of 15 cm. Each centimetre is divided into ten equal parts. Each small part is equal to one millimetre.

## INCH (in) AND FOOT (ft) AS UNITS OF LENGTH

In the UK, the foot along with inch, yard and mile, were officially started as the primary units of measurement for road signs and related measurements of distance and speed. The foot was also used globally to measure the altitude in the aviation industry.

The foot is used as a base unit in the **FPS system**, which uses **feet, pounds and seconds** to derive other units of measurement. The FPS system has been largely replaced by **SI system**, based on metres, kilograms and seconds.

### Inch (in)
- An **inch** is a unit of measurement in the American standard of measurements.
- Twelve inches equals one foot.
- Since 1959, the inch has been defined officially as **2.54 cm**.
- The symbol used for inch is **'in'**.

### Foot (ft)
- A foot (pl. feet) is a common length unit in imperial system and the current US customary unit system.
- A foot is equal to **0.3048 metre**.
- A foot has 12 inches, and 3 feet make a yard.
- The symbol for foot is **'ft'**.

## HOW TO MEASURE LENGTH ACCURATELY WITH METRE SCALE

Generally, we use a plastic metre scale or half metre scale for taking ordinary measurements. It is marked in centimetres as well as millimetres. Suppose we want to measure the length of a rectangular wooden block.

The zero mark on the scale is made to coincide with one end of the wooden block as illustrated in Fig. 2.6 (a). The reading on the other end of the wooden block gives its length.

---

### ERROR WHILE MEASURING LENGTH

**Error due to thickness of scale:**

As a plastic metre scale has some thickness, we may make error if we do not hold eye in correct position.

- If the eye is placed in position **A**, the reading is **5.7 cm** (wrong)
- If the eye is at position **C**, the reading is **5.5 cm** (wrong)
- The correct position of the eye is **B** which is vertically above the end of the wooden block.

The correct length of wooden block is **5.6 cm**.

**For more accurate reading:**
- The metre scale should be placed along its **edge** as illustrated in Fig. 2.6 (b).
- Scale with marked end **bevelled** or **tapered** should be used as illustrated in Fig. 2.7.

---

## ESTIMATING LENGTHS TO 1 mm OR 1 cm

In Fig 2.6 (b), the correct length of the wooden block is 5.6 cm. However, while measuring the length of another similar block, it is just possible that reading is between 5.6 cm and 5.7 cm. In such a situation, an **estimate** is made.

- If the far edge of the block is more towards **7 mm mark**, the reading is taken as **5.7 cm**.
- If the far edge of the block is more towards the **6 mm mark**, then reading is **5.6 cm**.

**For lengths more than 1 m:**
Estimation is done in centimetres. For example, if the length of a field is 50 m and between 78 cm and 79 cm, then approximate length will be either 50.78 m or 50.79 m.

- If the far end is more towards **78 cm mark**, estimated length is **50.78 m**.
- If the far end is more towards **79 cm mark**, estimated length is **50.79 m**.

## INVESTIGATION 2.2

### To find thickness of a coin by a centimetre scale or millimetre scale

Imagine you are asked to find the thickness of a five rupees coin by using a centimetre scale. If you use one such coin to measure thickness as shown in Fig. 2.8 (a), it will not be very accurate.

**Method:**
1. Take **20 such coins** and place them one upon another as shown in Fig. 2.8 (b).
2. Measure the total thickness of 20 coins.
3. Divide the total thickness by 20.

\[ \text{Thickness of 20 coins} = x \text{ cm} \]

\[ \text{Thickness of one coin} = \frac{x}{20} \text{ cm} \]

This will give a more accurate thickness of one coin.

---

## INVESTIGATION 2.3

### Diameter of a wire using a centimetre scale

Imagine you are asked to find the diameter of thin copper wire by using a centimetre scale. If you directly use the centimetre scale you will notice that the diameter of wire is less than 1 mm.

**Method:**
1. Wind the wire over a smooth pencil in the form of a **tight coil**, such that there are **30 turns** in the coil as shown in Fig. 2.9.
2. Measure the length of the coil.

\[ \text{Length of the coil} = \frac{\text{No. of turns in the coil}}{30} = 0.05 \text{ cm} = 0.5 \text{ mm} \]

## INVESTIGATION 2.4

### To measure length of curved line by using a thread

**Materials Required:**
- A white sheet of paper on which a curved line is drawn
- About 10 common pins
- A drawing board or foam board

**Method:**
1. Fix common pins on the sharp ends of curved line. In Fig. 2.10, A, B ...... F, etc., are the pins fixed on the curved line.
2. Tie a knot with cotton thread at the common pin A.
3. Using fingers of both hands, move the thread along the curved line such that it goes around pins B, C ...... F, etc.
4. Care should be taken that thread is **neither too tight nor too loose**, when moved along the curved line.
5. When the thread reaches the extreme end of the curved line, cut it from that point.
6. Remove the thread from A and then place it straight along the length of measuring tape.
7. The length of the thread is equal to the length of curved line.

## INVESTIGATION 2.5

### To measure the length of a curved surface such as wooden cylinder

**Materials Required:**
- A 15 cm scale or half metre rod
- A fine cotton thread about 20 cm long
- A wooden cylinder

**Method:**
1. Tie a knot at an end of the string. The knot represents starting point or the zero point of the string. Let us call this knotted point **A**.
2. Now wrap the string around the curved surface of the wooden cylinder.
3. Mark a point on the string with a pen, which meets the knotted point A, as shown in Fig. 2.11(a). Let the point be **B**.
4. Now spread the string straight on the 15 cm scale, such that it is **neither too taut nor too loose**, as shown in Fig. 2.11(b).
5. The length between the points **A** and **B** is the length of the curved surface of the wooden cylinder.

## MEASUREMENT OF TIME

**Time** is defined as an **interval (gap) between two events**.

For the measurement of time, we need some unit. Since the earliest days, the length of the day was taken as unit of time.

### Solar Day and Mean Solar Day

We know that the Earth rotates about its axis. This causes day and night. However, to the people on the surface of the Earth it appears as if the Sun is revolving around the Earth. The Sun appears to be at its highest position at **noon**.

- The interval between two successive noons is called a **solar day**.
- However, the distance of Earth from the Sun keeps on changing. Due to this change in distance, the length of solar day increases or decreases.
- The average of all solar days in which the Earth completes one revolution around the Sun is called the **mean solar day**.

### Units of Time

| Unit | Relation |
|------|----------|
| **Hour** | 1 mean solar day = 24 hours |
| **Minute** | 1 hour = 60 minutes |
| **Second** | 1 minute = 60 seconds |

**Summary:**
- 1 mean solar day = 24 hours
- 1 hour = 60 minutes
- 1 minute = 60 seconds

### Instruments for Measuring Time

- **Jantar Mantar** - Ancient astronomical instrument (Fig. 2.17a)
- **Stop clock** - Modern instrument for precise time measurement (Fig. 2.17b)
- Clock, Watch, Stop-watch - Common time-measuring devices

### READING TWENTY FOUR HOUR CLOCK

The time of arrival or departure of trains, aeroplanes, etc., is given by **24 hour clock**, as a day consists of 24 hours.

In **12 hour clock**:
- Time from 12 o'clock at night to 12 o'clock at noon is given a suffix **a.m.**
- Time from 12 o'clock noon to 12 o'clock night is given a suffix **p.m.**

### Rules for converting 24-hour time to 12-hour time

1. **00 hours** means 12 o'clock at night, i.e., midnight.
2. **12 hours** means 12 o'clock at noon.
3. The time between 00 hours to 12 o'clock at noon is taken as **a.m.**
4. In order to convert time between 12 hours to 24 hours on the 24 hour clock into p.m. on 12 hour clock, **12 is subtracted** from the given time.

### ADVANTAGES OF TWENTY FOUR HOUR CLOCK

- In this system, **a.m. or p.m. or morning or evening is not used**. This prevents a lot of confusion.
- The time is shown by **four digits**. The first two digits represent hours and next two digits represent minutes.
- The time mentioned from **00.01 to 12.00** represent morning to noon.
- The time mentioned from **12.01 to 23.59** represents noon to midnight.

**Examples:**
- 10.30 on this clock means **10.30 in the morning**
- 19.30 on this clock means (19.30 - 12) = **07.30 in the evening**
- The **same time is not repeated** in this clock for 24 hours

## UNIT 3 – TEMPERATURE AND ITS MEASUREMENT

### MEASUREMENT OF TEMPERATURE

When you touch ice, you feel cold. When you dip your finger in hot water, you feel hot. When you have fever, your body gets warm. The doctor takes temperature of your body to find how much fever you are running.

**Temperature** is a measure of the degree of **hotness or coldness** of a body.

**SI unit of temperature** is **kelvin (K)**.

### Thermometer

A device used for measuring the temperature of different objects is called a **thermometer**.

**Description of a Thermometer:**

Figure 2.20 shows the common thermometer used in laboratories. It is called **celsius thermometer**.

- The thermometer consists of a very fine glass tube having a very thin bore commonly called **capillary tube**.
- One end of the capillary tube is provided a very thin glass **bulb**.
- This bulb is filled with **pure mercury**.
- The other end of the capillary tube is **sealed**, after removing air from it.
- The capillary tube is protected by a thick glass tube called **stem**.
- The markings are made on the stem from **-10°C to 110°C**. These markings are called **graduations** or **degrees**.

### Important Points About Temperature

- The temperature of a given body can be **less than zero degree celsius**. Such a temperature is expressed by placing sign of minus before the temperature, e.g., **-10°C** or **-100°C**.
- The temperature of a flame or hot oil can be **above 100°C**.

---

### THERMOMETRIC SCALES

#### (a) Celsius Scale

- This scale is commonly used for measuring day to day temperatures.
- The lowest point on this scale is **zero** and is expressed as **0°C** (zero degree celsius). It corresponds to the **melting point of pure ice**.
- The highest point on this scale is **hundred** and is expressed as **100°C** (100 degree celsius). This corresponds to temperature at which **pure water turns into steam**.
- The length between these two points is divided into **100 equal parts** or markings. These markings are commonly called **graduation**.

#### (b) Standard International Scale or Kelvin Scale

The temperature of bodies can be below zero degree celsius. The question arises what is the last limit of temperature.

**Lord Kelvin** theoretically calculated that **-273°C** is the ultimate lower limit of temperature. This temperature could not be achieved even with the best of research tools available to the modern service.

- This last limit of temperature, i.e., **-273°C** was named as **absolute zero** by Lord Kelvin.
- Present day scientists have changed absolute zero to **Kelvin zero** in order to honour Lord Kelvin.
- The thermometer scale using Kelvin zero is called **Kelvin Scale**.

### Comparison of Celsius and Kelvin Scales

- **-273°C = 0 K**
- **0°C = 273 K**
- **100°C = 373 K**

**General formula** for converting celsius scale into kelvin scale:

\[
\text{Temperature in K} = 273 + \text{Temperature in °C}
\]

**To convert kelvin to celsius:**

\[
\text{Temperature in °C} = \text{Temperature in Kelvin} - 273
\]

### Key Points About Scales

- The temperature of **0°C** corresponds to the melting temperature of pure ice and is called the **lower standard point** or **lower fixed point**.
- The temperature of **100°C** corresponds to the boiling temperature of pure water and is called the **upper standard point** or **upper fixed point**.
- In kelvin scale (SI scale), **0°C** corresponds to **273 K** and **100°C** corresponds to **373 K**.

### Important Notes About Thermometers

- A celsius thermometer can measure temperatures between **0°C and 100°C**.
- It **cannot** measure higher temperatures, such as the temperature of a candle flame, because the thermometer will break due to excessive expansion of mercury.
- Modified form of celsius thermometer is used for recording the day's highest temperature and the night's lowest temperature. Such thermometer is called **maximum and minimum thermometer**.

### CLINICAL THERMOMETER (Doctor's Thermometer)

The normal body temperature of a healthy human being is **37°C**.

The thermometer used to measure the temperature of a human body is called **clinical thermometer** or **doctor's thermometer**.

**Features:**
- Marked from **35°C to 42°C** because:
  - Temperature of the human body does not fall below **35°C**
  - Temperature of the human body does not rise above **42°C**
  - In either case, **death occurs**
- Has a **triangular stem** with a very fine capillary tube
- The tube is provided with a **kink** near the bulb
- The **kink** allows the mercury to push its way in the capillary tube, but **does not allow it to flow back**, unless the thermometer is **jerked**

**How to Use:**
1. Wash the thermometer with water
2. Jerk it till mercury thread falls below **35°C**
3. Place it in the mouth cavity of the patient, under the tongue for **1 minute**
4. Take it out and read the temperature

### Key Points About Body Temperature

- Temperature of a healthy person is **37°C**
- If temperature is **above 37°C**, the person is running **fever**
- If temperature is **below 37°C**, the person is **weak**, i.e., his body is not generating enough heat energy

### Precautions

- The clinical thermometer should **never be washed with very hot water** or placed in the sun as it is likely to break.

### MEASUREMENT BY ESTIMATION

**Estimation** is a quick judgement about a measurement of some particular quantity.

In order to make estimations, we use our senses of **touch, sight, taste, smell or hearing**.

**Examples:**

1. **Sensation of Touch:** You have to decide quickly which piece of cloth is more rough than the other. You will just move your fingers on each piece and feel.

2. **Sensation of Taste:** Suppose you have to drink sweetened milk. You will add two or more spoons of sugar to it according to your taste. You just estimate the amount of sugar to be added by your sensation of taste.

---

### Important Questions Regarding Estimations:

**(a) When do we make estimations?**
- When we have to make a **quick judgement** and we do not have any tool to do so
- Even when we do have tools, because actual measurements may be **time consuming**

**(b) How do we make estimations?**
- We make use of our **five senses** and **similar experiences in the past**

---

### Limitations of Estimation

- Estimation is **not a very reliable method** of measurement
- One can easily go wrong
- However, by **careful practice** we can sharpen our skill in estimation

**Examples of Estimation in Daily Life:**
- A player while giving a pass to a fellow player in the game of hockey always estimates distance and hits the ball accordingly
- A fruit merchant will buy fruit orchards by estimating how much fruit he will get

### Find the average pulse rate of the class and the range of pulse rate of the students

**Materials Required:**
- A stop watch
- At least 20 students of your class

**Method:**
1. Write the name of each student on a white sheet of paper.
2. Feel the pulse of one student.
3. Once you are sure that you can feel pulse, start the stop watch.
4. Count the number of pulses in **one minute** and record it in front of the name of student.
5. Repeat the activity for the rest of students and record their pulse rates.
6. Add all the pulse rates and divide it with the number of students. This will give you the **average pulse rate** of class per minute.
7. Also find the student which has the **highest pulse rate** as well as the student which has **lowest pulse rate**. These numbers will give the **range of pulse rate** of the students of the class.

# Measurement of Area of a Regular Shape

Suppose you want to measure the area of a rectangular cardboard of length 6 cm and breadth 3 cm as shown in Fig. 2.26. The convenient unit to measure the area of a given cardboard should be cm².

Take a centimetre graph paper. Each small square on this graph paper has a side equal to 1 cm. Thus, the area of each small square on this graph paper is 1 cm² as illustrated in Fig. 2.27.

Place the cardboard PQRS on the centimetre graph paper and draw its outline with the help of a sharp pencil. Now remove the cardboard. Count the number of squares within the outline PQRS. The number of squares are 18.

Therefore,

**Area of the face of cardboard PQRS**

= Area of 18 small squares

= 18 × area of 1 small square

= 18 × 1 cm²

= **18 cm²** ............................ (i)

Notice that the length of the cardboard is 6 cm and its breadth is 3 cm. If we multiply length by breadth then:

Length × Breadth

= 6 cm × 3 cm

= **18 cm²** ............................ (ii)

If we compare (i) and (ii), then we can say that

**Area of the cardboard PQRS = Length × Breadth**

# Using Graph Paper to Find the Area of Irregular Shape

Suppose you want to measure the area of an irregular shape such as the shape of a leaf as shown in Fig. 2.28.

Place the leaf on the centimetre graph paper and draw its outline with the help of a sharp pencil as shown in Fig. 2.29.

Now, count the number of squares which are completely inside the outline. Also, count the number of incomplete squares which are half or more than half within the outline. Ignore the rest of the incomplete square which are less than half inside the outline.

Therefore,

**Approximate area of the leaf**

= (No. of complete squares + No. of incomplete squares which are half or more than half) × Area of 1 small square

= (2 + 4) × 1 cm²

= **6 cm²**

This is how the approximate area of irregular shape can be calculated.

## FORMULAE FOR CALCULATING AREAS OF REGULAR SURFACES

| Shape | Formula |
|-------|---------|
| Square | Area = Length × Length = (side)² |
| Rectangle | Area = Length × Breadth |
| Triangle | Area = \( \frac{1}{2} \times \text{Altitude} \times \text{Base} \) |
| Circle | Area = \( \pi \times (\text{Radius})^2 = \pi r^2 \) |

**Note:** \( \pi \) (pie) stands for \( \frac{22}{7} \) and the radius is the measure of shortest distance from centre of a circle to its circumference. Diameter of a circle is twice the radius of a circle.

---

### KEY IDEAS

1. The area of a regular surface can be found out by **counting the number of squares** enclosed by its boundary on a **graph paper**.
2. The area of a regular rectangular surface can also be found by finding the **product of its length and breadth**:
   \[
   \text{Area} = \text{Length} \times \text{Breadth}
   \]

## NUMERICAL PROBLEMS

### Problem 1
**A square piece of cardboard has side 25 cm. What is the area of cardboard in (a) cm² (b) mm² and (c) m²?**

**Solution:**

**(a) Area in cm²:**
\[
\text{Area} = (\text{side})^2 = (25 \, \text{cm})^2 = 625 \, \text{cm}^2
\]

**(b) Area in mm²:**
\[
1 \, \text{cm}^2 = 10 \, \text{mm} \times 10 \, \text{mm} = 100 \, \text{mm}^2
\]
\[
625 \, \text{cm}^2 = 625 \times 100 \, \text{mm}^2 = 62500 \, \text{mm}^2
\]

**(c) Area in m²:**
\[
1 \, \text{cm}^2 = \frac{1}{100 \, \text{m}} \times \frac{1}{100 \, \text{m}} = \frac{1}{10000} \, \text{m}^2
\]
\[
625 \, \text{cm}^2 = 625 \times \frac{1}{10000} \, \text{m}^2 = 0.0625 \, \text{m}^2
\]

---

### Problem 2
**The length of a hall is 15 m and breadth 4 m. Find the area of the hall in (a) m² (b) hectares.**

**Solution:**

**(a) Area in m²:**
\[
\text{Area} = \text{Length} \times \text{Breadth} = 15 \, \text{m} \times 4 \, \text{m} = 60 \, \text{m}^2
\]

**(b) Area in hectares:**
\[
1 \, \text{m}^2 = \frac{1}{10000} \, \text{hectare}
\]
\[
60 \, \text{m}^2 = \frac{1}{10000} \times 60 \, \text{hectare} = 0.006 \, \text{hectare}
\]

---

### Problem 3
**A flat circular plate has radius 14 cm. What is the area of plate?**

**Solution:**
\[
\text{Area of circle} = \pi r^2 = \frac{22}{7} \times (14 \, \text{cm})^2 = \frac{22}{7} \times 196 \, \text{cm}^2
\]
\[
= 616 \, \text{cm}^2
\]

---

### Problem 4
**The diameter of a circular field is 7 m. Find the area of the field.**

**Solution:**

Diameter = 7 m
\[
\therefore \text{Radius} = \frac{7}{2} \, \text{m} = 3.5 \, \text{m}
\]

\[
\text{Area of field} = \pi r^2 = \frac{22}{7} \times (3.5 \, \text{m})^2
\]
\[
= \frac{22}{7} \times 12.25 \, \text{m}^2 = 38.5 \, \text{m}^2
\]

---

### Problem 5
**A triangular cardboard has a base of 8 cm and its height upto vertex is 4 cm. What is the area of cardboard?**

**Solution:**

\[
\text{Area of triangular cardboard} = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}
\]
\[
= \frac{1}{2} \times 8 \, \text{cm} \times 4 \, \text{cm} = 16 \, \text{cm}^2
\]
