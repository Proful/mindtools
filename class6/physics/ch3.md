# UNIT 1 - FORCE AND ITS EFFECTS

## WHAT IS FORCE?

Force is the most commonly used word in everyday life. Let us consider a few examples.

### Example 1
Imagine you are going for a school picnic. The bus has some trouble with its battery. Driver asks the boys to push the bus. What do the boys do? **They apply force during pushing** so that the bus starts. When a body on the application of force, moves away from us, then the force so applied is called push.

### Example 2
Imagine that the door of your house is jammed as it often happens in rainy season. Your mother tries to pull it. However, the door does not open. She calls you for help. Both of you **apply force to pull the door.** When a body moves closer to us on the application of force, then such a force is called pull.

If you carefully examine the above examples, you will notice that word force is associated with either pull or push which causes some kind of motion.

In very simple terms, force is defined as push or pull.

---

## EFFECTS OF FORCE

From effects of force, we mean what force can do or what changes a force can bring about.

1. **A force can cause a motion in a stationary object**

When a force is applied on a stationary object it can make it move. For example, if you push a book placed on a table it starts moving. When you pull a chair, it starts moving.

However, it is not necessary that a force always makes a stationary body move. For example, if you try to push the wall of your classroom, it will not move. For that matter even if all the students in your class push the wall, it will not move. The reason is that, the force applied by all of you is not sufficient to move the wall.

However, your action of push, i.e., the force has at least tried to move the wall.

2. **A force can stop the moving objects or slow them down**

Imagine your friend is riding a bicycle. If you pull his bicycle from behind, it is going to slow down and may stop. It means, if you apply force in the direction opposite to the direction of the moving body, the body slows down.

Similarly, if we apply brakes to a moving bicycle, it first slows down and then stops. We ourselves have to apply force to stop our bodies while running. A cricket fielder stops a ball by applying force in the direction opposite to that of ball.

From above examples, it is clear that a force may stop a moving body or may reduce the speed of the moving body.

3. **A force can make a moving object move faster**

Imagine a cart puller pulling a cart up a sloping road slowly. If you push the cart from behind, then the cart will start moving fast. Similarly, when a moving vehicle is accelerated by pressing the accelerator, it starts moving faster. When a football player hits the moving football, the ball starts moving faster. When the bulldock pulling a cart applies greater force, the cart moves faster.

4. **A force can change direction of moving objects**

When a cricket player hits the ball with bat, his force changes the speed as well as the direction of the ball. Similarly, a football player changes the direction of the moving ball by angling his foot. You can change the direction of your moving bicycle by applying force on its handle on the right or left hand side.

5. **A force can change the shape of objects**

Imagine a spring is fixed in a wall with a nail. When we pull the spring, it gets stretched and hence its shape changes. Similarly, when we crumple a plane paper, its shape changes. By applying force, you can shape plasticine in any form.

6. **A force cannot cause any effect on the mass of an object**

Mass is an inherent property of a matter. When a force is applied on an object, it does not cause any effect on the mass of the object. Mass of an object is a constant quantity.

From the above examples, we are in position to define force.

**Force** is an external agent which changes or tends to change the state of rest or uniform motion of a body or changes its direction or shape.

Now, we are familiar with various effects of force, which are basically push or pull generated by the force. These forces of pull or push directly act on a body or act through a connector which may be rigid or non-rigid.

The force of push can act through a rigid connector only. However, the force of pull can act through a rigid or a non-rigid connector. For example, we can pull a cart by attaching a rope (non-rigid connector) or rigid handle of metal or wood.

**Contact forces** : All forces which act on a body directly or through a connector, are called **contact forces**.

However, there are other kind of forces which act on a body from a distance, i.e., these forces act through space and do not make direct contact with the body. For example, the Earth pulls a small object towards itself from a distance, due to the force of gravitation. Similarly, a magnet pulls a small steel or iron object from a distance or a charged glass rod attracts tiny bits of paper towards itself.

**Non-contact forces** : The forces which do not make direct contact with a body, but act through space, are called **non-contact forces**. The force of gravitation, the magnetic force and electrical force, are the examples of forces at distance or non-contact forces.

---

## CONSEQUENTIAL FORCES

These forces do not exist on their own. However, they are called into play when a force is applied on a given body.

For example, when a cricket ball is made to roll on the ground by applying force, it stops after covering a certain distance, although, no force is applied to stop it. It is because of the force of friction which comes into play as a result of rolling of cricket ball on the ground. This force of friction is a consequential force, which comes into play only when the ball rolls on the ground and acts in the direction opposite to the direction of the moving ball.

Similarly, when we stretch a spring or a rubber band by applying force, the consequential force of tension is called into play which tends to bring the spring or rubber band to its original shape.

**The consequential force** can be defined as a force which is called into play, when a force is applied on an object.

# FORCES IN NATURE

We come across large number of forces in nature. Some of the very important forces are described below.

## Muscular Forces

All living beings need energy for living. This energy is converted into force for doing various activities. The force is essentially produced by the muscles of living beings. *This force produced by the muscles of living beings is called* **muscular force** or **biological force**.

When we lift, push, pull, stretch or compress, we use our muscular force. For example, lifting of heavy weight, pulling of wheel cart, pushing a lawn roller, stretching a chest expander, compressing a bale of cotton, etc., involves muscular forces.

However, if the force required is too large and our body is incapable of producing it, we use muscular force of animals. For example, bulldogs are used for ploughing fields; horses are used for moving carriages; donkeys and camels are used for carrying heavy loads and elephants are used for moving logs in jungles. Sometimes, we use non-living objects for performing certain jobs. For example, we use axe for cutting wood, knife for cutting fruits, vegetables, meat, etc., and hammer for driving nail in the wall. In performing above activities, we use muscular force.

## Mechanical Forces

*The forces generated by a machine are called* **mechanical forces**.

No machine produces force on its own. In order to produce force, we must supply it with some kind of energy.

The force used to run a motor car engine is produced by burning petrol or CNG. The force used to run electric engine, is produced by electrical energy.

The wind turbines use the energy of wind to produce force which is used for grinding wheat. Sail boats also use the energy of wind to produce force which moves the boat.

The energy of flowing water is used to produce necessary force which runs a generator to produce electric current.

## Gravitational Force

Whenever an object is dropped, it always falls towards the ground. For example, ripe fruits on a tree fall towards ground. A stone on the top a hill rolls downward, if pushed. Why can't these objects rise up? It is because the Earth pulls all objects towards its centre. The pull exerted by the Earth on the objects is called force of gravity or Gravitational force.

Even when we throw an object vertically upward, the force of gravity of Earth at once starts acting on it. Firstly, it slows down the object, then stops it and finally pulls it towards itself.

It is the force of gravity which gives weight to an object. Weight is basically the force of gravity acting on a body.

The force (or pull) by which an object is attracted towards Earth is called weight.

If you hold a stone in your hand, you will feel as if some force is pulling your hand downward. In order to stop your hand moving downward, the muscles of hand apply an equal amount of force in the upward direction. This force just balances the force of gravity due to Earth and does not cause any motion.

# UNIT 2 - FRICTION AND ITS EFFECTS

## FORCE OF FRICTION OR FRICTIONAL FORCE

Roll a rubber ball on the ground. What do you observe after a few seconds? Ride a bicycle. When the bicycle starts moving with some speed, stop pedalling. What do you observe?

In case of rubber ball, it stops after travelling a certain distance. In case of bicycle, it slows down and then stops. In both cases, i.e., rubber ball and bicycle, they first slow down and then stop.

We have already learnt that force can slow down or stop the bodies. Therefore, some force must be acting on the rubber ball or the bicycle which stops them. This force is called force of friction. Friction refers to the resistance to motion that occurs whenever two bodies are in contact with each other. As explained earlier, force of friction is a consequential force. It is produced when an object moves in contact with another object. In above examples, the surface of the rubber ball or the bicycle was in contact with the surface of Earth.

---

## DID YOU KNOW?

Friction is a non-conservative force. So, energy is always lost in overcoming friction. This loss in energy is in terms of heat and sound energy. Generally, 20% of the fuel of an automobile is wasted in overcoming friction.

A force which acts at the surface of contact, when one body moves upon another body, is called **force of friction**.

---

## Types of Friction

There are three types of friction:
(i) Static friction
(ii) Sliding friction
(iii) Rolling friction

(i) Static friction: The frictional force that comes into play when one body tends to move over the surface of another, but the actual motion has yet not started is called static friction.

---

### Effects of Friction

When an object moves in contact with another object, the consequential force of friction can produce following effects.

(a) **Friction opposes motion**

As stated above, the rubber ball stops on account of force of friction. As a matter of fact, the force of friction always acts in the direction opposite to the direction of applied force. It opposes the motion of body and tries to stop the body.

(b) **Friction produces heat**

Take a ten rupees coin and rub its one side with table for 10 seconds. Touch the rubbed side of the coin with your hand. What do you observe? The coin feels very hot. It is because the force of friction between the table top and coin changes into heat energy.

It is for the same reason that our palms get hot when rubbed against each other. Similarly, the cave man produced fire by rubbing dry wood sticks in a dry wooden block.

(c) **Friction causes wear and tear**

Try to compare the soles of your new shoes with old shoes. You will notice that the soles of old shoes are worn out. It is because, when we walk, the material of the soles wears out due to friction. Similarly, the tyres of cycles and automobiles wear out due to friction.

---

### Advantages of Friction

1. We will not be able to walk if there is no friction between the soles of our shoes and the ground. Instead, we will slip. It is the force of friction which helps us to push the ground backward and hence, we move in the forward direction. It is for the same reason that it becomes difficult to walk on highly polished floors or wet ground, as they offer less friction.
2. It is for the same reason that the tyres of different kinds of vehicles are provided with deep grooves, so that they offer large friction with the surface of road, especially in wet conditions, and do not slip.
3. It is for the same reason that knot from silk thread slips easily, but not from the jute thread, because jute offers more friction than silk.
4. It is the force of friction which holds nails and screws in the wooden furniture.
5. It is force of friction which saves us from impact of meteors coming from the space. When a meteor enters the earth's atmosphere, the force of friction between the meteor and air generates enough heat such that it burns before reaching ground.
6. The moving vehicles such as bicycles, cars, scooters, etc., can be slowed down and stopped due to force of friction between the 'brakes shoes' against the moving wheel rims.
7. The writing on a paper with pencil or ball pen is possible on account of friction, because it does not allow the pencil or ball pen to slip on paper.

### Disadvantages of Friction

1. Friction wears out the soles of shoes. It wears out the tyres of all kinds of vehicles. Thus, the soles of shoes or tyres need replacement from time to time.
2. Friction wears out the moving parts of machinery. Thus, the moving parts need replacement from time to time.
3. Friction generates heat. Therefore, it causes wastage of energy.
4. Force of friction increases with weight of objects. Thus, we have to spend lot of energy to move heavy objects.

### Methods of Reducing Friction

1. If you carefully look at a fish or a bird in flight, you will notice that their bodies are narrow in front and then gradually broaden till they again become narrow at the tail.
   Such a shape is called **streamlined shape**. The streamlined shape offers minimum force of friction in air or water and hence helps the fish or birds to move faster.

When an object is shaped in such a way that it experiences minimum friction in air or water, the shape of the object is said to be **streamlined**.

Copying the animals, we have designed aeroplanes, ships, boats, rockets, cars, etc., which have a streamlined shape. This shape experiences least friction in air and water and hence, helps in reducing friction. This in turn helps them to move faster without any increase in the fuel consumption.

2. Polishing a rough surface smoothens irregularities on it. The smooth surface in turn offers very less friction. For example, carrom boards are highly polished so as to reduce friction.

3. By lubricating the moving parts of machinery, the friction can be considerably reduced. If the moving parts are light in weight, then light oil is used as lubricant. However, if the moving parts are heavy, then grease is used. Graphite is used as lubricants in machines which generate more heat. Talc powder is used on carrom boards.

4. Friction can be reduced by providing wheels. For example, a suitcase, if dragged on floor, will offer a large amount of friction. However, if wheels are provided to it, a very small amount of force is required to move it, because force of friction decreases.

5. Ball bearing consists of small steel ball. When these steel balls are placed between two cylindrical moving surfaces, they reduce friction considerably. Ball bearings are commonly used in bicycles, scooters, cars, trucks, electric motors, etc.

**Friction as necessary evil**

Force of friction brings about wear and tear in moving bodies. It produces heat and slows down moving body. A lot of energy is wasted in overcoming friction.

However, if there were no friction, the bodies will either slip or will not move. Furthermore, the moving bodies will not stop. Even walking on the surface of Earth would have been impossible. Thus, we can say that force of friction is a necessary evil.

---

### Methods of Increasing Friction

In certain situations, we need to increase friction, inspite of the fact it consumes more energy. Following examples will justify the statement.

1. Tyres of all kinds of vehicles are provided with deep grooves, so as to increase friction. This prevents the vehicle from skidding on wet roads and sharp turns.
2. The soles of shoes of athletes and mountaineers are provided with spikes and grooves. The spikes increase friction and prevent slipping.

## UNITS OF FORCE

You will need a very small force to lift your physics book, but a large force to lift your school bag. However, we cannot say a small force or a big force or a very big force, etc., for describing the force required to lift an object.

Thus, some unit is required to describe small or big forces.
The unit commonly used for measuring force, is called **kilogram force** (symbol **kgf**).

How big is kilogram force? It is the force required to lift a body of mass 1 kg vertically upward. In other words, the force of gravity acting on one kg mass is one **kgf**.

However, for measuring smaller forces the unit is **gram force** (symbol **gf**).

**One gram force** (1 gf) is the force required to lift a mass of 1 g vertically upward.

As 1 kg mass is equal to 1000 g, therefore 1 kgf = 1000 gf.

In Standard International (SI) system, unit of force is **newton** (symbol **N**).

It has been found that one kilogram force is equal to **10 newtons**.

If we have to lift a mass of 1 kg vertically upward, then the force required in Standard International (SI) system is 10 N.
10 N force is needed for lifting mass = 1 kg
or 10 N force is needed for lifting mass = 1000 g

1 N force is needed for lifting mass = 100 g
Thus, we can define **newton** as under:
The force required to lift 100 g of mass vertically upward is called **one newton**.

## SPRING BALANCE

A spring balance consists of a special spring. It is enclosed in a metal case having a slit cut at its centre along its length. The upper end of the spring is attached to the body of the case. The lower end of the spring is attached to a steel strip at the end of which is fixed a hook. At the junction of steel strip and spring is attached to a small pointer, which passes out of slit. On the side of slit is engraved a scale in gf or kgf depending upon the strength of spring.

The article whose weight is to be found out is attached to the hook. Due to the gravitational pull, the spring gets stretched. The weight of the article is read from the position of pointer on scale.

## MAGNITUDE AND DIRECTION OF FORCE

A force can be large or small. The magnitude tells us how large or how small a force is.

The amount or the strength of force is called its magnitude. The magnitude of force is generally shown by a straight line. It means the greater the length of line, the more is its magnitude. The direction of force is shown by placing an arrow head over the line pointing the direction in which force is acting.

### ADDITION OF FORCES

Suppose a boy trying to pull a heavy cart by applying a force of 200 N is unable to move it.

Now let another boy apply a force of 200 N along with the first boy in the same direction. It will be seen that cart will (i) start moving (ii) cart will move fast.

The reason is that the two forces applied in the same direction, get added and produce a large resultant force (net force). This force will be

200 N + 200 N = 400 N

---

### CANCELLATION AND SUBTRACTION OF FORCES

If two boys pull the above cart in opposite directions with a force of 200 N each as shown, it is observed that cart will not move at all.

It is because the resultant force (net force) becomes zero as the forces, being equal and opposite, cancel each other.

Resultant force = 200 N - 200 N = 0

Imagine a cart being pulled by two boys A and B, such that A can apply a force of 300 N and B can apply a force of 200 N. The net force or the resultant force will be the difference of the forces and will act in the direction of the bigger force.

Thus, resultant force = (300 N - 200 N) = 100 N and will act in the direction in which A is pulling.

From the above examples, following conclusions can be drawn.

(i) When two forces are applied on the same body and at the same point and in the same direction, then the resultant force (total force or net force) is equal to the sum of the forces acting separately. The direction of the resultant force will be the same as of each of the force.

(ii) When two forces are applied at the same point but in opposite directions, then the resultant force (total force or net force) is equal to the difference of the forces acting separately. The direction of resultant force will be the direction in which the larger force acts.

### TERMS RELATED TO MACHINES

- **Effort (E)**: The force applied on a machine to do some mechanical work.
- **Load (L)**: The weight moved or the resistance overcome by a machine in doing some mechanical work.
- **Fulcrum (F)**: A fixed point or an axis around which a machine turns in doing mechanical work.
- **Effort arm**: The perpendicular distance between the effort point and the fulcrum of a machine.
- **Load arm**: The perpendicular distance between the load point and the fulcrum of a machine.
- **Input energy**: The energy supplied to the machine for doing mechanical work.
- **Output energy**: It is useful work done or the energy produced by a machine.
- **Principle of machine**: For an ideal machine, the output energy (output work) is always equal to input energy (input work).
- **Efficiency of machine (η)**: The ratio between the output energy of machine to the input energy is called efficiency of machine.

Efficiency = Output energy / Input energy = Work done by the machine / Work done on the machine
