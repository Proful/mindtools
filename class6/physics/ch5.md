# UNIT 1 - LIGHT AND GENERAL TERMS USED IN UNDERSTANDING LIGHT

## WHAT LIGHT IS

If you enter a dark room, the objects present in the room are not visible. However, if you switch on a bulb, everything in the room becomes visible. Why?

The bulb gives out an invisible energy called light. When the light falls on the objects in the room, it bounces off from the surface of the objects. When this light enters our eyes, the eyes sense it and send a message to the brain. It is finally the brain which really sees. Eyes are only an aid in seeing objects around us.

Why do we say that light is invisible? Well, when light energy falls on the objects, we really do not see it. When light energy bounces off from the surface of objects and enters in our eyes, the sensation produced by this energy, helps our brain to see. We can see objects around us when there is light, while nothing is seen in the dark.

**Light is an invisible energy, which causes the sensation of vision.**

### Speed of Light

Light travels at a very fast speed, i.e., **3 × 10⁸ m/s** in air and vacuum. It means the speed of light is **300,000,000 m/s** or **300,000 km/s**.

## HOW NON-LUMINOUS BODIES CAN BE MADE LUMINOUS

Any non-luminous body can be made luminous by heating it. Take an iron wire and heat it on a gas flame. In about two minutes, the iron wire will get red hot and start emitting light. At this moment, the temperature of iron is between 600°C to 800°C.

### Why the Moon and the planets are considered non-luminous bodies

All of you must have seen Moon at night giving out milky white light. Similarly, you must have seen planet Venus, which appears like a very bright star during early evening. Why do we call them non-luminous bodies when actually light is coming from them?

Well, the simple reason is that they do not produce light of their own. Instead, they reflect the light of the Sun falling on them. Hence, they are non-luminous bodies.

---

## SOURCES OF LIGHT

- Sun is the primary source of light for the planet Earth. It is a huge ball of burning gases which gives out tremendous amounts of heat and light energy.
- Stars are also distant suns, extremely far away from our solar system. To us, they are weak sources of light.
- In addition to it, a glowing electric bulb, a burning candle, a lighted torch, a glowing fluorescent tube, a kerosene oil lamp, a petromax lamp, etc., are sources of light.
- Insects like glowworm and fireflies emit light in darkness. There are certain kinds of fish which emit light. The light received from them is very faint and we cannot see much just by their light. The process by which light is given out by living bodies is called **bioluminescence**.

---

## Luminous Bodies

All bodies which emit light energy by themselves, are called luminous bodies. The examples given above are the examples of luminous bodies. They provide us light.

### Non-luminous Bodies

Bodies such as metals, stones, trees, bushes, furniture, houses, electric poles, etc., do not emit light of their own. However, if the light energy falls on them, they become visible. Such bodies are called non-luminous bodies.

**The bodies which do not emit light energy of their own, but reflect the light energy falling on them, and hence, become visible are called non-luminous bodies.**

## GENERAL DEFINITIONS FOR LIGHT

### Optical Medium
Anything (material or non-material), through which light energy can pass partially or wholly is called an **optical medium**.

### Homogeneous Medium
An optical medium which has a uniform composition throughout, is called **homogeneous medium**.

### Heterogeneous Medium
An optical medium which has different compositions at different points, is called **heterogeneous medium**.

### Transparent Medium
A medium which allows most of the light energy to pass through it, is called **transparent medium**.

**Examples:** glass, diamond, thin layer of water, air, vacuum, certain kinds of clear plastics, alcohol, benzene, etc.

### Translucent Medium
A medium which partially allows the light energy to pass through it, is called **translucent medium**.

**Examples:** ground glass, muddy water, smoke, dust filled air, fog, mist, clouds, oiled paper, butter paper, tissue paper, etc.

### Opaque Body
Those bodies which do not allow the light energy to pass through them, are called **opaque bodies**.

**Examples:** bricks, stones, wood and articles made from it, wool, cotton, articles made from metals and coal. We cannot see through opaque bodies because they do not allow the light energy to pass through them.

### Point Source of Light
A source of light which is of the size of pinhead of a common pin, is called a **point source of light**.

### Extended Source of Light
Any source of light, which is bigger than the point source of light is called an **extended source of light**. A burning candle, a glowing electric bulb or fluorescent tube, etc., are extended sources of light.

### Ray of Light
The path along which the light energy travels in a given direction, is called a **ray of light**. It is represented by a straight line. The arrow head shows the direction in which light energy travels.

### Beam of Light
A collection of a large number of rays of light is called **beam of light**.

### Parallel Beam of Light
When a large number of rays travel parallel to each other, then collection of such rays is called a **parallel beam of light**.

**Examples:** The rays of sun entering a room through an open window constitute a parallel beam of light. Similarly, the rays coming out of a search light or spotlight constitute a parallel beam of light.

### Divergent Beam of Light
When the rays of light starting from a point travel in various directions, then the collection of such rays is called a **divergent beam of light**.

**Examples:** The rays coming out from a bulb or a burning candle or a car headlight constitute a divergent beam of light.

### Convergent Beam of Light
When the rays of light coming from different directions meet at a point, then such a collection of rays is called a **convergent beam of light**.

**Example:** If a parallel beam is made to pass through a convex lens, then it meets at a point. This kind of collection of rays is called convergent beam of light.

## KEY IDEAS

- Light does not require any medium for its propagation. It can travel through vacuum also. Light reaches the Earth from the Sun after travelling through vacuum and then through air.
- LED bulbs are considered as an energy saving option for lighting purposes. They do not have a filament that gets heated up to produce light. Electricity is directly converted to light in them and thus, there is less wastage of energy as heat. Unlike CFL, LED bulbs do not contain mercury vapour and so they are eco-friendly too.

# UNIT 2 - RECTILINEAR PROPAGATION OF LIGHT, PINHOLE CAMERA AND SHADOWS

## INVESTIGATION 5.1: To prove that light travels in straight lines

**Materials Required:**
- three stiff cardboards
- three wooden blocks
- a sharp pin
- geometry box
- adhesive tape
- candle

**Method:**
Place the cardboards one upon another. On the uppermost cardboard, draw diagonals. Make a hole with the help of a sharp pin at the point of intersection of diagonals through all the cardboards. Fix the cardboards along small wooden blocks with the help of adhesive tape. Arrange the cardboard A, B and C in straight line along the edge of table. Towards the cardboard A, place a lighted candle such that flame of candle is at the same height as the hole in cardboard. Look from the side of cardboard C. You will notice the candle flame is visible. Now, displace the cardboard B sideways. Again look from the side of cardboard C. You will notice that candle flame is not visible.

**Why is candle flame not visible?** It is because the light travels in straight lines. Thus, the light passing through the hole of cardboard A cannot bend to pass through the holes of B and C.

## APPLICATIONS OF RECTILINEAR PROPAGATION OF LIGHT

There are a number of everyday phenomena which are based on rectilinear propagation of light:

1. Formation of day and night suggests that light travels in straight lines.
2. Formation of shadows suggests that light travels in straight lines.
3. When a beam of sunlight enters a dark room through a ventilator or window, we can see the light travelling in a straight line.
4. When the head light of a car is switched on, the light rays appear to travel in straight lines.
5. If we almost close our eyes and look at the burning candle flame, it appears as if it is giving out a few beams of light, which are travelling in straight lines.
6. Beams of search light used in a lighthouse or airport suggest that light travels in straight lines.
7. Beams of light coming from the projection room in the cinema hall suggest that light travels in straight lines.
8. The light coming from small laser torches used as pointers suggest that light travels in straight lines.

**The property of light travelling in a straight line is called the rectilinear propagation of light.**

## PINHOLE CAMERA

It is the earliest camera to be made. It is a very simple device, which at one time was used to take photographs of stationary objects. It is a direct consequence of the rectilinear propagation of light.

### Principle of Pinhole Camera
It is based on the principle that light travels in straight lines.

### Construction of Pinhole Camera
It consists of a rectangular cardboard box, such that its one side is made of ground glass screen. The side opposite to ground glass has a hole in the middle, whose size is equal to the pinhead of a common pin. The box is blackened from inside so as to absorb any light which falls on its walls directly or indirectly.

### Working of Pinhole Camera
Consider a lighted candle AB, in front of a pinhole camera. The rays starting from different points of candle, travel in all directions. A ray of light starting from point A, along AH, after passing through the pin hole falls on ground glass screen at point A₁. Similarly, another ray starting from point B, along BH, after passing through the pinhole will fall on ground glass screen at point B₁. Thus, all the rays starting in between the points A and B, after passing through pinhole will meet the screen in between points A₁ and B₁. Thus, A₁B₁ is the image of object AB.

### Characteristics of Image Formed in a Pinhole Camera
(i) It is **real**, i.e., it is formed on the screen.  
(ii) It is **inverted**.  
(iii) It is generally **smaller** than the size of object.

### Key Ideas about Pinhole Camera
- The image formed in the pinhole camera is **temporary**.
- The image does not show the **details** of the object.
- A pinhole camera cannot be used to see the **motion** of a moving object.

### Factors Affecting the Size of Image
If the distance between pinhole and screen is reduced, the size of image will decrease. However, the image will become bright as the light spreads over a small area. If the distance between pinhole camera and screen is increased, the size of image will increase. However, the image gets less bright as light spreads over a large area.

**Thus,**
```
Size of image = Distance of screen from pinhole / Distance of object from pinhole
```

### Photography with Pinhole Camera
In order to take an actual photograph, the pinhole camera is taken in a dark room and covered with thick black cloth. The ground glass screen is replaced by a photographic plate. The camera is then faced towards the object to be photographed. The black cloth is removed only from the side of pinhole. When the light enters through pinhole and strikes plate, the plate gets exposed. The plate is then developed and printed in a photo studio.

## SHADOWS AND THEIR KINDS

### Shadow
A dark patch formed behind an opaque body, when it is placed in the path of light is called **shadow**. It is a region of absence of light.

### A Shadow Consists of Two Regions
**Umbra:** A region of total darkness. No ray of light enters in this region.

**Penumbra:** A region of partial darkness which surrounds the umbra. Some rays of light always reach this region and partially illuminate it.

### Formation of Shadow when Source of Light is a Point
In this case:
- The region between points A and B on the screen does not receive any ray of light and hence is **umbra** (region of total darkness).
- There is **no penumbra** formed.
- If the screen is moved away from the opaque body, the size of umbra increases.
- If the screen is moved towards the opaque body, the size of umbra decreases.

### Formation of Shadow when Extended Source of Light is Smaller than the Opaque Body
In this case:
- Region BC does not receive any ray of light and is **umbra**.
- Region AB receives light from one point and region CD receives light from another point, so they are **penumbra** regions.
- Size of umbra is **larger** than penumbra.
- When the screen is moved away from the opaque body, the size of umbra and penumbra increases. Conversely, if the screen is moved towards the opaque body, the size decreases.

### Formation of Shadow when Extended Source of Light is Bigger than Opaque Body
In this case:
- Region BC does not receive any ray of light and is **umbra**.
- Region AB and CD are **penumbra** regions.
- Size of umbra is **smaller** than penumbra.
- If the screen is moved backward, the umbra becomes very small and can finally disappear. The penumbra gets larger and more lighted up. Finally, penumbra becomes so faint that it is hardly visible. In such a situation, **no shadow is formed**.

### Why Birds Flying High Do Not Cast Shadows
This is a case where the extended source of light (Sun) is very large as compared to the opaque body (bird). The bird is too small as compared to the diameter of sun. The umbra formed by the bird is so small that it finishes in the air. The penumbra formed is very large and very faint. Thus, it is hardly visible. Hence, on the whole no shadow is formed on the Earth. Similarly, aeroplanes flying high in the sky do not cast their shadows on the Earth.

### Notes on Shadows
Shadow gives us information about the **shape** of the object, but no information about the **size** or **details** of the object.

## ECLIPSE

### What is an Eclipse?
The shadow of one heavenly body falling upon another heavenly body is called an **eclipse**.

The Earth revolves around the Sun and the Moon revolves around the Earth. However, the plane in which the Earth revolves around the Sun is not the same as that of Moon around the Earth. During their course of revolution, sometimes the Earth, Moon and the Sun happen to be in the same line. In such a situation, either the shadow of the Earth falls on the Moon for some time or the shadow of the Moon falls on the Earth for some time.

### Types of Eclipse
**Lunar Eclipse:** When the shadow of the Earth falls on the Moon, the Moon is not visible for some time. It is always caused on **Full Moon night**.

**Solar Eclipse:** When the shadow of the Moon falls on the Earth, the Sun is not visible for some time.

### Lunar Eclipse
Lunar eclipse is always caused on Full Moon night. During their course of revolution, the Sun, the Earth and the Moon happen to be in the same straight line such that the Earth is in between the Sun and the Moon. Then shadow of the Earth falls on the Moon. Thus, for some time, the light of the Sun does not fall on the Moon. As the Moon has no light of its own, it is not visible.

### Important Facts about Lunar Eclipse
- The lunar eclipse is generally **total**, i.e., the Moon completely disappears in the shadow of the Earth.
- It lasts for **two or more hours** because the Moon has to pass through a very large shadow of the Earth.
- Lunar eclipse is a **common phenomenon**.

### Solar Eclipse
- Total solar eclipse hardly lasts for **five to ten minutes** and is a **rare event**.
- It is because the shadow of the Moon is very small.
- Solar eclipse is generally **partial**.
- It is an uncommon phenomenon because many a time the shadow of the Moon does not reach the Earth.

### Precautions for Viewing Solar Eclipse
Solar eclipse should **never be viewed directly**. It is because during solar eclipse the Earth receives a very large amount of ultraviolet radiations. These radiations can burn the retina of the eye and hence, one can lose his eyesight. In order to watch the solar eclipse, a plane glass should be held over a candle flame, such that a thick layer of soot is formed on both its sides. The Sun should be observed through this glass. It cuts off most of the harmful radiation. **Under no circumstance, solar eclipse should be watched with dark glasses**, as they offer no protection from the harmful solar radiations.

### Key Ideas about Eclipses
- In fact, all planets and satellites cast their shadows in space.
- The shadow cast by the heavenly bodies on each other is called an eclipse.

Birds flying high do not cast shadows because:
- The bird is too small compared to the diameter of the Sun (extended source)
- The umbra formed by the bird is so small that it finishes in the air before reaching the ground
- The penumbra formed is very large and very faint, making it hardly visible on Earth

Therefore, no distinct shadow is formed on the ground.

**8. (a) What is a solar eclipse? Support your answer by a neat diagram.**

A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon comes between the Sun and the Earth, and the shadow of the Moon falls on the Earth. The Sun is not visible for some time from certain parts of the Earth. It occurs on a **New Moon day**.

**(b) Why does a solar eclipse last for a very small time?**

A solar eclipse lasts for a very small time (5-10 minutes) because the shadow of the Moon is very small. The Earth, while revolving around the Sun, soon passes out of the shadow region caused by the Moon.

**(c) Why is a total solar eclipse not very frequent?**

A total solar eclipse is not very frequent because:
- The Moon's shadow is very small
- Many times the Moon's shadow does not reach the Earth
- The alignment of Sun, Moon, and Earth must be perfect, which doesn't happen often

**9. (a) What is a lunar eclipse? Support your answer by a neat diagram.**

A lunar eclipse occurs when the Earth comes between the Sun and the Moon, and the shadow of the Earth falls on the Moon. The Moon is not visible for some time. It occurs on a **Full Moon night**.

**(b) Why does a lunar eclipse last longer than a solar eclipse?**

A lunar eclipse lasts longer (2 or more hours) because the Earth is a fairly large body and its shadow can completely cover the whole disc of the Moon. The Moon has to pass through a very large shadow of the Earth, taking more time.

**(c) Why is a lunar eclipse more frequent compared to a solar eclipse?**

A lunar eclipse is more frequent because the Earth's shadow is large enough to cover the Moon completely. The Earth is much larger than the Moon, so its shadow is much bigger, making lunar eclipses more common and often total.

**10. Why should a solar eclipse not be seen with naked eyes? How can a solar eclipse be seen? Explain.**

Solar eclipse should never be viewed directly because during solar eclipse the Earth receives a very large amount of ultraviolet radiations. These radiations can burn the retina of the eye, and one can lose eyesight.

**Safe way to view:** Hold a plane glass over a candle flame so that a thick layer of soot is formed on both sides. Observe the Sun through this glass. It cuts off most harmful radiation. **Never** use dark glasses as they offer no protection from harmful solar radiations.

## G. Answer the following questions:

**1. (a) What is the principle of a pinhole camera?**

The principle of a pinhole camera is based on the rectilinear propagation of light - that light travels in straight lines.

**(b) State three characteristics of the image formed by a pinhole camera.**

(i) It is **real** (formed on the screen)  
(ii) It is **inverted**  
(iii) It is generally **smaller** than the size of the object

**2. (a) How can you prove that light travels in straight lines? Explain it with an investigation.**

Take three stiff cardboards with holes at their centres. Arrange them in a straight line on a table. Place a lighted candle behind the first cardboard. Look through the holes from the other end - the candle flame will be visible. Now displace the middle cardboard sideways. The candle flame will no longer be visible. This proves that light travels in straight lines as it cannot bend to pass through the displaced holes.

**(b) Write any three applications of rectilinear propagation of light which are related to everyday phenomena.**

(i) Formation of day and night  
(ii) Formation of shadows  
(iii) Sunlight entering a dark room through a window shows light travelling in straight lines

**3. (a) What do you mean by the term shadow?**

A shadow is a dark patch formed behind an opaque body when it is placed in the path of light. It is a region of absence of light.

**(b) Describe formation of shadow when extended source of light is smaller than an opaque body.**

When the extended source is smaller than the opaque body, rays from different points of the source create both umbra and penumbra regions. The umbra is the central region where no light reaches (total darkness). The penumbra regions on the sides are partially illuminated where some rays reach. The umbra is larger than the penumbra. If the screen is moved away, both umbra and penumbra increase in size.

**4. (a) When does lunar eclipse take place?**

Lunar eclipse takes place on a **Full Moon night**, when the Sun, Earth and Moon are in a straight line with the Earth in between. The shadow of the Earth falls on the Moon.

**(b) Why is a lunar eclipse a more frequent phenomenon than a solar eclipse?**

Lunar eclipse is more frequent because the Earth is much larger than the Moon, so its shadow is much bigger and can completely cover the Moon's disc. Also, the Earth's shadow is large enough that the Moon can pass through it more often, making lunar eclipses a common phenomenon.
