## UNIT 1 – SIMPLE MACHINES AND THEIR IMPORTANCE

### WHY WE NEED A MACHINE

Machines play a very important role in our life. We can do a large number of tasks with our own hands. However, our hands are not sufficient to do all kinds of tasks. Thus, we use some devices or tools to make our work easier and complete it in a lesser time. By applying less force, we can do more work with the help of machines. Machines can change the direction of the force. They enable us to apply force in a convenient direction. For example, we cannot cut a tree with our bare hands. We need an axe. In this case, the axe is a kind of machine.

The following examples will illustrate the purpose for which machines are employed.

Suppose we want to lift the lid of an oil can. It will be very difficult to remove the lid with our bare hands. However, if we insert a spoon as shown in Fig. 4.1 and apply effort in the downward direction on the handle of the spoon, the lid comes off easily. In this case, the spoon is a kind of machine which has helped us to multiply our effort to remove the lid.

While drawing water from a well, we apply effort towards ourselves on the rope which is passing over a pulley. The direction of the effort applied is changed by the pulley, and hence, the bucket of water moves upward. Thus, the pulley is a machine which helps us to change the direction of the effort applied.

In case of a typewriter, we press the knobs of the machine through a small distance. However, the arms, having typefaces, move through a larger distance and strike against the paper. In this case, the effort is applied through a small distance, but the load moves through a larger distance.

If you are asked to lift a burning charcoal, what will you do? Certainly, you will use a firetog to lift the charcoal. In this case, the firetog is a machine, which helps us in doing unsafe and dangerous work.

### TERMS RELATED TO MACHINES

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Effort (E)** | The force applied on a machine to do some mechanical work. |
| **Load (L)** | The weight moved or the resistance overcome by a machine in doing some mechanical work. |
| **Fulcrum (F)** | A fixed point or an axis around which a machine turns in doing mechanical work. |
| **Effort arm** | The perpendicular distance between the effort point and the fulcrum of a machine. |
| **Load arm** | The perpendicular distance between the load point and the fulcrum of a machine. |
| **Input energy** | The energy supplied to the machine for doing mechanical work. |
| **Output energy** | It is useful work done or the energy produced by a machine. |
| **Principle of machine** | For an ideal machine, the output energy (output work) is always equal to input energy (input work). |
| **Efficiency of machine (η)** | The ratio between the output energy of machine to the input energy is called efficiency of machine. |

\[
\text{Efficiency (η)} = \frac{\text{Output energy}}{\text{Input energy}} = \frac{\text{Work done by the machine}}{\text{Work done on the machine}}
\]

---

### KEY IDEAS

A machine is said to be ideal, if its movable parts are weightless as well as frictionless.

Such a machine is not possible. In an actual machine, some input energy is always utilised (wasted) in moving the parts of the machine and overcoming the friction. Thus, the output energy of the actual machine is less than the input energy. In simple terms, in an actual machine the output energy is always less than the input energy.

---

### POINTS TO REMEMBER

Mechanical advantage as well as efficiency of machine has no units. It is a pure number.

The reason for this is that both are ratio which have same units. These units cancel with one another.

In terms of percentage value:

\[
\text{Percentage efficiency } (\eta) = \frac{\text{Output energy } \times 100}{\text{Input energy}}
\]

For the percentage efficiency, it implies that if a machine is 90% efficient, then for every 100 units of energy supplied to the machine, the machine converts only 90 units into useful work. The 10 units are wasted (utilised) in overcoming the friction and moving the parts of machine.

- **Mechanical Advantage (M.A.)**: The ratio between the useful load (resistance overcome) moved by the machine and the effort applied to do so is called mechanical advantage. If 'L' is the useful load moved by an effort 'E', then

\[
\text{Mechanical Advantage } (\text{M.A.}) = \frac{L}{E}
\]

- **Ideal Machine**: A machine in which output is equal to input, is called ideal machine.

Such machine is not possible, because in order to have such a machine, its movable parts should be weightless and frictionless.

### LEVER

Levers are simple machines. We use them in our daily life.

A lever is a rigid bar which can rotate about a fixed point or a fixed line, called the axis of rotation. The point or the axis of rotation is called the fulcrum.

An effort \( E \) applied at point \( A \) of the lever overcomes a load \( L \) at another point \( B \), as shown in Figs. 4.5 (a), (b) and (c). \( F \) is the fulcrum. The distance of the fulcrum from the point at which effort is applied, is called the effort arm. The distance of the fulcrum from the point at which the load acts, is called the load arm.

### NOTES

The shears of scissors are longer while that of pliers are shorter. Thus, the load arm of scissors is longer than the effort arm, so, its mechanical advantage is less than 1, while the load arm of pliers is shorter than effort arm, so its mechanical advantage is more than 1.

---

### PRINCIPLE OF LEVER

It states that the product of an effort with effort arm is equal to the product of load with load arm. Therefore, we get

\[
\text{Load} \times \text{Load arm} = \text{Effort} \times \text{Effort arm}
\]

\[
L \times BF = E \times AF
\]
\[
\Rightarrow \frac{L}{E} = \frac{AF}{BF}
\]

Thus, mechanical advantage of lever =

\[
\frac{L}{E} = \frac{\text{Effort arm}}{\text{Load arm}}
\]

Hence, the mechanical advantage of a lever is equal to the ratio of the effort arm to the load arm, which is known as the principle of a lever.

---

### LEVERS OF THE FIRST ORDER

In this type of levers, the fulcrum is situated between the load and the effort. The effort arm may be equal or less than or more than load arm. In case the effort arm is more than load arm, a small effort is required to move load. However, if effort arm is less than load arm, a large effort is required to move load. Thus, mechanical advantage can be more than 1, equal to 1 or less than 1.

### LEVERS OF THE SECOND ORDER

In this type of levers, the load is situated between the effort and the fulcrum.

In the levers of second order, the load arm is always smaller than the effort arm. Thus, the mechanical advantage is always more than 1 as effort arm is longer than load arm. Such a lever is also called effort multiplier or force multiplier lever.

Oars of a boat, wheel barrow, nut-cracker, etc., are levers of the second order or second class (Class II), as illustrated in Figs. 4.7 (a) to 4.7 (c).

### LEVERS OF THE THIRD ORDER

In this type of levers, the effort is situated between the load and the fulcrum.

In the levers of third order, effort arm is always smaller than the load arm. Thus, more effort is required to lift a lighter load. This lever is also called speed multiplier lever as load moves through larger distance as compared to the effort.

Sugar tongs, firetong, bread knife, etc., are levers of the third order or third class (Class III), as illustrated in Figs. 4.8 (a) to 4.8 (c).

### PULLEY

Pulley is a flat circular disc having a groove in its edge and capable of rotating around a fixed point passing through its central axis commonly called axle. In a way, axle is a fulcrum around which pulley rotates.

It is generally made of some metal. It is fixed on an axle. The groove is used to hold a rope as it moves over it. A pulley helps to apply the effort in a convenient direction. It is used to lift heavy objects, or move a heavy object. Even wooden pulleys are used in villages.

### Single Fixed Pulley System

It consists of a single pulley. It is attached to some high platform and around its groove passes a rope. One end of the rope is attached to the load. Effort is applied at the other end of rope [Fig. 4.9 (b)]. This pulley is commonly used in drawing water from wells.

### Single Movable Pulley System

A combination of two pulleys [Fig. 4.9 (c)] is called a single movable pulley system. In this kind of pulley system, the load of say 100 kgf can be raised by an effort of 50 kgf. You might have seen a crane lifting heavy loads. The crane is a practical application of levers and pulleys working in unison.

### Mechanical advantage of the single fixed pulley

The effort applied is equal to the load to be lifted in an ideal pulley, i.e., If, Effort = Load; Mechanical advantage = Load/Effort = 1.

Due to friction, the mechanical advantage is less than 1, i.e. the effort is more than the load in an actual pulley.

### INCLINED PLANE

Try to jump over a small wall. Notice that before jumping, you move a short distance backward and then run towards the wall before the actual jump. In doing so, you apply a large amount of effort.

However, the same result can be achieved by placing a wooden plank along the wall as shown in Fig. 4.10. You can easily walk over the plank and climb the wall and jump. The wooden plank acts as a simple machine, as it helps you to do the same work by applying less effort. Such a machine is called the inclined plane.

Thus, the effort required to push a load up an inclined plane is less than lifting the same load directly. So, the mechanical advantage of an inclined plane is greater than 1.

Any sloping flat surface along which a load can be easily pushed or pulled is called an **inclined plane**.

### Examples of Inclined Plane

The houses which are built at higher plinth are provided with ramps to push up the scooters and motorbikes [Fig. 4.11]. When the cargo is removed from the sailing ships, a gang plank is provided to unload the cargo [Fig. 4.12]. When drums or gas cylinders are loaded into or unloaded from the truck, they are pushed up or down the wooden inclined plane commonly called chute [Fig. 4.13].

### NOTES

Workers use wooden planks as inclined planes in the construction of buildings to carry the construction material because lesser effort is required to carry the heavy load up the building. The staircase used by the airline passengers is a modified form of inclined plane.

A double inclined plane is called wedge. Fig. 4.16(a) shows two inclined planes ABD and ACD joined at AD so as to form a wedge.

A wedge is a simple machine, which helps us to tear apart solid objects. By using a wedge, you can easily tear apart a piece of wood, which otherwise is not possible.

For pushing apart solid objects, the sharp end of the wedge is placed on it. It is then hammered from the wider end with a hammer. As the sharp end penetrates the wood or any other solid object, it tears apart the object in lateral direction.

Thus, thinner the wedge, easier it is to drive the wedge into the solid object (log) and split it into pieces. This is because the thin (sharp) edge reduces the surface area and thus, small force results in more pressure on the surface.

Axe, ploughs, knife, chisel, nails, saw, needle, etc., are various modifications of wedge.

### SCREW

A spiral (winding) inclined plane is called a screw. It appears like a nail with grooves on its circular surface. By cutting the spiral grooves on the surface of a metal rod, a screw is made. The spiral grooves so formed are known as the threads of the screw. A well driven screw forms a better grip and can support considerably larger loads.

Fig. 4.17 to Fig. 4.20 show the various forms of screw. A screw basically converts the circular motion into linear motion.

### Uses of Screws

1. They are used to fasten two pieces of wood or metal. As screw is a winding inclined plane, therefore it cannot be pulled out easily from the fastened pieces, as is in case of a nail.

2. A cork screw is used for pulling out cork from the bottles of ketchup or cold drink.

### WHEEL AND AXLE

**Fig. 4.21(a):** Wheel and axle - Wheel, Axle

**Fig. 4.21(b):** Each time you turn the knob of your water tap or the door knob of your house you are using a simple machine, i.e., wheel and axle.

Wheel and axle essentially consist of two cylinders of different diameters joined together, such that if one is made to rotate the other also rotates.

The cylinder with bigger diameter is called wheel, whereas the one with smaller diameter is called axle.

If the wheel rotates, the axle also rotates with it. In case of two wheels, the effort is applied on the bigger wheel (known as wheel) and load is attached with the smaller wheel (known as axle). The wheel and axle is used to provide linear motion by the rotation of wheel in vehicle as it reduces the friction of moving vehicle with ground. It is because rolling friction is less than the sliding friction.

Other examples of wheel and axle are steering wheel of a car; a screw driver; a drill used by carpenters, etc.

Sometimes a complete wheel is not provided. Instead, a handle is attached to the axle, which describes an imaginary circle. Figs. 4.22 (a) and (b) show the well and a grinder in which a handle attached to the axle describes imaginary circle.

### MAINTENANCE AND CARE OF MACHINES

If a machine is to be used for a long time, it needs care and maintenance. The following points should be remembered about the maintenance of machines.

1. Some parts in a machine rub against each other due to which these parts wear out and become loose. They also produce noise. A large amount of energy is lost in the form of friction. To avoid these problems, the moving parts of a machine should be regularly lubricated.

2. The non-movable parts of a machine are generally made of iron, which easily gets rusted in moist air. To avoid rusting, such parts should be painted.

3. The machines should be protected from dust which increases the wear and tear of the machines. When any machine is not in use, it should be kept covered.

A machine which is well lubricated and protected from rusting and dust, lasts longer and gives better service.

### NUMERICAL BASED ON MECHANICAL ADVANTAGE OR LEVERAGE

**1.** An effort of 8 kgf is applied on a machine for movement of a load of 100 kgf. Calculate the mechanical advantage of the machine.

**Solution:**
\[
\text{Mechanical advantage} = \frac{\text{Load}}{\text{Effort}} = \frac{100 \text{ kgf}}{8 \text{ kgf}} = 12.5
\]

**2.** A machine displaces a load of 125 kgf when an effort of 12.5 kgf acts on it. Calculate its mechanical advantage.

**Solution:**
\[
\text{Mechanical advantage} = \frac{\text{Load}}{\text{Effort}} = \frac{125 \text{ kgf}}{12.5 \text{ kgf}} = 10
\]
