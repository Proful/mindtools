### Chapter 1: Characteristics of Living Organisms


---

# What is Life?

* **Life** is the condition that distinguishes living organisms from non-living objects. Living things are capable of carrying out several biological processes such as obtaining food, growing, breathing, reproducing, responding to their surroundings, and removing wastes.

* Everything around us can be divided into **living things (biotic components)** and **non-living things (abiotic components)**. Animals, plants, birds, insects, fungi, and bacteria are living organisms, whereas rocks, air, water, soil, sunlight, and buildings are non-living.

* Although some non-living objects may appear to move (such as cars or robots), they do not possess life because they cannot perform biological processes independently.

---

# Characteristics of Living Organisms

Scientists identify living organisms based on certain common characteristics. Every living organism performs most or all of these life processes.

## 1. Living Organisms Need Food (Nutrition)

* Every living organism requires food because food is the ultimate source of energy needed to carry out life processes.

* Food provides:

  * Energy for movement and daily activities.
  * Raw materials for growth.
  * Materials for repairing damaged tissues.
  * Protection against diseases.
  * Nutrients for reproduction.

* Different organisms obtain food in different ways.

  * Green plants prepare their own food by **photosynthesis** and are called **producers** or **autotrophs**.
  * Animals cannot prepare their own food and depend on plants or other animals. They are called **consumers** or **heterotrophs**.
  * Fungi obtain food from dead and decaying matter and are called **decomposers**.

### Examples

* A cow eats grass.
* A lion eats deer.
* A sunflower prepares its own food.
* Mushrooms feed on dead leaves.

### Olympiad Fact

* Although plants prepare food by photosynthesis, they also need minerals and water from the soil to grow properly.

---

## 2. Living Organisms Respire

* **Respiration** is the process by which living organisms release energy from the food they eat.

* During respiration:

  * Food is broken down.
  * Energy is released.
  * Carbon dioxide and water are usually produced.

* Respiration occurs continuously throughout life.

* It is different from breathing.

  * **Breathing** is the physical process of taking in oxygen and giving out carbon dioxide.
  * **Respiration** is the chemical process occurring inside cells.

### Examples

* Humans breathe through lungs.
* Fish use gills.
* Earthworms breathe through moist skin.
* Plants exchange gases through tiny pores called **stomata**.

### Remember

**Breathing is only one part of respiration.**

---

## 3. Living Organisms Grow

* Growth means a permanent increase in the size and mass of an organism.

* Growth occurs because cells divide and increase in number.

* Growth is one of the easiest characteristics to observe.

### Plant Growth

Plants continue growing throughout most of their life.

Examples

* Tree trunks become thicker every year.
* Roots grow deeper.
* New branches develop.
* Flowers and fruits appear.

### Animal Growth

Animals grow only up to a certain age.

Examples

* A baby becomes an adult.
* A puppy becomes a dog.
* A chick becomes a hen.

### Olympiad Fact

Plants usually show **unlimited growth**, whereas most animals show **limited growth**.

---

## 4. Living Organisms Move

* Movement is another important characteristic of life.

* Animals move from one place to another in search of food, shelter, or mates.

Examples

* Birds fly.
* Fish swim.
* Snakes crawl.
* Kangaroos hop.
* Humans walk and run.

### Do Plants Move?

Many students think plants do not move.

Actually, plants also show movement.

Examples

* Sunflower turns towards sunlight.
* Touch-me-not plant closes its leaves when touched.
* Roots grow downward.
* Shoots grow upward.
* Creepers climb on supports.

Plant movement is usually slow and caused by growth or response to stimuli.

---

## 5. Living Organisms Respond to Stimuli (Sensitivity)

* A **stimulus** is any change in the surroundings.

* A **response** is the reaction shown by a living organism.

Living organisms constantly sense changes around them and respond appropriately.

### Common Stimuli

* Light
* Heat
* Cold
* Touch
* Sound
* Smell
* Water
* Chemicals

### Examples

* You blink when bright light enters your eyes.
* You quickly remove your hand after touching a hot pan.
* A deer runs away after hearing a loud sound.
* Sunflowers bend towards sunlight.
* Touch-me-not folds its leaves when touched.

### Olympiad Tip

The ability to respond to stimuli helps organisms survive by avoiding danger and finding food.

---

## 6. Living Organisms Reproduce

* Reproduction is the process by which living organisms produce young ones of their own kind.

* It ensures the continuation of a species.

Without reproduction, all species would eventually become extinct.

### Examples

* Cats give birth to kittens.
* Mango trees produce seeds.
* Birds lay eggs.
* Butterflies lay eggs on leaves.

### Types (Basic Level)

**Animals**

* Egg-laying animals

  * Hen
  * Duck
  * Crocodile

* Animals giving birth

  * Human
  * Dog
  * Cow

**Plants**

* Seeds
* Spores (basic introduction)
* Vegetative propagation (advanced introduction)

### Remember

Reproduction is essential for the survival of a species, **not for the survival of an individual organism**.

---

## 7. Living Organisms Excrete Waste

* Every living organism produces waste materials while carrying out life processes.

* If these wastes remain inside the body, they become harmful.

* Therefore, organisms remove wastes through **excretion**.

### Human Wastes

* Carbon dioxide
* Sweat
* Urine

### Plant Wastes

Plants remove wastes by:

* Releasing oxygen.
* Losing excess water through transpiration.
* Storing wastes in leaves or bark, which later fall off.

---

## 8. Living Organisms Have a Definite Life Span

Every organism is born, grows, reproduces and eventually dies.

Examples

| Organism    |  Approximate Lifespan |
| ----------- | --------------------: |
| Butterfly   |             Few weeks |
| Dog         |           10–15 years |
| Human       | 70–80 years (average) |
| Banyan Tree | Several hundred years |

Death is a natural stage in the life cycle.

---

## 9. Living Organisms are Made of Cells

* Every living organism is composed of one or more **cells**.

* Cells are the smallest units capable of performing all life processes.

Examples

* Amoeba consists of one cell.
* Humans contain trillions of cells.
* Trees contain billions of cells.

This is why the cell is called the **basic structural and functional unit of life**.

---

## 10. Living Organisms Adapt to Their Environment

* **Adaptation** is a feature or behaviour that helps an organism survive in its habitat.

* Adaptations develop over many generations.

### Examples

* Polar bears have thick fur for cold regions.
* Camels store fat in their humps and can survive long periods without water.
* Fish have streamlined bodies to swim efficiently.
* Cacti have spines instead of leaves to reduce water loss.

Adaptations increase the chances of survival and reproduction.

# Are Viruses Living or Non-living?

This is a favourite Olympiad question.

* Viruses show characteristics of both living and non-living things.
* Outside a living cell, they behave like non-living particles.
* Inside a living cell, they reproduce like living organisms.
* Therefore, viruses are considered to be at the boundary between living and non-living.

**Examples**

* Influenza virus
* Coronavirus
* Dengue virus

# Chapter 2: Classification of Living Organisms

Classification is one of the most important topics in Biology because millions of different organisms live on Earth. Scientists classify them into groups so that they can be studied, identified, and compared more easily.

---

# What is Classification?

* **Classification** is the scientific process of arranging living organisms into different groups based on their similarities and differences.

* Imagine a library containing thousands of books placed randomly. Finding one book would be extremely difficult. However, if the books are arranged according to subject, author, or language, finding a particular book becomes easy.

* In the same way, scientists classify living organisms into groups so that each organism can be easily identified and studied.

* Classification also helps scientists understand how different organisms are related to one another.

---

# Why is Classification Necessary?

There are millions of different kinds of living organisms on Earth. Without classification, studying them would be almost impossible.

### Advantages of Classification

* It makes the study of Biology simple and systematic.
* It helps scientists identify newly discovered organisms.
* It allows scientists around the world to use the same scientific system.
* It helps us understand the similarities and differences between organisms.
* It shows the evolutionary relationships between different species.
* It reduces confusion caused by different local names.

### Example

The animal known as a **buffalo** has different local names in different languages. However, scientists everywhere recognize it by its scientific classification.

---

# Basis of Classification

Scientists compare many characteristics before placing organisms into groups.

Some important characteristics include:

* Body structure
* Presence or absence of backbone
* Number of cells
* Method of obtaining food
* Method of reproduction
* Habitat
* Presence of flowers or seeds (in plants)
* Body covering
* Method of movement

No single characteristic is enough. Scientists study many features together.

---

# Kingdom Plantae (Plants)

Plants form one of the largest kingdoms of living organisms.

### Characteristics of Plants

* Plants prepare their own food through **photosynthesis**.
* They contain the green pigment **chlorophyll**.
* Their cells have a **cell wall** made of cellulose.
* Most plants remain fixed in one place.
* They usually have roots, stems, and leaves.
* Plants release oxygen during photosynthesis.
* They store food mainly in the form of starch.
* Growth usually continues throughout life.

---

## Why are Plants Called Producers?

* Plants prepare food directly from sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide.

* Since they produce food for themselves and indirectly for all other living organisms, they are called **producers**.

Without plants:

* Animals would not get food.
* Oxygen levels would decrease.
* Food chains would collapse.

# Chapter 3: Cell – The Basic Unit of Life

*"The cell is the smallest unit that can carry out all the activities of life."*

---

# Introduction to Cells

* Every living organism, from the tiniest bacterium to the largest blue whale, is made up of one or more **cells**.

* Cells are so small that most of them cannot be seen with the naked eye. They can only be observed using a **microscope**.

* Although cells vary in size, shape, and function, they all perform the basic life processes necessary for survival.

* Just as a building is made of bricks, the body of every living organism is made of cells. Therefore, cells are often called the **building blocks of life**.

---

# What is a Cell?

### Definition

* A **cell** is the **basic structural and functional unit of life**.

* It is called the **structural unit** because every living organism is built from cells.

* It is called the **functional unit** because all essential life processes, such as respiration, nutrition, excretion, growth, and reproduction, occur inside cells.

### Important Point

A single cell is capable of performing all life processes in unicellular organisms, whereas in multicellular organisms, different groups of cells perform specialized functions.

---

# Discovery of the Cell

The discovery of the cell was one of the greatest milestones in Biology.

### Robert Hooke (1665)

Robert Hooke examined a thin slice of cork using a simple microscope.

* He observed many tiny box-like compartments.
* These compartments reminded him of the small rooms used by monks in monasteries.
* He called these compartments **cells**, from the Latin word *cellula*, meaning "small room."

### Important Note

* Hooke observed only the **dead cell walls** of cork.
* He did not observe living cells because cork tissue is dead.

### Olympiad Fact

Although Robert Hooke discovered cells, he did **not** discover living cells.

---

# Antonie van Leeuwenhoek

A few years later, Antonie van Leeuwenhoek made powerful microscopes.

Using these microscopes, he became the **first person to observe living cells**.

He observed:

* Bacteria
* Protozoa
* Tiny organisms present in pond water

Because of his remarkable discoveries, he is often called the **Father of Microbiology**.

---

# Cell Theory

The Cell Theory explains the importance of cells in living organisms.

It was proposed mainly by Matthias Schleiden and Theodor Schwann, and later expanded by Rudolf Virchow.

## Main Points

### 1. All living organisms are made of one or more cells.

Examples

* Amoeba consists of one cell.
* Humans consist of trillions of cells.
* Mango trees contain billions of cells.

---

### 2. The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.

Every activity of the body depends upon the functioning of cells.

Examples

* Muscle cells help movement.
* Nerve cells carry messages.
* Blood cells transport oxygen.

---

### 3. New cells arise only from pre-existing cells.

Cells reproduce by dividing into new cells.

No new cell appears suddenly on its own.

---

| ---------------- | ----------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Red blood cell   | Disc-shaped             | Carries oxygen                      |

The shape of a cell is related to its function.

For example:

* Nerve cells are long so they can transmit messages over long distances.
* Red blood cells are disc-shaped to carry more oxygen efficiently.
* Muscle cells are elongated so they can contract and relax during movement.

# Structure of a Typical Cell

Although cells vary greatly, most cells contain three main parts:

1. Cell membrane
2. Cytoplasm
3. Nucleus

Some cells also contain additional structures called **organelles**, which perform specific functions.

---

# 1. Cell Membrane

The cell membrane is the thin, flexible outer boundary of the cell.

### Functions

* Protects the cell from the external environment.
* Gives the cell its shape.
* Holds the cell contents together.
* Controls the movement of substances into and out of the cell.
* Maintains a stable internal environment.

Because it allows only certain substances to pass through, it is called a **selectively permeable** or **semi-permeable membrane**.

### Example

* Oxygen enters through the cell membrane.
* Carbon dioxide leaves through the cell membrane.
* Nutrients enter the cell.
* Waste materials exit the cell.

### Olympiad Fact

Every living cell has a cell membrane, including plant and animal cells.

---

# 2. Cytoplasm

The cytoplasm is the jelly-like substance present inside the cell membrane.

It surrounds the nucleus and contains various organelles.

### Functions

* Supports cell organelles.
* Site of many chemical reactions.
* Stores nutrients.
* Helps transport materials within the cell.
* Provides shape and support to the cell.

Most life processes occur in the cytoplasm.

---

# 3. Nucleus

The nucleus is usually the largest organelle in the cell.

It is often called the **control centre** or **brain of the cell**.

### Functions

* Controls all cell activities.
* Contains hereditary material (DNA).
* Controls growth.
* Controls cell division.
* Helps in reproduction.

Without the nucleus, most cells cannot survive for long.

---

## Nuclear Membrane

The nucleus is surrounded by a membrane called the **nuclear membrane**.

Its function is to protect the genetic material inside the nucleus.

---

## DNA (Basic Introduction)

Inside the nucleus is a substance called **DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid)**.

DNA contains the hereditary information that is passed from parents to offspring.

Examples of inherited traits:

* Eye colour
* Hair colour
* Height (partly influenced by genes)
* Blood group

# Cell Organelles (Introduction)

Inside the cytoplasm are tiny structures called **organelles**.

Each organelle has a specific function.

At the Class 6 level, you mainly need to know:

* Nucleus – controls activities
* Chloroplast – makes food (plant cells only)
* Vacuole – stores materials
* Cell wall – provides support (plant cells only)

More advanced organelles such as mitochondria, ribosomes, and the endoplasmic reticulum are introduced in higher classes but are useful to know by name for Olympiad preparation.

---

### Did You Know?

* The human body contains **around 30–40 trillion cells**.
* Not all cells are alike—there are over **200 different types of human cells**, each specialized for a particular job.
* A single drop of blood contains **millions of red blood cells**.
* Nerve cells can be more than **1 metre long** in an adult human, making them among the longest cells in the body.

---

### Olympiad Facts

* The cell is the smallest unit capable of independent life.
* Robert Hooke named the cell after observing cork.
* Antonie van Leeuwenhoek was the first to observe living cells.
* The ostrich egg is the largest single animal cell.
* Bacteria are among the smallest living cells.
* Cell shape is closely related to cell function.
* In multicellular organisms, cells are organized into tissues, organs, organ systems, and finally the whole organism.

---

# Why Are Plant Cells Different from Animal Cells?

* Plants prepare their own food through **photosynthesis**, so their cells contain special structures called **chloroplasts**.
* Plants remain fixed in one place and need strong support. Therefore, their cells have a **rigid cell wall**.
* Animals move from one place to another, so their cells are generally more flexible and do not have a cell wall.
* Plant cells usually store large amounts of water, whereas animal cells store comparatively smaller quantities.

---

# Structure of a Plant Cell

A typical plant cell contains the following main parts:

* Cell wall
* Cell membrane
* Cytoplasm
* Nucleus
* Chloroplasts
* Large central vacuole

Each part performs a specific function.

---

# 1. Cell Wall

The **cell wall** is the outermost covering of a plant cell.

### Characteristics

* It is thick and rigid.
* It is made mainly of **cellulose**, a strong natural substance.
* It lies outside the cell membrane.
* It is freely permeable, allowing water and dissolved substances to pass through easily.

### Functions

* Gives the cell a definite shape.
* Provides mechanical support to the plant.
* Protects the cell from injury.
* Prevents the cell from bursting when excess water enters.
* Helps plants remain upright.

### Examples

* The hardness of tree trunks is mainly due to strong cell walls.
* The crisp texture of vegetables like carrots and cucumbers is due to their cell walls.

### Olympiad Fact

Only plant cells have a cell wall. Animal cells do **not** have a cell wall.

---

# 2. Cell Membrane

The **cell membrane** lies just inside the cell wall.

### Characteristics

* It is thin and flexible.
* It surrounds the cytoplasm.
* It is present in both plant and animal cells.

### Functions

* Protects the contents of the cell.
* Controls the movement of substances into and out of the cell.
* Maintains the internal environment of the cell.
* Allows useful substances such as oxygen, water, and nutrients to enter while allowing wastes to leave.

### Remember

The **cell wall provides strength**, whereas the **cell membrane controls movement of substances**.

---

# 3. Cytoplasm

The **cytoplasm** is a jelly-like substance filling most of the cell.

### Characteristics

* Lies between the cell membrane and the nucleus.
* Contains various cell organelles.
* Mostly made of water.

### Functions

* Supports all organelles.
* Site of many chemical reactions.
* Stores nutrients.
* Helps transport materials within the cell.

Without cytoplasm, organelles would not be able to function properly.

---

# 4. Nucleus

The nucleus is usually the most important organelle of the cell.

### Characteristics

* Generally round or oval.
* Surrounded by a nuclear membrane.
* Contains genetic material (DNA).

### Functions

* Controls all activities of the cell.
* Regulates growth and development.
* Controls cell division.
* Stores hereditary information.
* Passes characteristics from parents to offspring.

### Example

The colour of your eyes or hair is influenced by genes present in the DNA inside the nucleus.

### Olympiad Tip

The nucleus is often called the **"brain of the cell"** or **"control centre of the cell."**

---

# 5. Chloroplast

Chloroplasts are found only in green plant cells.

### Characteristics

* Small green organelles.
* Contain **chlorophyll**, the green pigment.

### Functions

* Absorb sunlight.
* Carry out photosynthesis.
* Produce glucose (food).
* Release oxygen.

### Why are leaves green?

Leaves appear green because chlorophyll reflects green light while absorbing other colours of sunlight.

### Olympiad Fact

Roots usually do **not** contain chloroplasts because they remain underground and do not receive sunlight.

---

# 6. Vacuole

A vacuole is a membrane-bound sac used for storage.

### Characteristics

* Plant cells usually contain one **large central vacuole**.
* Filled with a watery liquid called **cell sap**.

### Functions

* Stores water.
* Stores minerals.
* Stores sugars.
* Stores waste materials.
* Maintains the firmness (turgidity) of the plant.

### Example

When a plant does not receive enough water, the vacuole shrinks, causing the plant to wilt.

---

# Structure of an Animal Cell

Animal cells contain:

* Cell membrane
* Cytoplasm
* Nucleus
* Small vacuoles

Unlike plant cells, they do **not** contain:

* Cell wall
* Chloroplasts

---

# Features of Animal Cells

### Flexible Shape

* Since animal cells do not have a rigid cell wall, they can have many different shapes.

Examples

* Muscle cells are long.
* Nerve cells are highly branched.
* White blood cells have irregular shapes.

---

### Small Vacuoles

* Animal cells contain several small vacuoles instead of one large central vacuole.

These vacuoles store:

* Food
* Water
* Waste products

---

### No Chloroplasts

Animals cannot prepare their own food.

Therefore:

* No chlorophyll
* No chloroplasts
* No photosynthesis

Animals depend on plants or other animals for food.

---

# Similarities Between Plant and Animal Cells

Although they differ in several ways, plant and animal cells also have many similarities.

Both contain:

* Cell membrane
* Cytoplasm
* Nucleus
* DNA
* Vacuoles (different sizes)
* Living protoplasm

Both:

* Grow
* Respire
* Divide
* Excrete wastes
* Carry out metabolism

---

# Differences Between Plant Cell and Animal Cell

| Feature        | Plant Cell                 | Animal Cell                |
| -------------- | -------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Cell wall      | Present                    | Absent                     |
| Cell membrane  | Present                    | Present                    |
| Shape          | Usually rectangular        | Usually round or irregular |
| Chloroplast    | Present                    | Absent                     |
| Photosynthesis | Occurs                     | Does not occur             |
| Vacuole        | One large central vacuole  | Many small vacuoles        |
| Food           | Makes its own food         | Depends on other organisms |
| Support        | Cell wall provides support | Skeleton supports the body |

---

# Plant Cell vs Animal Cell – Easy Memory Trick

Remember the word **PCW** for **Plant Cells**:

* **P** – Photosynthesis
* **C** – Chloroplast
* **W** – Cell Wall

If you remember **PCW**, you can quickly identify a plant cell in an Olympiad question.

---

# Cell Shape and Function

Different cells have different shapes because each cell is adapted to perform a specific function.

### Red Blood Cell

Shape

* Disc-shaped

Function

* Carries oxygen throughout the body.
* Its shape provides a large surface area for efficient oxygen transport.

---

### Nerve Cell

Shape

* Very long and highly branched.

Function

* Carries electrical impulses rapidly between the brain, spinal cord, and other parts of the body.

---

### Muscle Cell

Shape

* Long and spindle-shaped.

Function

* Contracts and relaxes to produce movement.

---

### Root Hair Cell

Shape

* Long, thin projection.

Function

* Absorbs water and minerals from the soil.
* The long extension increases the surface area for absorption.

---

### Palisade Cell (Leaf Cell)

Shape

* Tall and rectangular.

Function

* Contains many chloroplasts.
* Performs photosynthesis efficiently.

---

# Cell Division (Basic Introduction)

Cells increase in number by **cell division**.

### Why is Cell Division Important?

* Growth of the body.
* Repair of damaged tissues.
* Replacement of worn-out cells.
* Reproduction in unicellular organisms.

### Example

When you get a cut on your skin, new skin cells are produced through cell division to heal the wound.

---

# Protoplasm

The living substance of a cell is called **protoplasm**.

It consists of:

* Cytoplasm
* Nucleus

Without protoplasm, a cell cannot remain alive.

### Olympiad Fact

The cell wall is **not** a living part of the cell, but the cell membrane, cytoplasm, and nucleus are living components.

---

# Common Mistakes

❌ Plant cells have only a cell wall.

✔ Plant cells have **both** a cell wall and a cell membrane.

---

❌ Chlorophyll and chloroplast are the same.

✔ Chlorophyll is the **green pigment**, while the chloroplast is the **organelle** that contains chlorophyll.

---

❌ All plant cells contain chloroplasts.

✔ Root cells generally do not contain chloroplasts because they are underground and do not receive sunlight.

---

❌ Animal cells are always round.

✔ Animal cells come in many shapes depending on their function.

---

❌ Vacuoles are found only in plant cells.

✔ Vacuoles are found in both plant and animal cells, but they are **large and central in plant cells** and **small and numerous in animal cells**.

---

# Did You Know?

* The longest cell in the human body is the **nerve cell (neuron)**, which can extend over a metre in length.
* The **red blood cell** loses its nucleus when it becomes mature, allowing more space for haemoglobin and increasing its oxygen-carrying capacity.
* Some giant algae consist of a **single cell** that can grow several centimetres long.
* Chloroplasts move within plant cells to capture the maximum amount of sunlight.

---

# Olympiad HOTS Questions

### 1. Why don't animals have chloroplasts?

Because animals cannot prepare their own food through photosynthesis. They obtain food from plants or other animals.

---

### 2. Why do plant cells have a rigid cell wall?

Since plants remain fixed in one place, they need extra strength and support. The cell wall prevents cells from bursting and helps the plant stand upright.

---

### 3. Why do root cells usually lack chloroplasts?

Root cells remain underground and do not receive sunlight. Since photosynthesis requires sunlight, chloroplasts are generally absent.

---

### 4. Why is the nucleus called the control centre?

Because it contains DNA and controls all the activities of the cell, including growth, metabolism, and cell division.

---

# Chapter Summary

* The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life.
* Plant cells have a **cell wall**, **chloroplasts**, and a **large central vacuole**.
* Animal cells lack a cell wall and chloroplasts but have a **cell membrane**, **cytoplasm**, **nucleus**, and **small vacuoles**.
* The cell membrane regulates the movement of substances into and out of the cell.
* The nucleus contains DNA and controls all cell activities.
* Chloroplasts carry out photosynthesis.
* Vacuoles store water, food, minerals, and waste materials.
* Cell shape is closely related to function.
* Cell division enables growth, repair, and reproduction.
* Understanding the similarities and differences between plant and animal cells is essential for answering many Olympiad questions accurately.

# Chapter 4: Photosynthesis and Respiration in Plants

*"Photosynthesis stores energy, while respiration releases energy."*

This chapter is one of the most important topics in Class 6 Science Olympiads. Many Olympiad questions test whether students truly understand how plants make food, why leaves are green, and the differences between photosynthesis and respiration.

---

# Introduction

* Every living organism needs food to survive. Food provides the energy required for growth, repair, movement, reproduction, and all other life processes.

* Unlike animals, **green plants can prepare their own food**. This ability makes plants unique because they do not depend on other organisms for nutrition.

* Plants prepare food by using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide in a process called **photosynthesis**.

* However, preparing food is only one part of a plant's life. Plants must also **release energy** from this food to perform various activities. This second process is called **respiration**.

* Therefore, plants perform **both photosynthesis and respiration**, but these are two completely different processes.

---

# Photosynthesis

## Definition

* **Photosynthesis** is the process by which green plants prepare their own food using **sunlight**, **carbon dioxide**, and **water** in the presence of **chlorophyll**.

* During this process, light energy from the Sun is converted into chemical energy stored in the form of glucose (a type of sugar).

* Oxygen is released as a by-product, making photosynthesis one of the most important biological processes on Earth.

### Simple Definition

**Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants make food using sunlight.**

---

# Why is Photosynthesis Important?

Without photosynthesis, life on Earth would not exist as we know it.

### Importance

* Produces food for plants.
* Provides food directly or indirectly for all animals.
* Releases oxygen required for respiration.
* Removes carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.
* Maintains the balance of gases in the air.
* Forms the starting point of almost every food chain.
* Stores solar energy as chemical energy in food.

### Example

A grass plant prepares food through photosynthesis.

↓

A deer eats the grass.

↓

A tiger eats the deer.

Thus, even carnivores depend indirectly on photosynthesis.

---

# Requirements for Photosynthesis

Four main things are required.

## 1. Sunlight

* Sunlight is the **primary source of energy** for photosynthesis.

* Without sunlight, plants cannot manufacture food.

* The energy from sunlight is captured by chlorophyll and used to convert simple substances into glucose.

### Example

Plants kept in complete darkness for several days stop producing food.

### Olympiad Fact

Photosynthesis usually occurs during the daytime because sunlight is necessary.

---

## 2. Chlorophyll

* Chlorophyll is the **green pigment** present inside chloroplasts.

* It absorbs light energy from the Sun.

* Leaves appear green because chlorophyll reflects green light while absorbing mainly red and blue light.

### Functions

* Traps sunlight.
* Initiates photosynthesis.
* Gives leaves their green colour.

### Important Point

Only green parts of plants containing chlorophyll can carry out photosynthesis efficiently.

---

## 3. Carbon Dioxide

* Carbon dioxide is obtained from the atmosphere.

* It enters the leaf through tiny openings called **stomata**.

### Characteristics

* Carbon dioxide makes up only about **0.04%** of the Earth's atmosphere.
* Even this small amount is enough for photosynthesis.

### Olympiad Tip

Plants absorb carbon dioxide but release oxygen during photosynthesis.

---

## 4. Water

* Water is absorbed by the roots from the soil.

* It is transported to the leaves through special tubes called **xylem vessels**.

### Functions

* Provides hydrogen for making glucose.
* Helps maintain cell turgidity.
* Acts as a raw material for photosynthesis.

Without sufficient water, photosynthesis slows down.

---

# Where Does Photosynthesis Occur?

Photosynthesis mainly occurs in the **leaves**.

Leaves are often called the **food factories of plants**.

### Why are leaves ideal?

* Broad surface area absorbs maximum sunlight.
* Rich in chloroplasts.
* Contain stomata for gas exchange.
* Connected to veins carrying water and food.

---

# Stomata

Stomata are tiny pores present mostly on the lower surface of leaves.

Each stoma is surrounded by two **guard cells**.

### Functions

* Allow carbon dioxide to enter.
* Allow oxygen to leave.
* Permit water vapour to escape during transpiration.
* Help regulate gas exchange.

### Olympiad Fact

Most land plants have more stomata on the lower surface of leaves to reduce water loss.

---

# Chloroplast

A chloroplast is the organelle where photosynthesis occurs.

### Characteristics

* Present only in green plant cells.
* Contains chlorophyll.
* Found mainly in leaf cells.

### Functions

* Captures sunlight.
* Converts light energy into chemical energy.
* Produces glucose.

---

# Products of Photosynthesis

Two major products are formed.

## Glucose

* Glucose is a simple sugar.
* It serves as food for the plant.

Plants use glucose for:

* Respiration
* Growth
* Repair
* Reproduction
* Formation of fruits and seeds

### Storage

Excess glucose is converted into **starch** for storage.

Examples

* Potato
* Rice
* Wheat
* Maize

These plants store large amounts of starch.

---

## Oxygen

Oxygen is released into the atmosphere.

Importance

* Needed by humans for respiration.
* Needed by animals.
* Needed by plants during respiration.
* Supports combustion.

### Interesting Fact

Much of the oxygen in Earth's atmosphere comes from green plants and microscopic algae.

---

# Word Equation for Photosynthesis

**Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen**

Conditions:

* Sunlight
* Chlorophyll

Students should remember this equation even if chemical symbols are not required at this level.

---

# Factors Affecting Photosynthesis

Several conditions influence the rate of photosynthesis.

### Light

More sunlight generally increases photosynthesis until a maximum level is reached.

---

### Water

Water shortage reduces photosynthesis.

This is why plants wilt during drought.

---

### Carbon Dioxide

Higher carbon dioxide concentration can increase photosynthesis.

---

### Temperature

Moderate temperatures are best.

Extremely high or low temperatures reduce photosynthesis.

---

### Chlorophyll

Leaves lacking chlorophyll cannot photosynthesize efficiently.

---

# Destinations of the Food Prepared

The food prepared by leaves is used in several ways.

### Used Immediately

Plants use glucose during respiration to obtain energy.

---

### Stored

Extra food is stored as starch.

Storage organs include:

* Roots (carrot)
* Stems (potato)
* Seeds (rice)
* Fruits (banana)

---

### Transported

Food is transported from leaves to other parts through **phloem tissue**.

---

# Respiration in Plants

Preparing food is not enough.

Plants also need energy.

This energy is released by **respiration**.

---

# Definition

Respiration is the process by which food is broken down to release energy.

The energy released is used for:

* Growth
* Cell division
* Transport of substances
* Repair
* Flowering
* Fruit formation

---

# Does Respiration Need Sunlight?

No.

Respiration occurs:

* During the day
* During the night
* In every living cell

Unlike photosynthesis, respiration never stops while the plant is alive.

---

# Requirements for Respiration

Plants require:

* Glucose
* Oxygen

Products formed:

* Carbon dioxide
* Water
* Energy

---

# Word Equation

**Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy**

---

# Why Do Plants Respire?

Plants need energy for:

* Growing taller.
* Producing flowers.
* Producing fruits.
* Absorbing minerals.
* Repairing damaged tissues.
* Making new cells.
* Reproduction.

Even a sleeping seed continues to respire slowly.

---

# Photosynthesis and Respiration Occur Together

Many students think plants either photosynthesize or respire.

Actually:

* During daytime:

  * Photosynthesis occurs.
  * Respiration also occurs.

* During nighttime:

  * Photosynthesis stops.
  * Respiration continues.

Therefore, respiration occurs **all the time**, whereas photosynthesis occurs **only in the presence of light**.

---

# Photosynthesis vs Respiration

| Photosynthesis               | Respiration                  |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Produces food                | Breaks down food             |
| Stores energy                | Releases energy              |
| Needs sunlight               | Does not need sunlight       |
| Requires chlorophyll         | Does not require chlorophyll |
| Uses carbon dioxide          | Uses oxygen                  |
| Releases oxygen              | Releases carbon dioxide      |
| Mainly occurs in green cells | Occurs in all living cells   |
| Happens only during daylight | Happens day and night        |

---

# Photosynthesis and the Food Chain

Every food chain begins with a producer.

Example:

Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle

The grass prepares food through photosynthesis.

Without producers:

* Herbivores would die.
* Carnivores would die.
* Food chains would collapse.

---

# Importance for Humans

Photosynthesis benefits humans in many ways.

* Produces oxygen.
* Provides food.
* Produces fruits and vegetables.
* Supplies grains.
* Provides wood.
* Produces cotton.
* Reduces atmospheric carbon dioxide.
* Helps reduce global warming.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Plants only photosynthesize.

✔ Plants both photosynthesize **and** respire.

---

❌ Photosynthesis occurs throughout the day and night.

✔ Photosynthesis occurs only when sunlight is available.

---

❌ Plants do not need oxygen.

✔ Plants need oxygen for respiration.

---

❌ Respiration occurs only in leaves.

✔ Respiration occurs in **every living cell** of the plant.

---

❌ Chlorophyll is present in roots.

✔ Most roots lack chlorophyll because they remain underground.

---

# Did You Know?

* A large tree can produce enough oxygen in a year to support several people.
* Ocean algae contribute a significant portion of the Earth's oxygen supply.
* Desert plants often open their stomata at night to reduce water loss, an adaptation known as **CAM photosynthesis** (an advanced Olympiad concept).

---

# Olympiad Facts

* Leaves are called the **food factories** of plants.
* Chloroplasts are the sites of photosynthesis.
* Chlorophyll traps light energy.
* Stomata regulate gas exchange.
* Xylem transports water from roots to leaves.
* Phloem transports prepared food from leaves to other parts of the plant.
* Photosynthesis stores solar energy as chemical energy.
* Respiration releases this stored energy for life processes.

---

# Quick Revision

* Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants prepare food using sunlight.
* Sunlight, chlorophyll, water, and carbon dioxide are essential for photosynthesis.
* Glucose and oxygen are the products of photosynthesis.
* Excess glucose is stored as starch.
* Respiration breaks down glucose to release energy.
* Respiration occurs continuously in all living cells.
* Photosynthesis and respiration are complementary but different processes.
* Xylem carries water, while phloem carries prepared food.
* Stomata help in gas exchange and transpiration.
* Photosynthesis is the foundation of almost every food chain and is essential for maintaining life on Earth.

# Chapter 5: Reproduction in Plants

*"Every seed has the potential to grow into a new plant."*

Reproduction is one of the most important characteristics of living organisms. Without reproduction, no species would survive for long. Plants have developed several interesting methods of producing new plants, ensuring that their species continues from one generation to the next.

---

# What is Reproduction?

## Definition

* **Reproduction** is the biological process by which living organisms produce new individuals of their own kind.

* In plants, reproduction ensures that new plants replace older ones that eventually die.

* Although an individual plant can survive without reproducing, the **species cannot survive** unless reproduction takes place.

### Examples

* A mango tree produces mango seeds that grow into new mango trees.
* A sunflower produces hundreds of seeds during one season.
* A coconut tree produces coconuts, each containing a seed capable of growing into another coconut tree.

### Olympiad Fact

Reproduction is essential for the **continuation of a species**, not for the survival of an individual organism.

---

# Types of Reproduction in Plants

Plants reproduce in two main ways.

## 1. Sexual Reproduction

* Involves the formation of **flowers**, which are the reproductive organs of most flowering plants.
* Male and female reproductive cells unite during fertilization.
* Produces seeds.
* Offspring usually show variation from their parents.

Examples

* Mango
* Rose
* Hibiscus
* Sunflower
* Mustard

---

## 2. Asexual Reproduction

* Does not involve flowers or seeds.
* A new plant develops from a part of the parent plant.
* Only one parent is required.
* Offspring are usually identical to the parent plant.

Examples

* Potato
* Ginger
* Bryophyllum
* Sugarcane
* Rose (stem cutting)

### Olympiad Tip

Plants produced by asexual reproduction are called **clones** because they are genetically identical to the parent.

---

# The Flower – Reproductive Organ of a Plant

Flowers are not only attractive; they play a vital role in plant reproduction.

A complete flower usually consists of four main parts:

1. Sepals
2. Petals
3. Stamens (male part)
4. Pistil or Carpel (female part)

---

# Parts of a Flower

## 1. Sepals

* Sepals are the outermost green, leaf-like structures.
* They protect the flower while it is in the bud stage.
* They prevent damage from insects, wind, and rain before the flower opens.

### Example

If you look at a rose bud, the green covering around it consists of sepals.

---

## 2. Petals

* Petals are usually brightly coloured.
* They attract insects, birds, and other pollinators.
* Some petals also produce fragrance to attract pollinators.

### Functions

* Attract bees and butterflies.
* Protect the reproductive parts.
* Help in pollination.

### Interesting Fact

Flowers pollinated by insects are often brightly coloured, while flowers pollinated by wind are usually small and less colourful.

---

# 3. Stamen (Male Reproductive Part)

The **stamen** is the male reproductive organ.

Each stamen consists of:

### Filament

* Thin stalk.
* Supports the anther.
* Holds the anther in a suitable position.

### Anther

* Located at the top of the filament.
* Produces pollen grains.

---

# Pollen Grains

* Pollen grains contain the **male reproductive cells**.

* They are extremely small and light.

* Different plants produce different shapes of pollen.

### Interesting Fact

A sunflower can produce **thousands of pollen grains** in a single flower.

---

# 4. Pistil (Female Reproductive Part)

The pistil is also called the **carpel**.

It consists of three parts.

---

## Stigma

* Sticky upper part.
* Receives pollen grains during pollination.

The sticky surface prevents pollen from falling away.

---

## Style

* A long tube connecting the stigma to the ovary.

Its function is to allow the pollen tube to grow towards the ovary.

---

## Ovary

The ovary is located at the base of the pistil.

Functions

* Contains one or more ovules.
* Protects the ovules.
* Develops into the fruit after fertilization.

---

# Ovule

The ovule contains the **female reproductive cell (egg cell)**.

After fertilization:

* Ovule → Seed
* Ovary → Fruit

This is one of the favourite Olympiad questions.

---

# Pollination

## Definition

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the **anther** to the **stigma** of a flower.

Pollination occurs before fertilization.

Without pollination, seeds cannot be formed.

---

# Types of Pollination

There are two main types.

---

## 1. Self-Pollination

* Pollen grains are transferred to the stigma of the **same flower** or another flower on the **same plant**.

### Advantages

* Less wastage of pollen.
* Reliable process.
* No dependence on insects or wind.

### Disadvantages

* Little variation in offspring.
* Less adaptability to changing environments.

### Examples

* Pea
* Wheat
* Rice

---

## 2. Cross-Pollination

* Pollen is transferred from the flower of one plant to the flower of another plant of the same species.

### Advantages

* Produces healthier offspring.
* Increases genetic variation.
* Better adaptability.

### Disadvantages

* Depends on pollinating agents.
* More pollen is wasted.

### Examples

* Mango
* Coconut
* Papaya
* Apple

---

# Agents of Pollination

Nature has many helpers that transfer pollen.

---

## Wind

Wind carries light, dry pollen grains.

Examples

* Maize
* Wheat
* Rice

Characteristics of wind-pollinated flowers

* Small flowers
* No fragrance
* Produce enormous amounts of pollen

---

## Insects

The most common pollinators.

Examples

* Bees
* Butterflies
* Beetles

Flowers pollinated by insects usually have

* Bright colours
* Sweet fragrance
* Nectar

---

## Birds

Some birds transfer pollen while feeding on nectar.

Example

* Sunbirds
* Hummingbirds (found in the Americas)

---

## Water

Rare method.

Seen mainly in aquatic plants.

Example

* Vallisneria

---

## Animals

Some bats and small mammals also help in pollination.

---

# Fertilization

## Definition

Fertilization is the fusion of the **male reproductive cell** with the **female reproductive cell**.

It takes place inside the ovule.

After fertilization

* A **zygote** is formed.
* The zygote develops into an embryo.

---

# Formation of Fruit and Seed

After fertilization, several changes occur.

| Before Fertilization | After Fertilization |
| -------------------- | ------------------- |
| Ovary                | Fruit               |
| Ovule                | Seed                |
| Zygote               | Embryo              |

This table is frequently asked in Olympiads.

---

# Seed

A seed is the mature ovule containing a young plant.

Each seed has three main parts.

---

## 1. Seed Coat

* Outer protective covering.
* Prevents injury.
* Protects against drying.

---

## 2. Embryo

* Tiny baby plant.
* Develops into the root and shoot.

---

## 3. Cotyledons

These are called **seed leaves**.

Functions

* Store food.
* Nourish the embryo during germination.

---

# Germination

## Definition

Germination is the process by which a seed develops into a young seedling.

---

# Conditions Required for Germination

Three conditions are essential.

---

## Water

Water softens the seed coat.

It also activates enzymes required for growth.

---

## Oxygen

The embryo respires continuously.

Oxygen provides energy for growth.

---

## Suitable Temperature

Seeds require warmth.

Very high or very low temperatures prevent germination.

---

### Is Sunlight Necessary?

No.

Most seeds do **not** require sunlight for germination.

Sunlight becomes important only after leaves develop and photosynthesis begins.

---

# Stages of Germination

### Stage 1

The seed absorbs water and swells.

---

### Stage 2

The seed coat breaks open.

---

### Stage 3

The **radicle** (young root) emerges first.

This helps absorb water.

---

### Stage 4

The **plumule** (young shoot) grows upward.

---

### Stage 5

Leaves appear.

The seedling begins photosynthesis.

---

# Seed Dispersal

If all seeds fell beneath the parent plant, they would compete for:

* Water
* Minerals
* Sunlight
* Space

Therefore, seeds are dispersed.

---

# Methods of Seed Dispersal

## Wind

Seeds are light and have wings or hairs.

Examples

* Cotton
* Dandelion
* Maple

---

## Water

Seeds float because of air spaces or fibrous coverings.

Examples

* Coconut
* Lotus

---

## Animals

Seeds stick to fur or are carried after fruits are eaten.

Examples

* Mango
* Guava
* Xanthium (cocklebur)

---

## Explosion

Some fruits burst open suddenly.

Examples

* Pea
* Balsam
* Castor

---

# Asexual Reproduction (Vegetative Propagation)

Many plants can grow from stems, roots, or leaves.

This is called **vegetative propagation**.

---

## Stem

Examples

* Potato (stem tuber)
* Sugarcane
* Rose
* Money plant

---

## Root

Examples

* Sweet potato
* Dahlia

---

## Leaf

Example

* Bryophyllum

Tiny buds grow along the leaf margins.

---

# Advantages of Vegetative Propagation

* Rapid multiplication.
* New plants grow quickly.
* Offspring remain identical.
* Useful for seedless plants like banana.

---

# Disadvantages

* Little genetic variation.
* Diseases spread easily.
* Entire crop may be affected by one disease.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Fruits become seeds.

✔ Ovaries become fruits, while ovules become seeds.

---

❌ Germination requires sunlight.

✔ Water, oxygen, and suitable temperature are essential. Sunlight is usually not required initially.

---

❌ All flowers produce fruits.

✔ Only flowers that undergo successful fertilization produce fruits.

---

❌ All plants reproduce through seeds.

✔ Many plants reproduce through stems, roots, or leaves.

---

# Did You Know?

* The smallest seeds belong to orchids and are almost dust-like.
* The largest seed in the world is the **coco de mer**, which can weigh more than **15 kg**.
* A single sunflower head may produce over **1,000 seeds**.
* Some seeds remain dormant for years before germinating when conditions become favourable.

---

# Olympiad Facts

* Flower is the reproductive organ of flowering plants.
* Stamen is the male reproductive part.
* Pistil (carpel) is the female reproductive part.
* Pollination occurs **before** fertilization.
* Ovule becomes the seed.
* Ovary becomes the fruit.
* The radicle develops into the root, while the plumule develops into the shoot.
* Germination requires water, oxygen, and a suitable temperature.
* Vegetative propagation produces genetically identical offspring.

---

# Quick Revision

* Plants reproduce by **sexual** and **asexual** methods.
* Flowers contain male (stamen) and female (pistil) reproductive organs.
* Pollination transfers pollen from the anther to the stigma.
* Fertilization occurs inside the ovule and forms a zygote.
* After fertilization, the ovary becomes the fruit and the ovule becomes the seed.
* A seed contains a seed coat, embryo, and cotyledons.
* Germination begins when the seed absorbs water and ends with the development of a young seedling.
* Seeds are dispersed by wind, water, animals, and explosive bursting of fruits.
* Vegetative propagation allows new plants to grow from stems, roots, or leaves without forming seeds.

# Chapter 6: Human Body Systems

*"The human body is made up of several organ systems that work together to keep us alive, healthy, and active."*

The human body is a highly organized system. Different organs perform different functions, but they do not work alone. Organs that work together to perform a major function form an **organ system**.

Just as different departments in a school work together to run the school smoothly, different organ systems work together to keep our body functioning properly.

---

# What is an Organ System?

## Definition

* An **organ system** is a group of organs that work together to perform a particular function in the body.

### Examples

| Organ System       | Main Function                            |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------- |
| Digestive System   | Digests food and absorbs nutrients       |
| Respiratory System | Exchanges oxygen and carbon dioxide      |
| Circulatory System | Transports oxygen, nutrients and wastes  |
| Skeletal System    | Supports and protects the body           |
| Muscular System    | Produces movement                        |
| Nervous System     | Controls and coordinates body activities |
| Excretory System   | Removes wastes from the body             |

### Olympiad Tip

Remember the order of organization:

**Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism**

---

# 1. Digestive System

Every living organism needs food. However, the food we eat cannot be used directly by the body.

For example:

* A chapati cannot directly enter our blood.
* A piece of apple cannot directly reach our muscles.

Food must first be broken down into simpler substances. This process is called **digestion**.

---

# What is Digestion?

## Definition

* **Digestion** is the process of breaking down complex food into simple, soluble substances that can be absorbed and used by the body.

### Why is Digestion Necessary?

Food is required for:

* Growth
* Repair of damaged tissues
* Energy production
* Formation of new cells
* Protection against diseases

Only digested food can enter the bloodstream.

---

# Parts of the Digestive System

The digestive system consists of the following organs:

* Mouth
* Teeth
* Tongue
* Salivary glands
* Oesophagus (Food pipe)
* Stomach
* Small intestine
* Large intestine
* Rectum
* Anus

### Journey of Food

**Mouth → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine → Large Intestine → Rectum → Anus**

This sequence is frequently asked in Olympiad exams.

---

# Mouth

The mouth is the starting point of digestion.

### Functions

* Receives food.
* Breaks food into smaller pieces.
* Mixes food with saliva.
* Helps in swallowing.
* Begins the digestion of starch.

---

# Teeth

Humans have different kinds of teeth, each designed for a specific job.

## Types of Teeth

### 1. Incisors

Functions

* Cut food.
* Located at the front.

Examples

* Biting an apple.
* Cutting vegetables.

---

### 2. Canines

Functions

* Tear food.
* Sharp and pointed.

Examples

* Tearing meat.

Humans have small canines compared to lions and tigers.

---

### 3. Premolars

Functions

* Crush food.
* Begin grinding.

---

### 4. Molars

Functions

* Grind food thoroughly.
* Largest and strongest teeth.

### Olympiad Fact

An adult normally has **32 permanent teeth**, while children usually have **20 milk teeth**.

---

# Tongue

The tongue is a muscular organ.

### Functions

* Mixes food with saliva.
* Helps in chewing.
* Pushes food for swallowing.
* Contains taste buds.

### Taste Buds Detect

* Sweet
* Sour
* Salty
* Bitter
* Umami (advanced concept)

---

# Saliva

Saliva is produced by the salivary glands.

### Functions

* Moistens food.
* Softens food.
* Helps swallowing.
* Begins digestion of starch with the enzyme **salivary amylase**.

### Olympiad Fact

Digestion actually begins in the mouth, not in the stomach.

---

# Oesophagus (Food Pipe)

The oesophagus is a muscular tube connecting the mouth to the stomach.

### Functions

* Carries food to the stomach.
* Does not digest food.

Food moves through the oesophagus by rhythmic muscular contractions called **peristalsis**.

### Peristalsis

* Wave-like muscular movement.
* Pushes food downward.
* Occurs even if a person is upside down.

---

# Stomach

The stomach is a muscular, J-shaped organ.

### Functions

* Stores food for a short time.
* Churns and mixes food.
* Produces gastric juice.
* Begins digestion of proteins.
* Kills many harmful germs present in food.

### Gastric Juice Contains

* Hydrochloric acid (HCl)
* Digestive enzymes
* Mucus

### Why doesn't the stomach digest itself?

The stomach produces mucus, which protects its inner lining from the acid.

---

# Small Intestine

The small intestine is the **longest part of the digestive system**.

It is about **6–7 metres long** in adults.

### Functions

* Completes digestion.
* Absorbs digested nutrients into the blood.
* Receives digestive juices from the liver and pancreas.

### Villi

The inner wall contains millions of tiny finger-like projections called **villi**.

Functions of villi

* Increase surface area.
* Absorb nutrients efficiently.
* Transfer nutrients into the bloodstream.

### Olympiad Fact

Most digestion and almost all absorption occur in the **small intestine**.

---

# Liver

The liver is the largest internal organ of the human body.

### Functions

* Produces bile.
* Stores glycogen.
* Removes harmful substances from blood.
* Helps digest fats.

### Bile

Bile is stored in the **gall bladder**.

It helps break large fat droplets into smaller droplets, making fat digestion easier.

---

# Pancreas

The pancreas produces pancreatic juice.

### Functions

* Helps digest carbohydrates.
* Helps digest proteins.
* Helps digest fats.

The pancreas also produces **insulin**, a hormone that controls blood sugar (advanced awareness).

---

# Large Intestine

After nutrients have been absorbed, undigested food enters the large intestine.

### Functions

* Absorbs water.
* Absorbs some salts.
* Houses helpful bacteria.
* Forms faeces.

### Interesting Fact

Helpful bacteria in the large intestine produce certain vitamins, such as vitamin K.

---

# Rectum

The rectum stores faeces until they are removed from the body.

---

# Anus

The anus is the opening through which faeces leave the body.

This process is called **egestion**.

---

# Digestion vs Egestion

Many students confuse these terms.

| Digestion                                    | Egestion                   |
| -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Breakdown of food                            | Removal of undigested food |
| Occurs mainly in stomach and small intestine | Occurs through the anus    |
| Produces nutrients                           | Removes waste              |

Cells use oxygen to:

# Chapter 6 (Continued): Human Body Systems

## Part 2 – Circulatory System, Skeletal System and Muscular System

These three organ systems work closely together. The **circulatory system** supplies oxygen and nutrients to every cell, the **skeletal system** provides support and protection, and the **muscular system** enables movement. Without the combined action of these systems, our body would not be able to function properly.

---

# 3. Circulatory System

Every cell in the human body requires oxygen, nutrients, and water to survive. Similarly, waste products such as carbon dioxide must be removed continuously.

The **circulatory system** acts like a transportation network that carries useful substances to cells and removes waste materials.

Imagine a city where trucks deliver food, water, and medicines to every house while garbage trucks collect waste. The circulatory system performs a similar function inside our body.

---

# What is the Circulatory System?

## Definition

* The **circulatory system** is the organ system responsible for transporting blood, oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste materials throughout the body.

* It ensures that every cell receives the substances needed to stay alive and function properly.

---

# Main Parts of the Circulatory System

The circulatory system consists of:

* Heart
* Blood
* Blood vessels

Each part performs a specific function.

---

# Heart

The **heart** is the main organ of the circulatory system.

It is a strong muscular organ about the size of a person's closed fist.

### Location

* Located slightly towards the left side of the chest.
* Protected by the rib cage.
* Positioned between the two lungs.

---

## Functions of the Heart

The heart works continuously throughout life.

Its main functions are:

* Pumps blood to every part of the body.
* Supplies oxygen to body cells.
* Carries nutrients to all organs.
* Removes carbon dioxide.
* Helps transport hormones.
* Maintains blood circulation.

### Interesting Fact

The human heart beats approximately **70–80 times every minute** when a person is at rest.

That means:

* About **100,000 beats per day**
* More than **35 million beats every year**

The heart works continuously without resting.

---

# Chambers of the Heart (Basic Introduction)

The human heart has **four chambers**.

* Right atrium
* Right ventricle
* Left atrium
* Left ventricle

For Class 6, remember that:

* The upper chambers receive blood.
* The lower chambers pump blood.

---

# Heartbeat

A **heartbeat** consists of one complete contraction and relaxation of the heart muscles.

Each heartbeat pumps blood throughout the body.

Doctors use a **stethoscope** to listen to heartbeats.

---

# Pulse

When the heart pumps blood, arteries expand slightly.

This expansion is felt as the **pulse**.

### Where can pulse be felt?

* Wrist
* Neck

Normally, the pulse rate is almost equal to the heartbeat.

---

# Blood

Blood is a special type of **connective tissue**.

An average adult has about **5 litres of blood**.

Blood transports substances throughout the body.

---

# Functions of Blood

Blood performs many important functions.

### Transport

* Carries oxygen from the lungs.
* Carries nutrients from the digestive system.
* Carries hormones from glands.
* Carries water throughout the body.

---

### Protection

* White blood cells destroy harmful germs.
* Antibodies help fight diseases.

---

### Regulation

Blood helps regulate:

* Body temperature
* Water balance
* pH level

---

### Removal of Waste

Blood carries:

* Carbon dioxide to the lungs.
* Urea to the kidneys.

---

# Components of Blood

Blood has four main components.

---

# 1. Plasma

Plasma is the **liquid part** of blood.

It forms about **55%** of blood.

### Functions

* Transports nutrients.
* Carries hormones.
* Carries waste materials.
* Transports proteins.
* Carries blood cells.

Plasma is mostly made of water.

---

# 2. Red Blood Cells (RBCs)

Red blood cells are the most numerous blood cells.

### Characteristics

* Disc-shaped.
* Flexible.
* Contain haemoglobin.
* Mature RBCs do not have a nucleus.

### Functions

* Carry oxygen.
* Carry some carbon dioxide back to the lungs.

### Haemoglobin

Haemoglobin is a red pigment.

Its functions are:

* Gives blood its red colour.
* Combines with oxygen.
* Transports oxygen throughout the body.

### Olympiad Fact

People with low haemoglobin may suffer from **anaemia**.

---

# 3. White Blood Cells (WBCs)

White blood cells protect the body.

### Functions

* Destroy bacteria.
* Destroy viruses.
* Produce antibodies.
* Prevent infections.

### Interesting Fact

When harmful germs enter the body, the number of white blood cells often increases.

---

# 4. Platelets

Platelets are tiny cell fragments.

### Functions

* Help blood clot.
* Prevent excessive bleeding.
* Help heal wounds.

### Example

When you get a small cut, platelets help stop the bleeding by forming a clot.

---

# Blood Vessels

Blood flows through tubes called **blood vessels**.

There are three main types.

---

# 1. Arteries

### Characteristics

* Carry blood away from the heart.
* Thick muscular walls.
* High blood pressure.
* Usually carry oxygen-rich blood.

### Example

The aorta is the largest artery in the human body.

---

# 2. Veins

### Characteristics

* Carry blood towards the heart.
* Thin walls.
* Contain valves.
* Usually carry oxygen-poor blood.

The valves prevent blood from flowing backwards.

---

# 3. Capillaries

Capillaries are the smallest blood vessels.

### Characteristics

* Extremely thin walls.
* Connect arteries and veins.

### Functions

* Exchange oxygen.
* Exchange nutrients.
* Exchange wastes.
* Exchange carbon dioxide.

Most exchange of materials occurs in capillaries.

---

# Arteries vs Veins

| Arteries                    | Veins                     |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Carry blood away from heart | Carry blood towards heart |
| Thick walls                 | Thin walls                |
| High pressure               | Low pressure              |
| Usually oxygen-rich         | Usually oxygen-poor       |
| No valves                   | Valves present            |

---

# Blood Circulation

The journey of blood is called **blood circulation**.

The heart continuously pumps blood in a closed pathway.

### Simple Path

Heart → Arteries → Capillaries → Veins → Heart

---

# Importance of the Circulatory System

Without blood circulation:

* Cells would not receive oxygen.
* Nutrients could not reach tissues.
* Wastes would accumulate.
* Body temperature could not be controlled.
* Life would not be possible.

# Chapter 7: Food, Nutrition, Health and Diseases

Food is one of the basic necessities of life. Every living organism needs food to grow, obtain energy, repair damaged tissues, and protect itself from diseases. # What is Food?

* **Food** is any nutritious substance that is eaten or drunk to provide energy, support growth, repair body tissues, and regulate body functions.

* Food supplies the nutrients needed for all life processes.

### Why Do We Need Food?

Food is essential because it:

* Provides energy for daily activities.
* Helps the body grow.
* Repairs damaged tissues.
* Builds muscles and bones.
* Protects the body from diseases.
* Regulates body processes.
* Maintains body temperature.

# Chapter 8: Matter – Materials Around Us

*"Everything around us that has mass and occupies space is called matter."*

Matter is one of the most fundamental concepts in science. Every object we see, touch, smell, or use in our daily lives is made of matter. Whether it is the air we breathe, the water we drink, the food we eat, or the chair we sit on, everything is matter.

This chapter introduces the properties of matter, states of matter, changes of state, physical and chemical changes, mixtures, separation techniques, and related Olympiad concepts.

---

# What is Matter?

## Definition

* **Matter** is anything that has **mass** and **occupies space**.

* Mass is the amount of matter in an object.

* Occupying space means that matter has **volume**.

### Examples of Matter

* Air
* Water
* Stone
* Wood
* Milk
* Iron
* Sugar
* Sand
* Human body
* Trees

All these objects have mass and occupy space.

---

# Things That Are NOT Matter

Some things do not have mass or occupy space.

Examples include:

* Light
* Heat
* Sound
* Electricity
* Magnetism
* Shadows

These are forms of energy, not matter.

### Olympiad Tip

If something **has mass and occupies space**, it is matter. If it is only a form of energy, it is **not** matter.

---

# Characteristics of Matter

All matter has certain common properties.

### 1. Matter Has Mass

Every object has some mass.

Examples:

* A book has mass.
* A pencil has mass.
* Even air has mass.

### Experiment

Blow up two balloons.

Hang them on opposite sides of a ruler.

Burst one balloon.

The side with the inflated balloon moves downward because air has mass.

---

### 2. Matter Occupies Space

Everything made of matter takes up space.

Example

Place a stone into a glass full of water.

Water overflows because the stone occupies space.

---

### 3. Matter is Made of Tiny Particles

Matter is made of extremely tiny particles called **atoms** and **molecules**.

These particles are too small to be seen with the naked eye.

### Example

A grain of sugar appears solid, but it contains millions of tiny particles.

---

### 4. Particles Have Spaces Between Them

The particles of matter are not packed tightly together.

There are tiny spaces between them.

### Experiment

Add sugar to a glass of water.

The sugar disappears, but the water level changes very little because sugar particles occupy spaces between water particles.

---

### 5. Particles are in Constant Motion

Particles are always moving.

Their speed depends on temperature.

* Higher temperature → faster movement
* Lower temperature → slower movement

This explains why perfume spreads throughout a room.

---

### 6. Particles Attract One Another

Particles exert attractive forces.

The strength of attraction varies.

* Strongest in solids
* Moderate in liquids
* Weakest in gases

---

# States of Matter

Matter exists in three common states.

* Solid
* Liquid
* Gas

(Some advanced Olympiads also introduce **plasma** and **Bose-Einstein condensate**, but these are beyond Class 6.)

---

# 1. Solids

## Characteristics

* Definite shape.
* Definite volume.
* Particles packed closely together.
* Strong intermolecular attraction.
* Very little movement of particles.
* Cannot be compressed easily.

### Examples

* Ice
* Stone
* Iron
* Wood
* Pencil
* Book

---

### Why Are Solids Hard?

Particles are tightly packed.

They cannot move freely.

Therefore solids maintain their shape.

---

### Examples in Daily Life

* Table
* School bag
* Brick
* Spoon

---

# 2. Liquids

## Characteristics

* Definite volume.
* No definite shape.
* Take the shape of their container.
* Flow easily.
* Particles are less tightly packed.
* Slightly compressible.

### Examples

* Water
* Milk
* Oil
* Juice
* Petrol

---

### Why Do Liquids Flow?

Particles can slide over one another.

This allows liquids to flow.

---

### Surface of Liquids

Liquids always have a horizontal upper surface when kept in a container.

---

# 3. Gases

## Characteristics

* No definite shape.
* No definite volume.
* Fill the entire container.
* Highly compressible.
* Particles move very rapidly.
* Very weak attractive forces.

### Examples

* Oxygen
* Nitrogen
* Carbon dioxide
* Hydrogen
* Helium

---

### Why Can Gases Be Compressed?

Large spaces exist between particles.

These spaces can be reduced by applying pressure.

---

# Comparison of Solids, Liquids and Gases

| Property             | Solid        | Liquid                  | Gas                    |
| -------------------- | ------------ | ----------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Shape                | Fixed        | Takes container's shape | Fills entire container |
| Volume               | Fixed        | Fixed                   | Not fixed              |
| Particle arrangement | Very close   | Moderately close        | Far apart              |
| Particle movement    | Vibrate only | Slide                   | Move freely            |
| Compressibility      | Very low     | Low                     | High                   |
| Flow                 | No           | Yes                     | Yes                    |

---

# Changes of State

Matter can change from one state to another by heating or cooling.

---

# Melting

## Definition

Melting is the process in which a solid changes into a liquid upon heating.

### Example

Ice → Water

### Melting Point

The temperature at which a solid melts is called its **melting point**.

Example

Ice melts at **0°C**.

---

# Freezing

## Definition

Freezing is the process in which a liquid changes into a solid on cooling.

### Example

Water → Ice

### Freezing Point

Water freezes at **0°C**.

---

# Evaporation

## Definition

Evaporation is the slow conversion of a liquid into vapour from its surface.

It can occur at any temperature.

### Examples

* Drying clothes.
* Sweat evaporating.
* Wet roads drying after rain.

---

### Factors Affecting Evaporation

* Temperature
* Wind speed
* Surface area
* Humidity

Higher temperature increases evaporation.

---

# Boiling

Boiling is the rapid conversion of a liquid into gas throughout the liquid.

Water boils at **100°C** under normal atmospheric pressure.

---

# Condensation

Condensation is the conversion of gas into liquid by cooling.

### Examples

* Water droplets on a cold bottle.
* Morning dew.
* Clouds forming.

---

# Sublimation

Some substances change directly from solid to gas without becoming liquid.

This process is called **sublimation**.

### Examples

* Camphor
* Naphthalene balls
* Dry ice (solid carbon dioxide)
* Iodine

---

# Physical Changes

## Definition

A physical change changes only the appearance or state of a substance.

No new substance is formed.

Usually reversible.

---

### Examples

* Melting ice.
* Breaking glass.
* Cutting paper.
* Boiling water.
* Freezing water.

---

### Characteristics

* No new substance formed.
* Usually reversible.
* Chemical composition remains unchanged.

---

# Chemical Changes

## Definition

A chemical change forms one or more new substances with different properties.

Usually irreversible.

---

### Examples

* Burning paper.
* Rusting of iron.
* Cooking food.
* Digestion.
* Curd formation.

---

### Characteristics

* New substances formed.
* Often irreversible.
* Energy may be released or absorbed.
* Colour may change.
* Gas may be produced.

---

# Physical vs Chemical Change

| Physical Change       | Chemical Change       |
| --------------------- | --------------------- |
| No new substance      | New substance formed  |
| Usually reversible    | Usually irreversible  |
| Composition unchanged | Composition changes   |
| Example: Melting ice  | Example: Burning wood |

---

# Pure Substances and Mixtures

## Pure Substance

Contains only one kind of substance.

Examples

* Pure water
* Pure gold
* Oxygen
* Copper

---

## Mixture

A mixture contains two or more substances physically combined.

Examples

* Air
* Salt water
* Lemonade
* Soil

---

# Types of Mixtures

### Homogeneous Mixture

Uniform throughout.

Examples

* Salt solution
* Sugar solution
* Air

---

### Heterogeneous Mixture

Not uniform.

Examples

* Sand and water
* Oil and water
* Soil

---

# Separation of Mixtures

Different methods are used depending on the properties of substances.

---

# Handpicking

Used when impurities are large.

Example

Removing stones from rice.

---

# Winnowing

Uses wind.

Example

Separating husk from grains.

---

# Sieving

Uses different particle sizes.

Examples

* Flour.
* Sand.

---

# Sedimentation

Heavy particles settle at the bottom.

Example

Mud in water.

---

# Decantation

Clear liquid is poured carefully.

Often follows sedimentation.

---

# Filtration

Separates insoluble solids.

Examples

* Tea leaves.
* Sand from water.

Filter paper allows liquid to pass but retains solids.

---

# Evaporation

Used to recover dissolved solids.

Example

Obtaining salt from seawater.

---

# Magnetic Separation

Uses magnets.

Example

Iron filings from sand.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Air is not matter.

✔ Air has mass and occupies space.

---

❌ Gases have no weight.

✔ Gases have mass and therefore weight.

---

❌ Boiling and evaporation are the same.

✔ Evaporation occurs only at the surface and at any temperature, whereas boiling occurs throughout the liquid at its boiling point.

---

❌ Melting and dissolving are the same.

✔ Melting changes a solid into a liquid by heating, while dissolving forms a solution without necessarily changing the state of the solid.

---

# Did You Know?

* Air is a mixture of gases, mainly nitrogen (about **78%**) and oxygen (about **21%**).
* Honey is a **supercooled liquid** that flows very slowly because of its high viscosity.
* Dry ice changes directly from solid to gas without becoming liquid.
* Water is one of the few substances that expands when it freezes, which is why ice floats on water.

---

# Olympiad Facts

* Matter has mass and occupies space.
* Solids have the strongest intermolecular forces.
* Gases are highly compressible because their particles are far apart.
* Ice melts and water freezes at **0°C**.
* Water boils at **100°C** at normal atmospheric pressure.
* Sublimation occurs in camphor, naphthalene, iodine, and dry ice.
* Physical changes do not produce new substances.
* Chemical changes produce new substances with different properties.
* Filtration separates insoluble solids from liquids, while evaporation recovers dissolved solids.

---

# Quick Revision

* Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space.
* Matter exists mainly as solids, liquids, and gases.
* Particles of matter are tiny, constantly moving, and attract one another.
* Heating and cooling cause changes of state such as melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, and sublimation.
* Physical changes do not form new substances, while chemical changes do.
* Mixtures can be homogeneous or heterogeneous.
* Common separation methods include handpicking, winnowing, sieving, filtration, decantation, evaporation, and magnetic separation.

# Chapter 9: Force, Motion and Simple Machines

*"A small force can produce a big effect when applied in the right way."*

Force and motion are everywhere in our daily lives. We push doors, pull drawers, ride bicycles, throw balls, and lift school bags. All these activities involve **forces** that produce **motion**. Humans have also invented **simple machines** to make work easier, faster, and more efficient.

This chapter is one of the highest-scoring chapters in Class 6 Science Olympiads because it includes many concept-based and application-based questions.

---

# What is Force?

## Definition

* **Force** is a **push or a pull** acting on an object.

* A force can change the state of motion, shape, size, or direction of an object.

### SI Unit

The SI unit of force is the **newton (N)**.

Named after Isaac Newton.

---

# Examples of Force

* Pushing a shopping trolley.
* Pulling a suitcase.
* Kicking a football.
* Opening a drawer.
* Stretching a rubber band.
* Pulling a bucket from a well.

Every one of these activities involves force.

---

# Effects of Force

A force can produce several effects.

---

## 1. Move a Stationary Object

A stationary football starts moving when kicked.

---

## 2. Stop a Moving Object

Applying bicycle brakes stops the bicycle.

---

## 3. Change Speed

Pedalling harder makes a bicycle move faster.

Braking reduces speed.

---

## 4. Change Direction

A batsman changes the direction of a cricket ball.

A goalkeeper changes the direction of a football.

---

## 5. Change Shape

Examples

* Stretching a rubber band.
* Squeezing a sponge.
* Crushing a plastic bottle.

---

# Types of Force

For Class 6, forces are mainly divided into two groups.

---

# Contact Forces

These require physical contact.

---

## Muscular Force

Force produced by muscles.

Examples

* Lifting a school bag.
* Carrying a bucket.
* Writing with a pencil.
* Climbing stairs.

---

## Frictional Force

Friction acts between two surfaces in contact.

It always opposes motion.

### Advantages

* Helps us walk.
* Helps vehicles stop.
* Allows writing on paper.
* Prevents slipping.

### Disadvantages

* Produces heat.
* Causes wear and tear.
* Wastes energy.
* Reduces machine efficiency.

---

### Reducing Friction

* Lubrication (oil or grease).
* Ball bearings.
* Polishing surfaces.

---

### Increasing Friction

* Rough shoe soles.
* Tyre treads.
* Sand on slippery roads.

---

# Non-Contact Forces

These act without touching the object.

---

## Gravitational Force

Gravity is the force that pulls objects towards the Earth.

### Examples

* Apple falling.
* Rain falling.
* Jumping back to the ground.

### Olympiad Fact

Gravity acts on every object having mass.

---

## Magnetic Force

Magnets attract certain materials.

Examples

* Iron
* Nickel
* Cobalt

### Uses

* Refrigerator doors.
* Magnetic compass.
* Cranes lifting scrap iron.

---

## Electrostatic Force

Occurs due to electric charges.

Example

Rub a balloon on your hair.

The balloon can attract small bits of paper.

---

# Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

---

## Balanced Forces

Balanced forces are **equal in magnitude but opposite in direction**.

Result:

* No change in motion.

### Example

Two equally strong teams pulling a rope in opposite directions during tug-of-war.

The rope does not move.

---

## Unbalanced Forces

Unequal forces.

Result:

* Motion changes.

### Example

One team pulls harder.

The rope moves towards that team.

---

# Motion

## Definition

Motion is the **change in position of an object with time**.

If an object's position changes, it is said to be moving.

---

# Rest

An object is said to be **at rest** if its position does not change with time.

Example

* Parked car.
* Book on a table.

---

# Types of Motion

---

## 1. Rectilinear Motion

Movement along a straight line.

Examples

* Train on a straight track.
* Lift moving vertically.

---

## 2. Circular Motion

Movement along a circular path.

Examples

* Clock hands.
* Ceiling fan.
* Ferris wheel.

---

## 3. Rotational Motion

Object rotates about its own axis.

Examples

* Earth rotating.
* Spinning top.
* Potter's wheel.

---

## 4. Oscillatory Motion

Back-and-forth motion about a fixed point.

Examples

* Swing.
* Pendulum.
* Guitar string.

---

## 5. Periodic Motion

Motion repeated at regular intervals.

Examples

* Earth's revolution around the Sun.
* Pendulum.
* Clock hands.

### Olympiad Tip

Every oscillatory motion is periodic, but not every periodic motion is oscillatory.

---

# Speed

## Definition

Speed is the **distance travelled in unit time**.

### Formula

**Speed = Distance ÷ Time**

### SI Unit

**metre per second (m/s)**

Other common unit:

**kilometre per hour (km/h)**

---

# Distance

Distance is the total length of the path travelled.

### SI Unit

metre (m)

---

# Time

Time measures how long an event takes.

### SI Unit

second (s)

---

# Measuring Speed

Examples

* Speedometer measures vehicle speed.
* Odometer measures total distance travelled.

---

# Simple Machines

Humans use machines to make work easier.

---

# What is a Machine?

A **machine** is a device that makes work easier by changing the magnitude or direction of force.

Machines do **not** reduce the total amount of work done, but they reduce the effort required.

---

# What are Simple Machines?

Simple machines have few moving parts.

There are **six classical simple machines**.

* Lever
* Pulley
* Wheel and axle
* Inclined plane
* Screw
* Wedge

---

# 1. Lever

A lever is a rigid bar that rotates about a fixed point.

---

## Parts of a Lever

### Fulcrum

Fixed point.

---

### Load

Object to be moved.

---

### Effort

Force applied.

---

### Examples

* Seesaw.
* Crowbar.
* Bottle opener.
* Nutcracker.
* Scissors.

---

# Classes of Levers

---

## First-Class Lever

Fulcrum lies between effort and load.

Examples

* Seesaw.
* Scissors.
* Crowbar.

---

## Second-Class Lever

Load lies between fulcrum and effort.

Examples

* Wheelbarrow.
* Nutcracker.
* Bottle opener.

---

## Third-Class Lever

Effort lies between fulcrum and load.

Examples

* Tweezers.
* Fishing rod.
* Human forearm.

---

# 2. Pulley

A pulley is a grooved wheel with a rope passing over it.

### Uses

* Draw water from wells.
* Lift heavy loads.
* Construction cranes.

### Advantages

* Changes direction of force.
* Makes lifting easier.

---

# 3. Wheel and Axle

A large wheel connected to a smaller axle.

Examples

* Bicycle wheel.
* Steering wheel.
* Door knob.

---

# 4. Inclined Plane

A sloping surface connecting two different heights.

Examples

* Ramp.
* Staircase.
* Slide.

### Advantage

Allows heavy objects to be moved upward using less effort.

---

# 5. Screw

A screw is an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder.

Examples

* Jar lid.
* Bolt.
* Screwdriver screw.

### Uses

* Fastening objects.
* Lifting devices like screw jacks.

---

# Chapter 12: Heat and Temperature

*"Heat is a form of energy, while temperature tells us how hot or cold an object is."*

Heat plays an important role in our daily lives. It helps us cook food, keeps us warm during winter, dries clothes, and powers many machines. Understanding heat and temperature also helps explain why metals expand on heating, why sea breezes occur, and why thermometers work.

Heat and temperature are often confused, but they are **not the same**. This chapter explains these concepts in detail and covers the transfer of heat, expansion, thermometers, conductors and insulators of heat, and Olympiad-level applications.

# What is Heat?

## Definition

* **Heat** is a form of energy that flows from a hotter object to a colder object because of a difference in temperature.

* Heat always flows naturally from a hot object to a cold object until both reach the same temperature.

### Examples

* A hot cup of tea cools down.
* Ice melts in warm water.
* A metal spoon becomes hot in hot soup.
* Hands become warm when rubbed together.

---

# Sources of Heat

Heat can be produced from many sources.

### Natural Sources

* Sun
* Volcanoes
* Hot springs

The **Sun** is the primary natural source of heat for Earth.

---

### Artificial Sources

* Gas stove
* Electric heater
* Candle
* Coal
* Firewood
* Microwave oven

---

# Importance of Heat

Heat is essential because it:

* Keeps the Earth warm.
* Helps plants grow.
* Cooks food.
* Dries clothes.
* Produces electricity in thermal power plants.
* Helps in many industrial processes.
* Maintains body temperature.

Without heat from the Sun, life on Earth would not exist.

---

# What is Temperature?

## Definition

* **Temperature** is the measure of how hot or cold an object is.

It indicates the average kinetic energy of the particles in a substance.

---

# Heat vs Temperature

Many students confuse these terms.

| Heat                           | Temperature                                                           |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Form of energy                 | Measure of hotness or coldness                                        |
| Flows from hot to cold         | Does not flow                                                         |
| Measured in joules (J)         | Measured in °C, °F, or K                                              |
| Depends on amount of substance | Independent of the amount for a given material at uniform temperature |

**32°F**

Water boils at:

**212°F**

---

### Kelvin Scale (K)

Scientific temperature scale.

Water freezes at:

**273 K**

Water boils at:

**373 K**

---

# Transfer of Heat

Heat always moves from a hotter body to a colder body.

There are **three methods** of heat transfer.

* Conduction
* Convection
* Radiation

---

# 1. Conduction

## Definition

Conduction is the transfer of heat through a substance **without the movement of the substance itself**.

It mainly occurs in solids.

---

### Example

A metal spoon placed in hot tea gradually becomes hot.

Heat travels through the spoon.

---

### Why Does This Happen?

Particles near the hot end vibrate faster.

They transfer energy to neighbouring particles.

---

# Conductors of Heat

Good conductors allow heat to pass easily.

Examples

* Copper
* Aluminium
* Silver
* Iron

---

# Insulators (Poor Conductors)

Poor conductors do not allow heat to pass easily.

Examples

* Wood
* Plastic
* Rubber
* Cork
* Air
* Wool

---

# Everyday Applications

Metal cooking utensils have:

* Metal body (good conductor)
* Plastic or wooden handles (poor conductors)

This prevents our hands from getting burnt.

---

# 2. Convection

## Definition

Convection is the transfer of heat by the movement of liquids or gases.

---

### Example

When water is heated:

* Hot water becomes lighter.
* It rises upward.
* Cold water moves downward.
* This creates **convection currents**.

---

### Everyday Examples

* Boiling water.
* Heating milk.
* Sea breeze.
* Land breeze.
* Hot air balloons.

---

# Sea Breeze

Occurs during the daytime.

* Land heats faster than water.
* Air above land becomes warm and rises.
* Cooler air from the sea moves towards land.

This cool wind is called **sea breeze**.

---

# Land Breeze

Occurs at night.

* Land cools faster than water.
* Air above the sea becomes warmer.
* Warm air rises.
* Cool air from land moves towards the sea.

This is called **land breeze**.

---

# 3. Radiation

## Definition

Radiation is the transfer of heat **without any material medium**.

It can occur through empty space.

---

### Example

Heat from the Sun reaches Earth through radiation.

Since space is nearly a vacuum, conduction and convection cannot occur there.

---

### Everyday Examples

* Feeling warmth near a fire.
* Heat from electric heaters.
* Heat from the Sun.

---

# Comparison of Heat Transfer Methods

| Conduction            | Convection        | Radiation                             |
| --------------------- | ----------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Mainly in solids      | Liquids and gases | No medium required                    |
| No movement of matter | Matter moves      | Heat travels as electromagnetic waves |
| Example: Metal spoon  | Boiling water     | Sun heating Earth                     |

---

# Expansion on Heating

Most substances expand when heated.

---

# Expansion of Solids

Example

Railway tracks have small gaps.

Reason

They expand during hot weather.

Without gaps, the tracks may bend.

---

# Expansion of Liquids

Liquids expand when heated.

Example

The mercury or coloured alcohol inside a thermometer rises when heated.

---

# Expansion of Gases

Gases expand the most.

Example

A hot-air balloon rises because heated air expands and becomes less dense.

---

# Contraction on Cooling

Most substances contract when cooled.

Example

Electric wires are slightly loose during summer but become tighter in winter.

---

# Special Property of Water

Water behaves differently between **0°C and 4°C**.

* Water becomes denser as it warms from 0°C to 4°C.
* It reaches its maximum density at **4°C**.
* Below 4°C, water expands as it cools further into ice.

This is why:

* Ice floats on water.
* Lakes freeze from the top, allowing aquatic life to survive below.

---

# Melting and Freezing

## Melting

Solid → Liquid

Example

Ice melts into water.

---

## Freezing

Liquid → Solid

Example

Water freezes into ice.

---

# Boiling

Liquid changes rapidly into gas throughout the liquid.

Water boils at:

**100°C**

(at normal atmospheric pressure)

---

# Evaporation

Slow conversion of liquid into gas from the surface.

Occurs at any temperature.

---

# Factors Affecting Evaporation

* Temperature
* Wind speed
* Surface area
* Humidity

Higher temperature and stronger winds increase evaporation.

---

# Cooling by Evaporation

Evaporation removes heat.

Examples

* Sweating cools the body.
* Water kept in an earthen pot becomes cool.
* Perfume feels cool when applied.

---

# Clothing and Heat

### Summer

Wear:

* Cotton clothes

Reason

Cotton absorbs sweat and allows quick evaporation.

---

### Winter

Wear:

* Woollen clothes

Reason

Wool traps air.

Air is a poor conductor of heat.

This reduces heat loss.

---

# Vacuum Flask (Thermos Flask)

A thermos flask keeps liquids hot or cold for a long time.

It reduces heat transfer by:

* Conduction
* Convection
* Radiation

---

# Fire Safety

If clothes catch fire:

* Do **not** run.
* Stop immediately.
* Drop to the ground.
* Roll over to smother the flames.
* Cover with a blanket if available.

---

# Common Misconceptions

❌ Heat and temperature are the same.

✔ Heat is energy; temperature measures hotness.

---

❌ Metals are always hot.

✔ Metals only **feel** colder or hotter because they conduct heat efficiently.

---

❌ The Sun heats Earth by conduction.

✔ Heat from the Sun reaches Earth mainly by **radiation**.

---

❌ Ice contains no heat.

✔ Ice contains thermal energy; it is simply at a lower temperature than warmer objects.

---

❌ Wool produces heat.

✔ Wool does not produce heat; it **reduces heat loss** by trapping air.

---

# Did You Know?

* The surface of the Sun is about **5,500°C**, while its core is much hotter.
* Deserts become very hot during the day and very cold at night because sand heats and cools quickly.
* A camel's thick fur helps protect it from both intense daytime heat and cold desert nights.
* Engineers leave expansion joints in bridges to allow them to expand and contract with temperature changes.

---

# Olympiad Facts

* Heat flows from a hotter body to a colder body.
* Temperature is measured with a thermometer.
* Normal human body temperature is **37°C**.
* Water freezes at **0°C** and boils at **100°C** (at normal atmospheric pressure).
* Conduction occurs mainly in solids.
* Convection occurs in liquids and gases.
* Radiation does not require a material medium.
* Metals are good conductors of heat; wood, plastic, rubber, and air are poor conductors.
* Ice floats because it is less dense than liquid water.
* Wool keeps us warm by trapping air, which is a poor conductor of heat.

---

# Quick Revision

* Heat is a form of energy that flows from hotter objects to colder ones.
* Temperature measures how hot or cold an object is.
* Heat is transferred by conduction, convection, and radiation.
* Metals are good conductors of heat, while materials like wood and wool are poor conductors.
* Most substances expand when heated and contract when cooled.
* Water shows unusual behaviour between 0°C and 4°C, allowing ice to float.
* Evaporation causes cooling and is influenced by temperature, wind speed, humidity, and surface area.
* Cotton clothes help keep us cool in summer, while woollen clothes help retain body heat in winter.
* A thermos flask minimizes heat transfer to keep liquids hot or cold for longer.

# Chapter 13: Air, Atmosphere, Weather and Climate

*"Air is invisible, but it is one of the most important natural resources for life on Earth."*

We cannot see air, yet it surrounds us all the time. Every living organism depends on air for survival. Plants need carbon dioxide for photosynthesis, animals and humans need oxygen for respiration, and moving air (wind) influences weather, climate, transportation, and even electricity generation.

The atmosphere acts like a protective blanket around the Earth. It keeps our planet warm, protects us from harmful solar radiation, and contains the gases essential for life.

This chapter explains the composition of air, properties of air, atmosphere, weather, climate, wind, humidity, clouds, rainfall, and related Olympiad concepts.

---

# What is Air?

## Definition

* **Air** is a mixture of gases that surrounds the Earth.

* Air is colourless, odourless, tasteless, and invisible.

Although we cannot see air, we can feel it when it moves as wind.

---

# Importance of Air

Air is essential because it:

* Provides oxygen for respiration.
* Supplies carbon dioxide for photosynthesis.
* Helps in burning.
* Carries sound.
* Enables birds and aircraft to fly.
* Helps in pollination and seed dispersal.
* Regulates Earth's temperature.
* Protects life from harmful solar radiation.

Without air, life on Earth would not exist.

---

# Composition of Air

Air is a **mixture** of several gases.

Approximate composition of dry air:

| Gas                   |  Percentage |
| --------------------- | ----------: |
| Nitrogen              |         78% |
| Oxygen                |         21% |
| Argon and other gases |  About 0.9% |
| Carbon dioxide        | About 0.04% |
| Water vapour          |    Variable |

### Olympiad Tip

Remember:

**78% Nitrogen → 21% Oxygen → 1% Other gases**

---

# Nitrogen

Nitrogen is the most abundant gas in the atmosphere.

### Functions

* Helps plants grow after being converted into usable compounds through the nitrogen cycle.
* Dilutes oxygen and reduces the risk of rapid combustion.
* Used in fertilizers after conversion into nitrates or ammonium compounds.

### Interesting Fact

Although the atmosphere contains a large amount of nitrogen, most plants cannot use nitrogen gas directly.

---

# Oxygen

Oxygen is the second most abundant gas.

### Functions

* Essential for respiration.
* Supports combustion.
* Dissolves in water for aquatic animals.

### Olympiad Fact

Oxygen itself does **not burn**, but it **supports burning**.

---

# Carbon Dioxide

Carbon dioxide is present in very small amounts.

### Functions

* Used by green plants during photosynthesis.
* Helps regulate Earth's temperature.
* Naturally released during respiration and combustion.

### Sources

* Respiration
* Burning fuels
* Forest fires
* Volcanoes

---

# Water Vapour

Water vapour is the gaseous form of water.

Its amount changes depending on weather and location.

### Importance

* Forms clouds.
* Produces rain.
* Influences humidity.
* Helps regulate climate.

---

# Dust and Smoke

Air also contains tiny solid particles.

Examples

* Dust
* Smoke
* Pollen
* Ash
* Microorganisms

Too many suspended particles reduce air quality.

---

# Properties of Air

Although invisible, air has many important properties.

---

## 1. Air Occupies Space

### Experiment

Place an empty glass upside down in water.

Water does not enter completely because air occupies space.

---

## 2. Air Has Mass

Air is made of particles and therefore has mass.

### Experiment

Inflate two balloons equally.

Burst one balloon.

The inflated balloon side becomes heavier.

---

## 3. Air Exerts Pressure

Air presses on everything around us.

This pressure is called **atmospheric pressure**.

Examples

* Drinking with a straw.
* Syringes.
* Suction cups.

---

## 4. Air Can Be Compressed

Unlike solids and liquids, gases can be compressed.

Examples

* Bicycle pump.
* Air compressor.
* Gas cylinders.

---

## 5. Air Expands on Heating

Warm air becomes lighter because it expands.

Examples

* Hot-air balloons.
* Sea breeze.

---

# Atmosphere

## Definition

The **atmosphere** is the thick blanket of gases surrounding the Earth.

Gravity keeps the atmosphere attached to the Earth.

Without the atmosphere:

* There would be no breathable air.
* Harmful ultraviolet rays would reach Earth directly.
* Meteoroids would strike the Earth's surface more frequently.
* Daytime and nighttime temperatures would vary extremely.

---

# Layers of the Atmosphere (Basic Awareness)

---

## Troposphere

Lowest layer.

* Weather occurs here.
* Clouds form here.
* Most air is present here.

---

## Stratosphere

Contains the **ozone layer**.

The ozone layer absorbs most harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun.

---

## Mesosphere

Many meteoroids burn up in this layer due to friction with the air.

---

## Thermosphere

Very thin air.

Some satellites orbit in or above this region.

---

## Exosphere

Outermost layer.

Gradually merges into outer space.

---

# Wind

## Definition

Wind is **moving air**.

Wind is produced because warm air rises and cooler air moves in to replace it.

---

# Uses of Wind

* Sailing boats.
* Flying kites.
* Generating electricity with wind turbines.

* Drying clothes.

**Wind Energy → Electrical Energy**

### Advantages

* Sustainable.

---

# Weather

Weather is the **day-to-day condition of the atmosphere** at a particular place and time.

---

* Save electricity.

---

# Ozone Layer

The ozone layer lies in the **stratosphere**.

### Functions

* Absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) rays.
* Protects humans from skin damage.
* Protects plants and animals.

---

# Greenhouse Effect (Basic Awareness)

Certain gases trap heat in the atmosphere.

These are called **greenhouse gases**.

Examples

* Carbon dioxide
* Methane
* Water vapour

A natural greenhouse effect keeps Earth warm enough for life, but excessive greenhouse gases can contribute to global warming.

---

# Common Misconceptions

✔ Air is mostly **nitrogen**.

---

❌ Oxygen burns.

✔ Oxygen supports burning but does not burn itself.

# Did You Know?

* A single large tree can help improve local air quality by absorbing carbon dioxide and releasing oxygen.

---

# Olympiad Facts

* Air is a mixture of gases.
* Nitrogen forms about **78%** of air.
* Oxygen forms about **21%** of air.
* Carbon dioxide is used in photosynthesis.

* The **troposphere** is the layer where weather occurs.

* Wind is moving air.
* Weather changes daily, while climate describes long-term patterns.

---

# Quick Revision

* Air is an invisible mixture of gases surrounding the Earth.
* Nitrogen and oxygen are the two major components of air.
* Air has mass, occupies space, exerts pressure, and can be compressed.

* Wind is moving air caused by differences in heating.

# Chapter 14: Water, Water Cycle, Soil, Rocks and Minerals

*"Water is the basis of life, while soil and rocks form the foundation of our planet."*

Water covers about **71% of the Earth's surface**, yet only a small fraction is available as fresh water for drinking and agriculture. Soil supports plant growth, while rocks and minerals provide the raw materials used in buildings, roads, industries, and everyday products.

Understanding water, soil, rocks, and minerals helps us appreciate natural resources and learn why they must be conserved. These topics are important in Class 6 Science Olympiads because they involve both scientific concepts and environmental awareness.

---

# Water

## Definition

* **Water** is a colourless, odourless, and tasteless liquid essential for all living organisms.

* It is one of the most important natural resources on Earth.

### Chemical Formula

**H₂O**

Each water molecule contains:

* Two hydrogen atoms
* One oxygen atom

---

# Importance of Water

Water is essential because it:

* Supports all forms of life.
* Helps plants make food.
* Regulates body temperature.
* Aids digestion.
* Transports nutrients in living organisms.
* Is used for cooking and cleaning.
* Is essential for agriculture.
* Is used in industries.
* Helps generate hydroelectric power.

Without water, life on Earth would not exist.

---

# Distribution of Water on Earth

| Type                         | Percentage |

Although Earth appears to have plenty of water, **only a very small percentage is easily available for human use**.

# The Water Cycle

* Sleet

Water collects in:

* Rivers
* Lakes
* Oceans
* Groundwater

The cycle then repeats.

* Sandy, clayey, and loamy soils have different properties and uses.
* Soil erosion can be reduced by planting trees and adopting proper farming methods.
* Rocks are classified as igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic.
* Minerals are valuable natural substances used in many industries.
* Fossils preserved in sedimentary rocks provide evidence of ancient life.
* Conserving water, soil, rocks, and minerals helps ensure these resources remain available for future generations.

# Chapter 15: The Solar System, Earth, Moon, Stars and the Universe

*"The universe is unimaginably vast, and our Earth is just one small planet orbiting an ordinary star called the Sun."*

For thousands of years, humans have looked up at the night sky with curiosity. Ancient civilizations observed the movements of the Sun, Moon, stars, and planets, while modern scientists use powerful telescopes and spacecraft to explore the universe.

Our Solar System consists of the Sun, eight planets, their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, comets, and countless smaller objects. Understanding these celestial bodies helps explain day and night, seasons, eclipses, tides, and the origin of our place in space.

This chapter concludes the Class 6 Science Olympiad syllabus and introduces important concepts in astronomy and space science.

# The Milky Way Galaxy

Our Solar System is part of the **Milky Way Galaxy**.

### Facts

* Contains hundreds of billions of stars.
* Appears as a bright band across the night sky in dark locations.
* Takes the Sun about **225–250 million years** to complete one orbit around its center.

# The Solar System

## Definition

The **Solar System** consists of the Sun and all objects that revolve around it under the influence of its gravity.

It includes:

* Eight planets
* Dwarf planets
* Moons
* Asteroids
* Comets
* Meteoroids

# The Sun

The Sun is the **centre of the Solar System**.

It is a **star**, not a planet.

### Importance

The Sun:

* Provides light.
* Provides heat.
* Drives the water cycle.
* Enables photosynthesis.
* Maintains Earth's climate.
* Holds the Solar System together through gravity.

---

### Composition

The Sun is mainly made of:

* Hydrogen
* Helium

Energy is produced by **nuclear fusion** in its core.

### Olympiad Facts

* The Sun contains about **99.8%** of the Solar System's mass.
* Light from the Sun takes about **8 minutes 20 seconds** to reach Earth.

# Planets

A **planet** is a large celestial body that:

* Revolves around the Sun.
* Has enough gravity to become nearly spherical.
* Does not produce its own light.

Planets shine because they **reflect sunlight**.

# The Moon

The Moon does not produce its own light.

It reflects sunlight.

### Facts

* Average distance from Earth: about **384,400 km**.
* Gravity is about **one-sixth** of Earth's.
* No air.
* No liquid water on the surface.

As the Moon revolves around Earth, different portions of its sunlit side become visible.

Main phases:

* New Moon
* Waxing Crescent
* First Quarter
* Waxing Gibbous
* Full Moon
* Waning Gibbous
* Last Quarter
* Waning Crescent

One complete cycle takes about **29.5 days**.

# Rotation and Revolution

---

## Rotation

Rotation is the spinning of a body on its own axis.

### Earth

* Rotation period: about **24 hours**.
* Causes **day and night**.

---

## Revolution

Revolution is the movement of a body around another body.

### Earth

* Revolves around the Sun in about **365¼ days**.
* Causes **seasons** (along with Earth's tilted axis).

---

# Leap Year

Since Earth takes about **365¼ days** to orbit the Sun, an extra day is added every four years.

This year is called a **leap year**, with **366 days**.

---

# Seasons

Earth experiences four main seasons because its axis is tilted by about **23.5°**.

* Spring
* Summer
* Autumn (Fall)
* Winter

Different parts of Earth receive different amounts of sunlight throughout the year.

---

# Eclipses

## Solar Eclipse

Occurs when:

**Moon → Earth → Sun** ❌ (Incorrect order)

Correct alignment:

**Sun → Moon → Earth**

The Moon blocks sunlight from reaching part of Earth.

---

## Lunar Eclipse

Correct alignment:

**Sun → Earth → Moon**

Earth's shadow falls on the Moon.

# Why Do Stars Twinkle?

Stars appear to twinkle because Earth's atmosphere bends (refracts) their light.

Planets usually do not twinkle because they appear as tiny discs rather than point sources.

# Common Misconceptions

❌ The Sun is a planet.

✔ The Sun is a **star**.

---

❌ The Moon produces its own light.

✔ The Moon reflects sunlight.

---

❌ Pluto is still one of the eight planets.

✔ Pluto is now classified as a **dwarf planet**.

---

❌ Stars twinkle because they are moving.

✔ Stars appear to twinkle due to Earth's atmosphere.

---

❌ The Earth's distance from the Sun causes the seasons.

✔ Seasons are mainly caused by the **tilt of Earth's axis**, not by changes in its distance from the Sun.
