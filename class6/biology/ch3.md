# INTRODUCTION

All the cells in a multicellular organism do not perform all the life processes. Some cells help the organism digest its food, while other cells help it to get oxygen from the atmosphere. Some cells help in transporting nutrients, waste and so on, while some cells help in excreting the waste. Some cells help in learning what is around the organism, while some cells help in reproduction.

Multicellular organisms have **organs** that carry out specific functions. Many organs together form an **organ system**. An organ system carries out a specific life process such as digestion, respiration, circulation and so on.

The human body has a number of organ systems. Some of the major ones are the digestive, respiratory, circulatory, skeletal and nervous systems.

Let us learn about the digestive system, respiratory system and the circulatory system in this lesson.

# The Digestive System

## NUTRITION AND NUTRIENTS

Think about how you have grown from a baby to the individual that you are today. This growth has been a direct result of the nutrition that your body gets from the food that you eat. Nutrition is the process by which the body obtains and uses food.

Food contains **nutrients**. A nutrient is a substance that the body needs to maintain itself and also to grow. The nutrients in food are useful in many ways.

- Some nutrients are used to get energy, which is used to carry out work and other processes.
- Some nutrients are used for the growth and repair of our body.
- Some nutrients are used to keep us healthy and disease-free.

The nutrients that our body needs can be broadly divided into five types.

- **Carbohydrates** are used to get energy. *Chaptis*, rice, noodles, potatoes and so on contain carbohydrates.
- **Proteins** are used for growth and repair of the body. Eggs, meat, pulses, milk, cheese and so on contain proteins.
- **Fats** are also used to get energy. They are used to keep the body warm. Oils, butter, ghee and so on contain fats.
- **Vitamins** are needed to be healthy. Fruits and vegetables contain vitamins.
- **Minerals** such as iron, calcium and phosphorus are also needed to keep the body healthy. Fruits and vegetables contain minerals too.

Carbohydrates, proteins and fats are called **macronutrients** as they are required in large quantities. Vitamins and minerals are called **micronutrients** as they are required in very small quantities.

Other than these five major nutrients, our body also needs water and **roughage** (fibre). Water is needed to transport substances within the body. Roughage keeps the digestive system working efficiently. Roughage is found only in foods that we get from plants.

A diet that has all the nutrients in the right required quantities is called a **balanced diet**. Make sure that your diet has all the nutrients your body needs to be healthy.

However, the food that you eat cannot be used as it is by your body. It has to be broken down into simpler substances which your cells can use. This breaking down of food materials is done by the digestive system.

### Know more

Carbohydrates are made of many sugar molecules linked together. Proteins are made of many amino acids linked together. Similarly, fats are made of many molecules of fatty acids and glycerol linked together.

# THE HUMAN DIGESTIVE SYSTEM

The digestive system essentially is a single muscular tube that starts at the mouth and ends at the anus. All the food that you eat passes through this tube, which has a different structure in different parts according to the function of the part.

The digestive system consists of two parts.

- The **alimentary canal** consists of the mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, rectum and anus.
- The **associated glands** consist of salivary glands, pancreas and liver that are connected to the alimentary canal.

The stomach, small intestine and associated glands secrete **enzymes**. Enzymes are substances that help chemical reactions to take place in the body. They help to break down nutrients into simple substances that the cells of the body can use.

The entire process of nutrition can be divided into five main steps.

1.  **Ingestion** (through the mouth)
2.  **Digestion** (in the mouth, stomach and small intestine)
3.  **Absorption** (small intestine and large intestine)
4.  **Assimilation** (into the cells)
5.  **Egestion** (through the anus)

## The Mouth

The process of taking food into the body is called ingestion. Food is ingested through the mouth. The mouth has the lips, gums, teeth and tongue. The teeth break down the food into smaller particles when you bite or chew. The tongue helps in swallowing the chewed food.

Digestion is the process by which complex food is broken down into simple substances. Two kinds of digestion occur in the mouth:

- Mechanical digestion by the teeth
- Chemical digestion by saliva

# The Teeth

Mechanical digestion is carried out by the teeth, which crush food into smaller food particles. We have different types of teeth to make this process more efficient.

For ease of understanding, let us discuss the teeth in the upper jaw of an adult.

**Incisors** These teeth are found in the centre of the jaw. There are four incisors in a jaw. The incisors are flat and have a sharp edge like a chisel. They are used to cut and bite food.

**Canines** These teeth are present next to the incisors. There are two canines in each jaw. The canines have a pointed edge. They are used to grip and tear food such as meat.

**Premolars** These teeth are found next to the canines. There are two premolars after each canine, and thus there are four premolars in each jaw. The premolars have a flat surface. They are used to crush and grind food.

**Molars** These teeth are found after the premolars. The last molars are called the **wisdom teeth**, and they appear only around the age of 18. Molars also have a flat surface and are used to crush and grind food.

An adult human thus has 32 teeth.

However, in some adults, one or more of the wisdom teeth do not appear. So, the number of teeth in an adult human may vary.

### The salivary glands

The chemical digestion of food is done with the help of the salivary glands. This process begins in the mouth.

You have three pairs of salivary glands—one under the tongue, one at the base of the ear and one at the base of the jaw.

# Spotlight

## The structure of a tooth
A tooth is divided into the crown (the part seen above the gum) and the root (the part within the gum).
- The outermost layer of the crown is called the enamel. This is the hardest substance in the body.
- Below the enamel is the dentine, which forms the bulk of the tooth.
- Within the dentine is the pulp, which has nerves and blood vessels.

## How are teeth damaged?
As we chew food, small food particles get stuck in the gaps between the teeth and remain there. Bacteria and these leftover pieces of food form a soft, sticky substance called plaque. The plaque needs to be removed from the teeth regularly by brushing. If it is not removed, the bacteria convert the food into acid. This acid corrodes or damages the enamel of the teeth, leading to cavities or caries.

As the acid continues to eat away the tooth, the caries eventually reach the pulp. This results in severe pain, as the pulp has nerves in it. If the caries progresses too far, the tooth may need to be extracted.

## Care of teeth
You should follow some steps to take good care of the teeth.
- When you sleep, saliva does not move much in the mouth and thus cannot clean it. This allows bacteria to grow and thus damage the teeth. So, brush your teeth well both in the morning and at night before you go to bed.
- Make sure that all surfaces of the teeth are cleaned while brushing.
- Do not eat or drink foods that contain large amounts of sugar.
- Always rinse your mouth well after every meal as this helps to remove most of the food that is stuck between the teeth.

The salivary glands secrete **saliva**, which is a mixture of water and an enzyme called **amylase**.

Saliva has many functions.

- The amylase helps in the digestion of starch. It breaks down starch into **maltose** (a kind of sugar).
- It helps to bind food together to form a mass called the **bolus**, which is easy to swallow.
- It helps clean the mouth and teeth.
- It helps to keep the mouth moist for easier swallowing and speaking.

### Know more

There are many kinds of sugar. Glucose, fructose (found in fruits), galactose, sucrose, lactose (found in milk) and maltose are some kinds of sugar.

### The tongue

The tongue is a muscular organ that is attached to the back of the floor of the mouth. The tongue has many functions.

- It moves the food around the mouth so that the food is chewed well.
- It helps to **taste** food with the help of **taste buds** found on its surface.
- It helps to mix food with saliva.
- It helps to clean the mouth by helping to remove food particles stuck between teeth.
- It helps in speaking.

### Spotlight

It was earlier thought that the tongue had specific zones for each taste (**sweet**, **sour**, **salty** or **bitter**). But this has now been proved to be wrong. All the tastes can be tasted on all the parts of the tongue. (Now, a fifth taste called **umami** is recognised!)

However, the edges of the tongue are more sensitive to tastes than the middle of the tongue. Also, the back of the tongue is more sensitive to bitter tastes. This may be because it will make us spit out poisons or spoiled food before they are swallowed.

# The Oesophagus

The oesophagus is also called the **food pipe**. It is a long, muscular tube that connects the mouth to the stomach. No digestion occurs in the oesophagus.
The muscles of the oesophagus contract and relax rhythmically to push the food (bolus) down to the stomach. This contraction and relaxation is called **peristalsis**. Peristalsis is also seen in other parts of the alimentary canal.

# The Stomach

The stomach is a bag-like muscular organ, located in the left upper part of the abdomen. The wall of the stomach is made of muscles, which help to mix the food and churn it to form **chyme**.

The walls of the stomach have glands that secrete **gastric juice**. The gastric juice consists of the following:

- The enzyme **pepsin** breaks down proteins.
- Hydrochloric acid activates pepsin and also kills microorganisms.
- Water acts as a medium for the action of the acid and the enzymes.

The presence of acid in the stomach inactivates salivary amylase and thus carbohydrates are not digested here. A certain amount of fat is digested in the stomach by a type of enzyme called lipase.

Depending on the food eaten, chyme stays in the stomach for 1.5–3 hours. The chyme is then sent to the small intestine for further digestion and absorption.

### Know more

The stomach wall also has a layer of mucus. This protects the stomach wall from being damaged by the action of pepsin and hydrochloric acid.

# The Small Intestine

The small intestine is a long, coiled tube that is about 6–7 m long. Both digestion and absorption of food take place here. The small intestine consists of three parts:

## Duodenum

This is the first part of the small intestine. Digestive juices from the liver (**bile**) and the pancreas (pancreatic juice) are secreted into the duodenum.

**The liver** The liver is a reddish brown, triangular organ that lies just below the diaphragm on the right side of the body. It secretes green-coloured bile. Bile is stored in the gall bladder and released into the duodenum when needed.

Bile helps to break down large fat droplets to smaller ones. This helps the fat-digesting enzyme lipase to work faster and easier on them. The breakdown of large fat droplets into smaller droplets is called emulsification.

**Fig. 3.6 The liver and the pancreas**

**The pancreas** The pancreas is a small leaf-shaped organ sandwiched between the stomach and the folds of the intestine. The pancreatic juice contains the enzymes amylase, trypsin and lipase. These enzymes break down starch, proteins and fat, respectively.

- **starch** → amylase → maltose
- **proteins** → trypsin → peptides
- **fats** → lipase → fatty acids + glycerol

**Ducts (thin passages)** from the gall bladder and pancreas join together and empty into the duodenum.

## Jejunum

This is the second part of the small intestine. The absorption of most of the digested food happens here.

## Ileum

This is the third part of the small intestine. This is where the remaining food materials are digested and absorbed. Enzymes like **erepsin**, **maltase**, **sucrase** and **lactase** are secreted by the wall of the small intestine.

- **peptides** → erepsin → amino acids
- **maltase** → maltose → glucose
- **sucrase** → sucrose → glucose + fructose
- **lactase** → lactose → glucose + galactose

Thus, by the time the chyme reaches the end of the small intestine, all the nutrients would have been broken down to their simplest substances.

The inner surface of the small intestine is folded into many finger-like projections called **villi**, which are in turn folded to form microvilli.

**Fig. 3.7 Villi and microvilli in the small intestine**

Villi help to increase the area over which absorption of digested food takes place.

The villi are richly supplied with blood vessels. This helps them absorb nutrients into the blood stream. The nutrients are then transported through the blood to all the cells in the body.

### The Large Intestine

The large intestine is about 1.5 m long and is divided into three parts—the caecum, colon and rectum. Undigested food moves slowly through the large intestine; most of the water is absorbed to leave behind the semi-solid product called **faeces**.

The faeces are stored in the **rectum** till the next **bowel movement**. The rectum opens to the outside through the **anus**. The anus has a circular muscle called the **anal sphincter** which keeps the anus closed. When this muscle relaxes, the faeces are expelled out of the body. The process by which undigested food is expelled from the body is called **egestion**.

### ASSIMILATION OF NUTRIENTS

The glucose, amino acids and fatty acids that are absorbed in the digestive system are used for many functions.

- The glucose is used by cells in the body to get energy for their activities. Some glucose is stored in the liver.
- The amino acids are used for repair and to build up parts like muscles.
- Fatty acids and glycerol are used as energy reserves and insulation.

# The Respiratory System

Every organism on Earth uses energy for all of its activities. Plants make their own food and then break it down to get energy. Animals including humans get energy for their activities from glucose.

The process by which glucose is broken down to release energy is **respiration**. Generally, respiration occurs in the presence of oxygen. (Respiration can also occur in the absence of oxygen.)

Let us first see how the body gets oxygen from the air.

## THE HUMAN RESPIRATORY SYSTEM

The human respiratory system consists of the nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi and lungs.

### The Nose

The nose has two openings, called **nostrils**, through which air enters the body. The nostrils lead into **nasal chambers**.

- **The nasal passages** are lined with hairs. The hairs act as a filter and trap dust and germs that enter with the air.
- Special cells in the nasal passages secrete **mucus** to trap dust and germs.
- The nasal passages also have cells that help you to detect smells.
- The nasal passages also help to warm and moisten the air that enters the body.

### Know more

When the lining of your nasal chambers are irritated due to dust in the inhaled air, you sneeze to expel the offending dust particles.

### The Pharynx

The pharynx is the part that is just behind the nose and mouth.

It is the common passage for both air and food. The pharynx leads into the larynx.

# The Larynx

The entrance of the larynx is protected by a flap-like structure called the **epiglottis**.
The epiglottis covers the entrance of the larynx when you swallow food and thus prevents food from entering the lungs. If the epiglottis has not closed properly and food enters the larynx, you cough to expel the food.

The larynx is also called the **voice box**. Two thin membranes called **vocal chords** are stretched across the larynx like curtains. You produce sounds by making air pass between the tightly stretched vocal chords in the voice box.

# The Trachea

The trachea or the **windpipe**, is the tubular part after the larynx. It runs right in front of the oesophagus. The trachea has special cells with tiny hair-like structures which help to trap and push out dust or germs.

The walls of the trachea have C-shaped rings made of **cartilage** that prevent the trachea from collapsing.

# The Bronchi

The trachea divides into two **bronchi** (singular: bronchus) inside the chest. Each bronchus enters a lung and further divides into smaller tubes called **bronchioles**.

# The Lungs

You have a pair of lungs which are protected from injury by the ribcage.

The left lung is slightly smaller than the right lung to make space for the heart in the chest. The left lung has only two lobes while the right lung has three lobes.

The lungs are covered by the **pleura**, which is made of two membranes. The space between the membranes is filled with a fluid that helps to cushion and protect the lungs.

Just below the lungs is a thick sheet of muscle called the **diaphragm**. The diaphragm separates the chest cavity from the abdominal cavity. The contraction and relaxation of the diaphragm helps us breathe.

# The alveoli

The bronchioles divide repeatedly till they end in microscopic, sac-like structures called **alveoli** (singular: **alveolus**). Millions of alveoli make up the lungs.

The alveoli have thin walls and are richly supplied with capillaries. Thus, gas exchange between the blood and the inhaled air can take place easily. A group of alveoli form an **alveolar sac**.

# FROM BREATHING TO RESPIRATION

The entire process of taking in air is just the starting point for respiration. The complete process has three steps—breathing, gas exchange and respiration.

## Breathing

Breathing (also called external respiration) is carried out by the respiratory system. It consists of two steps—**inhalation** and **exhalation**.

Inhalation is the action of breathing in air through the nostrils. The inhaled air passes through the organs of the respiratory system to reach the alveoli.

Exhalation is the action of breathing out air through the nostrils. The air from the alveoli passes through the organs of the respiratory system back to the outside.

| The volume of the chest cavity and lung increases. | The volume of the chest cavity and lung decreases. |
| The air pressure inside the lungs drops compared to the outside pressure and air from the outside rushes in. | The air pressure inside the lungs rises compared to the outside pressure and air rushes to the outside. |

Breathing is controlled by the brain and so is **involuntary** (You cannot control it.). However, you can train yourself to hold your breath for a while. You can also breathe faster or slower, but this is only for a short while.

# Gas Exchange

The oxygen in the inhaled air passes into the capillaries that surround the alveoli through **diffusion** (Fig. 3.15).

Diffusion is the process by which a substance moves from a place where it is present in a higher concentration to another place where it is present in a lower concentration.

The inhaled air has around 21% oxygen, while the blood in the capillaries will have a lower amount. Thus, oxygen in the inhaled air moves across the membrane of the alveoli into the capillaries.

The oxygen in the capillaries binds to haemoglobin present in the red blood cells to form oxyhaemoglobin. Thus, oxygen is carried to all parts of the body through the blood.

Carbon dioxide is picked up from the rest of the body and brought through the blood to the lungs. The carbon dioxide in the blood diffuses into the alveoli since its concentration in the inhaled air is only 0.04%, while it is higher in the blood. The air in the alveoli is now rich in carbon dioxide and is exhaled.

## Respiration

**Respiration** is the breakdown of food to release energy. Since this process happens within cells, it is also called cellular respiration or internal respiration.

- When respiration occurs in the presence of oxygen, it is called aerobic respiration.
- When respiration occurs in the absence of oxygen, it is called anaerobic respiration.

During aerobic respiration, glucose reacts with oxygen to release carbon dioxide, water and energy. This process is also called oxidation.

glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy

You will study about anaerobic respiration in a higher class.

## COMMON RESPIRATORY DISEASES

### Asthma
This disease is caused when substances irritate the respiratory passages. The airways become swollen and prevent easy flow of air through them. This makes breathing difficult. The person feels a tightness in the chest, shortness of breath or wheezing.

**Treatment** Medicines that open up the airways, called bronchodilators, are used to treat asthma. These medicines are given directly into the air passages using an inhaler or a puff.

### Bronchitis
This disease is caused mostly by bacteria or viruses. It can sometimes be caused by pollutants such as smoke. There is swelling of the air passages. The person may have coughing and difficulty breathing.

**Treatment** If the bronchitis is caused by bacteria, it is treated with antibiotics. If it is caused by a pollutant, bronchodilators may be used. Rest and drinking plenty of fluids are recommended.

### Pneumonia
This disease is caused by bacteria, viruses or fungi. The alveoli become swollen and filled with fluid. The patient has a high fever, chills and cough with mucus. Pneumonia may be fatal unless treated.

**Treatment** Pneumonia is treated with antibiotics, rest and plenty of fluids.

### Tuberculosis (TB)
This disease is caused by a particular kind of bacterium. The lungs become swollen and start bleeding. The patient has a fever, night sweats, chest pain and sudden weight loss. The patient also has a continuous cough that brings up blood.

**Treatment** Tuberculosis is treated with antibiotics, which may need to be taken for up to six months without a break. Tuberculosis can be prevented by taking the BCG vaccine.

### Eco corner
Many of the respiratory diseases in humans are either caused or made worse by the pollutants in the air. Therefore, we have to stop polluting the atmosphere to prevent some of these diseases.

# The Circulatory System

You have learnt how the digestive system breaks down the food that you eat into glucose, amino acids, fatty acids and glycerol so that they can be used by the body. You have also learnt how glucose is oxidised in cells to release energy. But how do the glucose and oxygen travel from the small intestine and the lungs to the cells?

Your body has its own transport system that helps to move nutrients and waste within the body. This transport system is called the **circulatory system**.

## THE HUMAN CIRCULATORY SYSTEM

The human circulatory system consists of blood, the blood vessels and the heart.

### Blood

Blood consists of two parts:

- A liquid called **plasma**
- Cells called **corpuscles**

#### Plasma

Plasma is a straw-coloured (pale yellow) liquid that is mostly (90%) water with substances dissolved in it. It distributes nutrients to every part of the body, and also collects waste from every part of the body.

### The corpuscles

There are three kinds of corpuscles in human blood.

**Red blood corpuscles (RBCs)** These cells are also called **erythrocytes** and carry oxygen. They are shaped like biconcave discs (depressed in the centre) and do not have a nucleus. They are red due to the iron-containing haemoglobin. Iron helps haemoglobin carry oxygen.

**White blood corpuscles (WBCs)** These cells are also called **leukocytes**. They are more or less round in shape, although some of them can change shape. They are larger than the RBCs, are colourless and have a nucleus. The main function of the WBCs is to protect the body by:
- engulfing germs and digesting them.
- producing special proteins called **antibodies** which destroy germs.

**Platelets** They are also called **thrombocytes** and do not have a nucleus. They play an important role in the clotting of blood. A **blood clot** is formed at the site.

**Fig. 3.16 The blood corpuscles**

## Functions of blood

Blood has three main functions.

### Transport
- It transports oxygen from the lungs to all the cells in the body, and carbon dioxide from every cell in the body to the lungs.
- It carries waste products to the kidneys so that they can be excreted.
- It transports the nutrients that have been absorbed in the intestine to the rest of the body, where they may either be utilised or stored.

### Protection
- It helps to kill disease-causing germs that enter the body.
- It helps in the formation of blood clots and prevents excessive bleeding.

### Regulation
- It helps to regulate the temperature of the body by distributing heat produced during chemical reactions throughout the body.

# The Blood Vessels

Blood is carried to all parts of the body through long tubes called blood vessels. There are three types of blood vessels.

### Arteries
Arteries are blood vessels that carry blood away from the heart. They have thick, muscular, elastic walls. This helps them to withstand the force of the blood flowing through them. They are usually found deep within the body.

### Capillaries
As an artery moves away from the heart, it branches into arterioles which further divide into very small blood vessels called capillaries. Capillaries have thin walls made of one layer of cells. This thin wall allows them to exchange gases, nutrients and waste easily between the blood and the cells.

### Veins
The capillaries, after the exchange of gases, nutrients and waste is completed, join together to form venules which then join to form veins. Veins carry blood back to the heart. They have thin walls and are less muscular than arteries. Veins have valves that prevent blood from flowing backwards.

# The Heart

The heart is a fist-sized, muscular organ found in the centre of the chest cavity, between the lungs. It is tilted slightly to the left. The main function of the heart is to pump blood for circulation throughout the body. It is made of a special kind of muscle, called **cardiac muscle** which can contract and relax without any rest.

## Internal structure of the heart

The heart is divided into the left and right sides by a muscular wall called the septum.
Each side is further divided into an atrium or **auricle** (found at the top), and a ventricle (found at the bottom). Thus, the heart has four chambers—two atria and two ventricles.
The atria are smaller and less muscular than the ventricles. Also, the right side of the heart is less muscular than the left side.

A valve is present at the opening of each atrium into the ventricle of the same side. This valve is called **atrioventricular valve** or **AV valve**. The AV valves let blood flow only from the atrium to the ventricle.

## Blood vessels attached to the heart

Three kinds of veins bring blood to the heart:
- The **superior vena cava** brings deoxygenated blood (rich in carbon dioxide and poor in oxygen content) from the upper body. It empties into the right atrium.
- The **inferior vena cava** brings deoxygenated blood from the lower body. It empties into the right atrium.
- **The pulmonary veins bring oxygenated blood** (to which oxygen has been added) from the lungs to the heart. They empty into the left atrium.

Two arteries take blood away from the heart. Valves are also present at the point where these arteries leave the ventricles.
- **The pulmonary arteries carry deoxygenated blood** from the right ventricle to the lungs.
- The aorta carries oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the rest of the body.

### Blood circulation through the heart

1.  Deoxygenated blood from the body enters the right atrium through the superior vena cava and the inferior vena cava.
2.  The right atrium contracts and pushes the blood into the right ventricle.
3.  The right ventricle contracts and pushes the blood into the pulmonary artery.
4.  The pulmonary artery carries the blood to the lungs.
5.  The blood is oxygenated in the lungs and is carried to the left atrium by the pulmonary veins.
6.  The left atrium contracts and pushes the blood into the left ventricle.
7.  The left ventricle contracts and pushes the blood into the aorta, which distributes the blood to the rest of the body.

Both atria contract at the same time, followed by both ventricles. Notice that blood flows through the heart twice during one complete cycle. This is why the process is called double circulation. The circulation of blood between the lungs and the heart is called pulmonary circulation. The circulation of blood in the entire body is called systemic circulation.

### Heartbeat and pulse

One cycle of contraction and relaxation of the heart is called a heartbeat.
The adult human heart beats around 70 times a minute. The heart beats faster during exercise and slower during rest. The heart also beats faster in children and slows down as you become older.

The heartbeat can be heard by using a stethoscope. This instrument makes the sound of the heartbeat louder and thus makes it easier to hear.

As the heart pushes blood into the aorta, the arteries expand and contract. This rhythmic change can be felt by placing the fingers at certain points in the body and is called the pulse. The pulse rate and the heart rate are the same.

## Blood Groups

Blood can be classified into four types based on the presence of a specific protein (protein A or protein B) on the RBCs.

A person with protein A on the RBCs is said to have ‘A’ blood group and a person with protein B is said to have ‘B’ blood group. A person with ‘AB’ group has both proteins on their RBCs, and a person with ‘O’ group has neither protein A nor protein B on their RBCs.

# Blood transfusion

If a person loses a considerable amount of blood due to an accident or during a surgery, blood from another person is injected into the patient. This is called **blood transfusion**.

During transfusion, blood of the correct group has to be transfused. If blood of the incorrect group is transfused, the blood may clot and this may be fatal.

- A person with the A blood group can accept blood of type A or O.
- A person with the B blood group can accept blood of type B or O.
- A person with the AB blood group can accept blood of any type and is thus called a universal recipient.
- A person with the O blood group can accept only blood of type O, but can donate blood to anyone. Thus, this person is a universal donor.

A **blood bank** is a place where donated blood is stored. The donated blood is used when needed.

## Blood pressure

As the heart contracts and relaxes, blood is pushed through the blood vessels. This causes the blood to exert a force on the walls of the blood vessels. The force applied by blood on the walls of the blood vessels is called **blood pressure**. It is measured using a **sphygmomanometer** and is expressed as millimetres of mercury (mm Hg).

### SciTech

The normal blood pressure of a healthy person is 120/80 to 140/90 mm Hg. A higher blood pressure than normal is called **hypertension** and a lower blood pressure than normal is called **hypotension**.

## MAINTAINING A HEALTHY HEART

Follow these practices to take good care of your heart.

- Eat a balanced diet. Eat plenty of fruits and vegetables.
- Do not eat oil-rich, sugar-rich or junk food frequently. It could lead to obesity.
- Exercise regularly.
- Do not eat too much salt. It could lead to hypertension over time.
