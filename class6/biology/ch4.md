# INTRODUCTION

The World Health Organization defines **health** as a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.¹ Thus, there are three states that define health.

## Physical health
We are said to be physically healthy when our bodies are free from diseases, and our organs function properly. A physically healthy person has healthy skin, hair and nails; an appropriate weight; good appetite; and correct balance and movement of limbs.

## Mental health
Mental health affects how we think, act, feel and so on. It also determines how we handle stress and take decisions.

## Social health
Social health is related to how we interact with people and how people react to us. A socially healthy person has a good relationship with their family and the people around them.

# WHAT IS A DISEASE?

Any change from a normal state that causes discomfort is called a **disease**. The origin of the word disease can be traced back to old French, where des means ‘lack of’, and aise means ‘comfort’.

Disease can be due to many reasons. Some of them are:
- germs or **microorganisms**
- organs not functioning properly
- an unbalanced diet
- a polluted environment
- **allergies**
- use of tobacco, drugs or alcohol

# TYPES OF DISEASES

Diseases can be divided broadly into two categories.
- **Congenital diseases** are present in the body from birth. You will learn more about them in higher classes.
- **Acquired diseases** develop or are observed after birth. They are further divided into two types.
  - **Communicable diseases** can spread from one person to another.
  - **Non-communicable diseases** do not spread from person to person.

# COMMUNICABLE DISEASES

Communicable diseases (also called **infectious diseases** or **contagious diseases**) are caused by germs or microorganisms such as bacteria, viruses, fungi and protozoa. **Disease-causing microorganisms** are called **pathogens**. (In Greek, pathos means ‘illness’, and gen means ‘causing’.)

# Mode of Transmission

Communicable diseases can be transmitted from person to person in many ways.
- They can spread by **direct contact with an infected person**.
- They can spread when you share **items** such as toothbrushes, towels, handkerchiefs, utensils, razors and so on with an infected person.
- They can spread through the air when you breathe in **droplets** that have been sneezed or coughed by an infected person (examples: cold, flu, measles, chickenpox).
- They can spread when you eat **contaminated food** or drink **contaminated liquids** (examples: typhoid, cholera, dysentery).
- They can also spread through the bites of insects such as mosquitoes.

- **Pathogens**
  - **Viruses**: They are extremely small organisms that can multiply only inside a living cell. The common cold, influenza (flu), measles, mumps, COVID-19, dengue, chickenpox, viral jaundice, rabies, AIDS and polio are all caused by viruses.
  - **Know more**: Smallpox was a dreaded viral disease. It is believed to have killed around four lakh people every year. Smallpox has now been eradicated (destroyed completely) successfully. Polio is another disease that has caused the deaths of lakhs of people. India is following the Pulse Polio campaign, which is vaccinating lakhs of children every year.
  - **Bacteria**: They are unicellular organisms that are less complex than the cells in our body. Typhoid, cholera, pneumonia, tetanus, tuberculosis, leprosy and diphtheria are all caused by bacteria.
  - **Protozoa** (singular: protozoan): They are unicellular organisms that are slightly more complex than bacteria. Malaria and amoebiasis are caused by protozoa.
  - **Fungi**: They are multicellular organisms. (Mushrooms and yeasts are kinds of fungi.) Ringworm and athlete’s foot are caused by fungi.
  - **Worms**: Sometimes, worms can enter our body through contaminated food and water. Many of these worms live in the digestive system. Taeniasis, filariasis and ascariasis are examples of diseases caused by worms.

# Prevention of Communicable Diseases

Take the following steps to avoid infections.

## Food habits
- Eat clean and healthy food. Food that is cooked at home may be cleaner than the food that you get outside.
- Wash fruits and vegetables thoroughly with clean water to remove harmful substances and germs.
- Use only clean water for drinking and for cooking food. Water can be purified at home by boiling or by using water purifiers and water filters.
- Store both cooked and uncooked food in a refrigerator or freezer to prevent the spoilage of food.

## The environment
- Fix screens on windows and doors to keep mosquitoes, flies and other insects out of the house.
- Throw garbage in the garbage bin, and keep the bin covered. Otherwise, flies and other insects will breed there.

## Personal hygiene
**Hygiene** is the set of practices that help to maintain health and prevent diseases.
- Take a bath regularly with soap to keep your body clean and free from germs.
- Wash your hands well with soap before and after eating.
- Wash your hands well with soap and water after using the bathroom.
- Trim the nails on your fingers and toes regularly. Do not bite your nails.
- Wash your hair regularly with shampoo to keep the hair and scalp free from dirt and germs. Do not share combs and hairbrushes with others.
- Brush your teeth at least twice a day and definitely before you go to bed. This keeps the teeth and gums healthy.
- Always use clean cloth or tissues to wipe your face.
- Cover your mouth with a handkerchief when you sneeze or cough.

# NON-COMMUNICABLE DISEASES

These diseases have many causes.

## Genetic Causes
Damage or harm to the chromosomes in the cells causes some diseases. **Albinism, haemophilia** and **colour blindness** are some examples of these diseases.

**Know more**: In haemophilia, the blood does not clot properly when needed. So this is a life-threatening disease. People with colour blindness cannot differentiate between certain colours (for example, red and green). People with albinism are very pale, with pale hair too.

## Physical or Chemical Exposure
Prolonged exposure to heat and sunlight leads to burns, **sunburn** and **heat stroke**. Exposure to cold leads to **frostbite**. The body can also be injured on exposure to electricity (electric shock), sudden change in pressure from high to low (**decompression sickness**) and **radiation**. The body can also be injured because of chemicals such as lead, mercury, alcohol and carbon monoxide.

## Defects of the Immune System
The immune system of the body protects the body against germs. Sometimes, the immune system reacts excessively when a foreign substance enters it. An excessive reaction of the body to a usually harmless substance is called an **allergy**. This substance that causes the allergy is called the **allergen**. Some common allergens are dust, pollen, some chemicals in food, nuts, eggs, bee venom, mushrooms, shellfish and so on. The skin, respiratory passages and alimentary canal are usually affected. Allergies that involve the respiratory passages are dangerous. Rashes, breathlessness, diarrhoea and vomiting are some common symptoms of allergies.

## Abnormal Growth of Cells
Sometimes, a cell in the body may start to divide uncontrollably, leading to the production of a mass of cells. This is called **cancer**. Cancer may be caused due to genetic causes, the environment, certain chemicals, certain kinds of light (for example, **ultraviolet light** in sunlight) or certain viruses.

## Defect in a Process
Sometimes, diseases are caused when a chemical process in the body does not happen normally. This could be because of a defect in a **hormone**. A hormone is a substance used to control certain parts of the body. **Diabetes** is caused by the underproduction of the hormone **insulin**.

## Abnormal Nutrition
Diseases may be caused by the deficiency or increased intake of nutrients.

**Deficiency of proteins and/or carbohydrates**: A deficiency of proteins in the diet leads to a reduction in muscle size and loss of body weight. **Kwashiorkor** is caused by a severe deficiency of proteins. **Marasmus** is caused by a severe deficiency of both proteins and carbohydrates.

**Deficiency of vitamins and minerals**: Sometimes a person’s diet may be deficient in vitamins and/or minerals. Even though vitamins and minerals are required only in very small amounts (they are micronutrients), their lack leads to deficiency diseases such as night blindness, beriberi and so on. *(See Table 4.2)*

**Excess intake of nutrients**: When we eat more food than is needed, it may lead to a condition called **obesity**. Obesity may lead to high blood pressure and diabetes. Excess intake of vitamins and minerals can also lead to diseases.

## Mental Causes
Some diseases are caused by our mental state too. **Depression** (where a person is abnormally sad for a long time) is an example of such a condition.

## Age-related Causes
People may get some diseases as they grow older. **Cataract** (the lens in the eye losing its transparency), **arthritis** (pain in the joints), **Alzheimer’s disease** (mental deterioration) and **Parkinson’s disease** (where the brain is not able to control the body properly) are some examples of these diseases.

# Prevention of Non-communicable Diseases

Some non-communicable diseases (related to nutrient intake and exposure to substances) can be prevented by taking appropriate measures.
- Follow a nutritious diet.
- Do not eat fast foods like burgers and pizza regularly. They will make you unhealthy.
- Do physical exercises such as playing sports or doing yoga. This keeps your body working correctly.
- Sleep around 7–8 hours every night. This helps the body repair itself.
- If you have trouble seeing things, visit a doctor (optician) to check if you need to use spectacles.
- Keep away from substances like tobacco, alcohol and drugs. They are harmful and addictive.
