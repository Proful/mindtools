# INTRODUCTION

Everything that we see around us is made of building blocks. Nonliving things such as buildings are made of bricks. Similarly, all living things are made of building blocks called **cells**.

A cell is the smallest living structure that performs all life processes such as ingestion, digestion, exchange of gases, excretion and so on. This is why the cell is called the **basic unit of life**. Some cells can live independently, while some are put together to form larger organisms. The male gamete and the female gamete that we studied in the previous lesson are individual cells.

# MICROSCOPES

**Microscopes** are the most important instruments that have helped us to discover and study cells. Let us now learn a little about microscopes. A microscope is an instrument that is used to view small objects by making them appear larger.

Microscopes have pieces of glass with curved surfaces called **lenses** that help them to make objects appear larger.

### Spotlight
A lens is a piece of glass or some other transparent material. It has one or two curved surfaces. Lenses are used in spectacles, cameras, magnifying glasses, microscopes and telescopes.

**Fig. 2.2 A lens**

Two Dutch spectacle makers, Zacharias Janssen and his father, Hans Janssen, are believed to be the inventors of the microscope.

### Types of Microscopes
The **simple microscope** uses only one lens and works like a magnifying glass.

The **compound microscope** uses two lenses and gives a more magnified image than the simple microscope does. Compound microscopes are used in school laboratories.

Nowadays, a third kind of microscope is also used. It is called the **electron microscope**. It is large and complex. It is used in large laboratories and can be used to study extremely small objects.

# THE DISCOVERY OF THE CELL

The discovery of the cell took a long time to be believed since most scientists did not have the tools to study life like we do today. Let us learn about some of the great scientists who discovered and studied cells.

**Robert Hooke** He was the first person to observe and coin the term cells. He placed a thin slice of cork (the lightweight bark of the cork oak tree) under a compound microscope and observed it. He saw box-like structures, which reminded him of the rooms (called 'cells') of monks in monasteries. So, he called the box-like structures, cells.

**Antonie van Leeuwenhoek** He made and used hundreds of simple microscopes. He described a tremendous variety of cells, including bacteria, yeast, red blood cells, muscle cells, sperms cells and so on.

### Cell Theory
Theodor Schwann, Matthias Schleiden and Rudolf Virchow proposed the cell theory of life, which says the following.
- All living things are made of cells.
- A cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living things.
- All cells come from pre-existing cells.

The study of cells helped prove that all living things increase in number by reproduction. Previously, people believed that living things could grow from dust, sand and so on.

### Career Watch

#### Microbiologist
A **microbiologist** studies microorganisms like bacteria, protozoa, fungi and so on. They study the organisms that cause diseases, and also those that help us.

Microbiologists primarily work in laboratories in universities, research laboratories and hospitals. In hospitals, they identify diseases by recognising the microorganisms that cause them.

# UNICELLULAR AND MULTICELLULAR ORGANISMS

We have already learnt that cells are the basic building blocks of all living organisms. Based on the number of cells present in them, organisms may be divided into two broad groups.

**Unicellular organisms** These organisms are made of only one cell. It is thought that the first organisms that formed on the Earth were unicellular. Unicellular organisms can live and function by themselves. Amoeba, bacteria and so on are examples of unicellular organisms.

**Multicellular organisms** These organisms are made of more than one cell. They were formed later in evolution.

# CELL DIVERSITY

There are many types of cells in the living world. They differ greatly in size and shape.

## Cell Size
Cells are usually very small since this helps them to carry out their functions efficiently. However, there is a vast variety in cell sizes.
- Most cells are very small and can be seen only with a microscope. They are usually measured in **micrometres** or **μm**. (1 micrometre is one-thousandth of a millimetre.) Bacteria are usually up to a few micrometres in size.
- Some cells are very long. For example, some nerve cells in humans which may be up to 1 m long.
- The ostrich egg is one of the largest known cells. It is around 15 cm long.

## Cell Shape
Cells are also found in a variety of shapes. In multicellular organisms, the shapes of cells depend on their functions.
- Many bacteria are **spherical** or round.
- The single-celled organism **Chlamydomonas** is **oval** in shape.
- Red blood cells are **circular and flattened** so that they can move easily through blood vessels.
- Muscle cells are **cylindrical** with either tapering or non-tapering ends.
- Many types of cells in plants are **rectangular** in shape.

# PARTS OF A CELL

All cells have a similar basic structure. Let us first study the structures that are common to both plant cells and animal cells.

## Cell Membrane
Each cell is covered by a thin membrane called the **cell membrane** or the **plasma membrane**. It is a thin, delicate structure that is found in all cells. The cell membrane has two main functions.
- It protects the cell and keeps the contents of the cell contained within the cell.
- It allows only some substances to either enter the cell or leave the cell. For this reason, the cell membrane is called semi-permeable or selectively permeable.

## Cytoplasm
The **cytoplasm** is a viscous liquid found within the cell membrane. It is the place where all major life functions such as respiration and digestion take place.

The cytoplasm has many small structures dispersed through it. These structures are called **organelles**.

### Know more
The cells that Robert Hooke observed using his microscope appeared to be empty since they were dead. Living cells have **protoplasm** inside, in which all the life processes of a cell takes place. The protoplasm consists of the cytoplasm and all the cell organelles.

*membrane: a thin layer that acts as a boundary in a cell*

## Nucleus

The **nucleus** (*plural*: **nuclei**) is a large spherical structure that is usually found in the centre of a cell. It has two main functions.
- It contains all the information that the cell needs to carry out its activities, such as the production of substances, growth and **cell division**. Thus, it is also called the control centre of the cell.
- It stores a special material, DNA, which is passed on from parents to their offspring (young ones).

The main parts of a nucleus are as follows.
- The **nuclear membrane** holds the contents of the nucleus. It has tiny pores that allow substances to enter and leave the nucleus.
- The liquid **nucleoplasm** contains thread-like **chromosomes**, made of DNA, which contain all the information that the cell needs. Each organism has a specific number of chromosomes. For example, all human cells (except the gametes) have 46 chromosomes.
- A nucleus has one or two dense bodies called **nucleoli** (singular: **nucleolus**).

### Vacuoles
**Vacuoles** are spaces in the cytoplasm that are bound by a membrane. Vacuoles contain water and dissolved substances.

In animal cells, vacuoles are very small. Each cell may have many vacuoles. They are usually used to secrete substances.

In plant cells, there is usually only one vacuole, which is large and centrally located. It is used to store water and other substances.

### Other Organelles
As we have developed more powerful microscopes, we have found that cells have many more organelles. Some of them are as follows:
- The **endoplasmic reticulum** transports substances within the cell.
- **Mitochondria** (singular: **mitochondrion**) generate energy from food.
- **Golgi bodies** package and sort substances and send them out of the cell.
- **Ribosomes** produce proteins.
- **Lysosomes** help to digest worn-out cells.
- **Centrosomes** (found only in animal cells) are involved in cell division.

## PARTS PRESENT ONLY IN PLANT CELLS

Plants cells have all the parts that we have studied about till now. In addition, plant cells have some more parts.

### Cell Wall
The **cell wall** is the outermost rigid structure in plant cells that is found outside the cell membrane. It is made of **cellulose**, a complex carbohydrate. The functions of the cell wall are as follows:
- It protects the plant cell from harmful microorganisms and also from injury. However, it allows substances like water to enter freely.
- It gives shape and rigidity to the plant cells. The cell walls act like a skeleton and help the plants to stand upright.

### Plastids
**Plastids** are small organelles in the cytoplasm of plant cells that contain food or pigment. Plastids are of three kinds.

**Chloroplasts** They contain chlorophyll, which is necessary to carry out photosynthesis. They are found in leaves and green stems.

**Fig. 2.11 Chloroplasts in the cytoplasm of plant cells**

**Chromoplasts** They contain other coloured pigments and give colour to flowers and fruits.

**Leucoplasts** They are colourless and store food materials such as starch, proteins and fats in parts like roots and seeds.

# SOME EXAMPLES OF PLANT AND ANIMAL CELLS

## Plant Cells
Most cells in a plant perform one of two main functions. They either make food or they help in the transport of food or water.

## Leaf cells
The main function of leaves is to make food. Hence, leaves have specialised cells that have chloroplasts. Chloroplasts use the energy in sunlight and carbon dioxide to make glucose. This process is called photosynthesis, which you have studied in the previous lesson.

## Cells involved in absorption and transportation
Roots have specialised cells called root hairs, which are elongated and very thin at one end. They spread out in the soil and help the plant absorb water and minerals. The stem has special long cells that help to transport food and water to all parts of the plant.

## Animal Cells
Animal cells are of a vast variety, with each type performing a different function. Let us see some of these cells and their functions.

## Nerve cells
These cells use electric impulses to transmit information from one part of the body to the other.

## Lung cells
These cells help in the exchange of gases between the body and the atmosphere.

## Heart muscle cells
These cells contract and relax repeatedly for the entire lifespan of the animal. They do not rest for even a minute.

# CELL DIVISION

All cells have a fixed life span and hence die. The dead cells need to be replaced with new cells. A young one grows into an adult by adding new cells. This replacement or addition of cells is done through a process called **cell division**. During this process, a cell divides into new cells. Thus, cell division is important for the survival of organisms.
