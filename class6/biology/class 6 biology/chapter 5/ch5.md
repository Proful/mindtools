## HABITATS

You know that the camel lives in the desert, whereas the polar bear lives in the frozen, icy Arctic region. You will not find a camel in the Arctic or a polar bear in a hot desert. Each of them lives in a particular place. All other organisms too usually live in a particular place and not in others.

The **place where an organism usually lives** is called its **habitat**. A habitat includes physical conditions such as sunlight and temperature, non-living things such as soil, air and water, and the living things that share that place. The habitat provides shelter, food, water and everything else that the organism needs to survive and reproduce.

Habitats on the Earth can be broadly divided into two types—**aquatic habitats** and **terrestrial habitats**.

## Eco corner

Humans, as a species, originally developed in Africa. From there, we have now spread to occupy all terrestrial habitats, changing our habits to suit each habitat. Our actions have caused tremendous changes in all of these habitats.

## Aquatic Habitats

Around three-fourths of the Earth’s surface is covered with water. Many plants and animals live in and on water. Aquatic habitats can be divided into marine (oceans and seas) and freshwater habitats (rivers, lakes and so on).

## Terrestrial Habitats
Many plants and animals live on land.
Terrestrial habitats may be natural, such as forests and grasslands, or human-made, such as farms, towns and cities. These habitats are broadly divided into five types.

### Forest habitats
These habitats are rich in biodiversity.¹ They have abundant plant and animal life. Trees are a distinctive feature here. A variety of other organisms, including monkeys and tigers are found here.

### Grassland habitats
These habitats are also rich in biodiversity. Large areas of land are covered with grass here. Lions, gazelles and elephants are found in these habitats.

### Desert habitats
These habitats are found in regions where the rainfall is less than 25 cm a year. The temperatures can vary from very hot in the day to very cold in the night. Cactuses and camels are found in these habitats.

### Mountain habitats
These habitats include hills and mountains. They may be either warm or cold and with or without snow. The temperature also changes with altitude.² The lower areas are warmer than the higher areas. The pine, fir and mountain goats are found in these habitats.

### Polar habitats
These habitats are found in very cold regions near the north and south poles. This region is poor in biodiversity since many organisms do not live here. They are also called cold deserts since they have the same harsh conditions as hot deserts (but are cold instead of hot) and have little life. Mosses, polar bears and penguins are found here.

¹ **biodiversity** the wide variety of living things found in an area

# Career Watch

## Ecologist
An ecologist studies the relationships between organisms, and between an organism and its environment.

Ecologists work with environmental organisations, universities, research institutions and even the government. They study how the activity of humans harms the environment and help humans develop areas with least effect on the environment. They help to protect the environment.

# ADAPTATION

The organisms that live in a habitat have changed over millions of years to suit the place that they live in. This is called **adaptation**. The process by which a living thing gradually changes over a long time to suit the changing environment is called **adaptation**.

---

# Need for Adaptation

Organisms may adapt for the following reasons.

- to find or make food from the available resources
- to protect themselves from predators
- to protect themselves from the environment
- to find favourable conditions for reproduction

All habitats have non-living components called **abiotic factors** such as temperature, humidity, soil and so on. When these abiotic factors change gradually over thousands of years, the biotic factors (living organisms) also have to change or adapt so that they can survive.

Organisms that adapt can survive, while those that are unable to adapt die out and become extinct in that habitat. Different organisms have adapted in different ways and that is why there is such a wide variety of organisms around us.

# Adaptations in Aquatic Plants

Aquatic plants may be divided into three types—floating plants, fixed plants and submerged plants.

---

# Floating plants

These plants are not fixed to the soil under the water. They float on water.

- They have **air spaces** inside their stalks that help them float on water.
- Their leaves have a **waxy coating** to stop them from rotting in water. Stomata are found on the upper surface of the leaves (stomata are usually found on the lower surface).

### Fixed plants
These plants are firmly fixed to the soil under the water but their leaves and flowers float on the surface of the water.
- Their leaves are broad and have a waxy coating. Their stomata are also on the upper surface of the leaves.
- These plants have thin, long, hollow stems to help them to float.
- The water lily and lotus are examples of fixed plants.

### Submerged plants
These plants are totally submerged in water and fixed to the soil.
- The roots are small and thin. They serve to anchor the plant to the soil.
- The leaves are thin and narrow. This helps the leaves avoid being torn due to the flow of water.
- They take in carbon dioxide dissolved in water for photosynthesis and oxygen dissolved in water for respiration.

---

### Adaptations in Aquatic Animals
There are many kinds of animals that live in marine and freshwater environments. Let us learn about some of the most common aquatic animals—fish.
- Fish breathe through **gills**. Gills help the fish take in dissolved oxygen from the water that flows over them. The carbon dioxide produced in the body is also released into the water through the gills. The gills are covered by a thin plate called the **operculum**.

Some fish have a **swim bladder**, which is a sac-like structure filled with air, inside their body. The fish can move up and down, or stay in one place without sinking by controlling the air inside the bladder.

**Know more**

Sharks do not have swim bladders. Thus, sharks have to keep swimming or they will sink to the bottom.

There are many other types of aquatic animals like the whale, octopus, crocodile and so on. Each of these animals has adaptations to suit its habitat.

For example, whales have a streamlined body. Their front limbs are modified into flippers to help them swim. They have lungs and not gills. So, they come up to the surface to breathe. They breathe through a hole at the top of their head called a **blowhole**.

Most deserts are very dry, with minimal rainfall.

# Adaptations in Desert Plants

The cactus is a typical example of a desert plant.

- The cactus stores water in its stem. This stored water is used when needed.
- The leaves of the cactus are reduced to spines to prevent the loss of water through transpiration. Photosynthesis is carried out by the green stem.
- The plant is covered with a waxy coating to reduce the loss of water.
- The roots grow over a wide area to absorb water.

---

The *babul* or *kikar* (acacia) is a desert tree.

- It has small leaves to reduce water loss and spines to protect itself.
- Its roots grow deep in search of water.

# Adaptations in Desert Animals

There are many animals that live in deserts like the camel, fennec fox, meerkat, horned lizard and so on. Let us study the adaptations of the camel.

- The camel has a large **hump** on its back where fat is stored. This stored fat is used to get energy when food is scarce. When the fat is ‘burned’ (used up) in the body, it releases water, which can also be used.
- The camel uses water from its muscles rather than blood when water is scarce. The blood therefore continues to flow normally even when the camel has lost a large amount of water.
- The camel passes very little urine which is very thick. The faeces are also quite dry to save water.
- The camel does not sweat. It can tolerate high temperatures (as high as 42 °C). It can also increase its body temperature to about 42 °C and thus does not have to sweat to stay cool.
- The breathing rate in camels is low. This ensures that very little water vapour is lost during exhalation.
- The feet of the camel have broad, thick, leathery soles which do not sink into the hot sand when walking.
- The long legs of the camel help to keep the body away from the hot sand and fat-filled hump.

### ADAPTATIONS TO MOUNTAIN HABITATS

Mountain habitats may be cold and windy. Some areas of these habitats may be covered with snow for some part of the year or for most of the year.

#### Adaptations in Mountain Plants

Many kinds of trees like the pine, fir, spruce and so on live on mountains. Let us study the adaptations that are seen in these plants.

- The trees are **cone-shaped** to help snow to slide off easily.
- The leaves are **needle-shaped** to conserve water since the trees cannot absorb water from the ground during winter. Also, these leaves allow wind to pass through them and prevent the tree from falling due to the wind.
- These trees have **thick bark** to protect themselves from the cold.

#### Adaptations in Mountain Animals

Many animals like the yak, snow leopard, mountain goat and so on live on mountains. Let us study the adaptations of the mountain goat.

- thick fur
- strong hooves

- The mountain goat has **thick fur** to protect itself from the cold weather. The fur is also light coloured to help it stay hidden from predators.

- The mountain goat has strong hooves to climb easily without slipping.
- Places at high altitudes have thin air with less oxygen than at sea level. To be able to get sufficient oxygen, the blood of animals like the mountain goat has more red blood cells.

Some animals like the bear **hibernate** during winter to conserve energy and stay warm. During hibernation, the body of the bear uses stored food.

### ADAPTATIONS FOR FLIGHT IN BIRDS

Many animals like birds, bats and insects spend a lot of their time in the air. Such animals are called aerial animals. Let us see the adaptations in a bird.

- Wings and feathers
- Streamlined, lightweight body

**Fig. 5.9 A flying bird**

- The bodies of birds are streamlined to help them move through air easily.
- Their forelimbs are modified into wings.
- The body is covered with **feathers**, which help the birds fly and also keep them warm.

- Birds have strong flight muscles that help them move the wings tirelessly.
- The bones of birds are **hollow and light** to keep their body weight low so that flight is easier.
- Birds also excrete thick urine that helps them to save water.

### SPECIAL ADAPTATIONS: AIR PLANTS

Air plants or aerial plants generally grow on other plants for support. Orchids, some kinds of ferns and mosses are examples of air plants.

- Most air plants are found in thick forests where they grow high up on trees to get maximum sunlight for photosynthesis.
- They have aerial roots that absorb moisture from the surrounding air.
- The leaves of some air plants curl up to store rainwater.
