## INTRODUCTION

Much of the life on Earth owes its existence to plants. Visit a park or forest and observe the great variety in the plants there. Plants are of many sizes. The **duckweed** is less than 1 cm in size, while the **giant sequoia** is more than 90 m tall. Some plants produce flowers, while others do not; some have seeds that we can see easily, while others have extremely small seeds.

However, most plants have some common structural features like **roots, stems and leaves**. In addition, flowering plants bear flowers.

---

## THE ROOT SYSTEM

Let us begin with the root system. The parts of the plant that lie within the soil form the root system. Roots grow away from sunlight, towards the soil, and towards water. Root systems may be classified into **taproot systems** (Fig. 1.2) and **fibrous root systems** (Fig. 1.3).

### Taproot System

This system consists of a thick main root called the **primary root**, with many smaller branches called **secondary roots** arising from the primary root. The branches have thin **root hairs** that absorb water and minerals from the soil. **Dicot** plants such as carrot, beetroot and pea plants have a taproot system.

### Fibrous Root System

In this system, a bunch of roots of similar size arise from the base of the stem. These roots have root hairs too. **Monocot** plants such as grass, onion and maize plants have a fibrous root system.

---

### Functions of the Root System

The main functions of the root system are as follows.

* It anchors the plant firmly to the soil. (By doing so, it also holds soil together and stops it from being eroded.)
* Root hairs absorb water and minerals from the soil.
* Some roots act as storage organs and store food. The carrot and beetroot are examples of such roots.

---

## THE SHOOT SYSTEM

The part of the plant above the soil forms the shoot system. It consists of the branches, leaves, flowers and fruits, which are all supported by the stem.

### Based on Venation

If you observe leaves, you will notice that the veins are arranged in two main patterns (**venation**).

Some leaves have veins that run in long lines parallel to each other. This pattern is known as **parallel venation** and is seen mostly in monocot plants such as grass, maize and onions.

Some leaves have veins that branch outwards from the midrib to the leaf margin to form a web-like network. This pattern is known as **reticulate venation** and is seen mostly in dicot plants such as apple trees and mango trees.

## ARRANGEMENT OF LEAVES

For leaves to carry out photosynthesis, they have to be arranged in a way that will maximise their exposure to sunlight. The arrangement of leaves on the stem is called **phyllotaxy**.

- Some plants have leaves arranged in an **alternate** pattern, that is, one leaf arising from each node but arranged in opposite directions to maximise exposure to the sunlight. Peepal leaves are arranged in this way.

- Some plants have two leaves arising from each node but in **opposite** directions. Guava leaves are arranged in this way.

- Some plants have three or more leaves arising from each node and arranged in a circular or **whorled** pattern. Oleander leaves are arranged in this way.

## FUNCTIONS OF THE LEAF

The leaves have three main functions in a plant. They are as follows:
- They make food for the plant through **photosynthesis**.
- They keep the plant cool through the process of **transpiration**.
- They play a major role in the exchange of gases.

### Photosynthesis

The process by which plants make their own food from carbon dioxide and water in the presence of chlorophyll and using the energy in sunlight is called photosynthesis. (photo means ‘light’ and synthesis means ‘putting together’.)

carbon dioxide + water →[chlorophyll + sunlight]→ glucose + oxygen

During photosynthesis, glucose and oxygen are formed.

Since plants can make their own food, they are called **autotrophs** (auto means ‘self’ and troph means ‘nutrition’). This mode of nutrition is called **autotrophic nutrition**.

Photosynthesis is the main reason for the variety of life we see on Earth. Plants not only produce food for themselves; the food they produce is eaten directly or indirectly by animals. Oxygen produced during photosynthesis is used by plants and animals for breathing.

### Transpiration

Only about one-tenth of the water that plants take in is used for photosynthesis. The rest of it is sent out of the plant by transpiration. Transpiration is the loss of water from the plant to the atmosphere through the surface of leaves.

Water along with minerals is absorbed by the roots and transported to the leaves through the stem. In the leaves, the water absorbs heat and turns to water vapour, which is lost to the air through openings called **stomata**. This creates a vacuum due to which more water is absorbed (or pulled in) by the roots. This pull is called **transpirational pull**. As the water continues to evaporate from the leaves, it keeps the plant cool.

### Exchange of Gases

Plants need two main gases, carbon dioxide and oxygen. They take in and give out these gases through stomata.

Plants use carbon dioxide during the day for photosynthesis. The oxygen produced during photosynthesis is given out only during the day since photosynthesis can occur only in the presence of sunlight.

Plants use oxygen to break down food to get energy, for growth, and to produce fruits and flowers. This process is called **respiration**, and it produces carbon dioxide. This carbon dioxide is given out during both day and night since respiration never stops.

## Leaf Modifications for Special Functions

Some leaves are modified to perform special functions in addition to, or other than, photosynthesis. Let us learn about some of these modifications.

### Tendrils for support
Observe a pea plant. It has a weak stem and climbs up a support. Pea plants have leaves that are modified into thin, wiry, coiled structures called **tendrils**. The tendrils coil around the support and firmly hold on to it, giving the stem additional support.

### Spines to reduce loss of water
Look closely at a cactus. The green part is actually the stem. The leaves are reduced to thin spines to reduce loss of water through transpiration in dry conditions. The spines also help to protect the plant from being eaten by grazing animals.

### Scale leaves for protection and storage
Some plants have protective leaves called **scale leaves**. They can be thin and dry as in ginger, or thick and fleshy as in the onion. The scale leaves of ginger protect axillary buds. The thick and fleshy leaves of the onion store food and water.

### Fleshy leaves for storage
Some plants have **thick fleshy leaves** that are used to store water. These plants are called **succulents**. The aloe vera is an example of a plant with thick fleshy leaves.

### Insectivorous plants

The most fascinating leaf modifications are seen in insectivorous or carnivorous plants. Most insectivorous plants grow in places where the soil is deficient in nitrogen. These plants eat insects to get nitrogen.

**Venus flytrap** The midrib acts as a hinge and the edges of the leaves have spines (Fig. 1.16). When an insect sits on the touch-sensitive lamina, the two halves of the leaf snap shut and trap the insect, which is then digested.

**Pitcher plant** The lamina forms a pitcher-like structure with digestive juices (Fig. 1.19). The leaf apex forms the lid of the pitcher. The petiole becomes leaf-like and makes food. When an insect sits on the rim of the pitcher, it slips inside and is digested.

## Vegetative Propagation through Leaves

The process by which a new plant grows from leaves, stems or roots is called vegetative propagation.

Some plants, for example, the Bryophyllum and many succulents can propagate through their leaves. In the case of the Bryophyllum, small plantlets start to grow from the edges of the leaves. These plantlets fall to the ground and grow into individual plants.

**Purple sundew** The lamina has hairs that secrete a sweet sticky liquid. Insects are attracted to the liquid and get caught in it when they sit on the leaf. The lamina also secretes digestive juices which digest the insects.

**Bladderwort** The bladderwort is a water plant. The lamina of the leaf is highly segmented. Some of these segments form sac- or bladder-like structures. When insects and larvae enter the bladders, the latter close and trap their prey. This is the fastest response to stimulus seen among plants.

## The Flower

Plants not only need to grow, they also need to reproduce so that they can multiply to ensure the survival of their kind. This function is carried out by the flowers. Flowers may be of different types—small or large, colourful or white, scented or unscented and so on. The flower is usually the most attractive part of the plant.

To understand how flowers help in reproduction, it is important to understand their structure.

## THE PARTS OF A FLOWER

Take a large gul mohar or silk cotton flower in your hand and observe its parts from the outside to the inside. The flower is attached to the main stem or branch through a **stalk** or **pedicel**. The end of the stalk to which the other parts of the flower are attached is slightly thickened and is called the **thalamus** or **receptacle**. The main parts of the flower are usually arranged in four circles or **whorls**.

**Calyx** This is the outermost whorl. It is made of small, green, leaf-like structures called **sepals** at the base of the flower. The main function of the **calyx** is to protect the flower when it is a bud.

When the flower blooms, the sepals may be visible at the base of the flower or may be fused with the petals.

**Corolla** The most visible parts of the flower are usually the **petals**. They form the next whorl inside the calyx, called the **corolla**. In most flowers, the petals are colourful to attract insects.

**Androecium** The next inner whorl contains the male reproductive parts of the flower. This whorl is called the **androecium** and is made of stamens.

**Gynoecium** The innermost whorl contains the female reproductive parts of the flower. This whorl is called the **gynoecium** and is made of carpels.

Each stamen consists of an **anther**, which contains the male reproductive cells (**gametes**), and a **filament**, which connects the anther to the rest of the flower.

Each carpel has a sticky **stigma**, an **ovary** at the base (which contains **ovules** carrying the female gametes), and a **style** connecting the two.

## TYPES OF FLOWERS

If a flower has all four whorls, it is called a **complete flower**. The hibiscus flower is an example of a complete flower. If any of the whorls are missing, the flower is **incomplete**, for example, maize flowers.

Many flowers have both the androecium and gynoecium. They are called **bisexual flowers**. However, some flowers have only the androecium or only the gynoecium, and are therefore male or female. Such flowers are called **unisexual flowers**. The flowers of the papaya plant are either male or female.

## POLLINATION

Touch the anthers of a newly opened flower such as a hibiscus or lily. You will find a yellow powder sticking to your fingers. This is called **pollen** and is made of **pollen grains**. The pollen grains contain the male gametes.

The process of reproduction starts with **pollination**. Pollination is the transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma.

When pollen from a flower falls on the stigma of the same flower or on the stigma of another flower of the same plant, the process is called **self-pollination**.

When pollen from a flower falls on the stigma of a flower from another plant of the same kind, the process is called **cross-pollination**.

Self-pollination can happen without any outside agent’s help. But the process of cross-pollination requires the help of agents such as insects, wind or water.

## Agents of Pollination

### Insects and animals

Have you seen insects like the honeybee visiting a flower to collect nectar? When they do, the sticky pollen grains stick to their body. When the same insect visits another flower, the pollen grains may fall on the stigma of the new flower. Thus, pollination occurs. Bees, wasps, butterflies, moths, beetles, flies, bats and birds are some animals that pollinate flowers.

Many plants such as the sweet pea and orchids have flowers that are pollinated by insects. Most animal-pollinated flowers are brightly coloured and scented so that they are attractive to the animals.

### Wind

Flowers that are pollinated by wind usually produce pollen grains in large quantities. The pollen grains are light and dry so that they can be carried by the wind easily.

### FERTILISATION

Once the pollen grain has landed on the stigma, a thin tube called the **pollen tube** starts to grow from the pollen grain into the style. This tube carries the male gamete to the ovary. In the ovary, the male gamete fuses with the female gamete (found in the ovule) to form a zygote. The fusing of the male gamete and the female gamete is called **fertilisation**.

# [file name]: 1_14_01_plant_life.jpg

After fertilisation, the following changes are observed:
- The sepals, petals and stamens dry out and fall off.
- The ovary becomes larger and becomes the **fruit**.
- The ovule becomes the **seed**.
- The zygote becomes the **embryo**.

## THE FRUIT

The fruit is the ripened or developed ovary of the flower. Some vegetables that we use in our cooking, such as the cucumber, tomato and pumpkin, are actually fruits.

The strawberry, fig, apple and pear are not real fruits. They develop from parts other than the ovary and are thus called **false fruits**.

### Parts of a Fruit

A fruit can be divided into two main parts—an outer part called the **pericarp** (fruit wall), and the seeds. The pericarp develops from the wall of the ovary and consists of three parts.
- The **epicarp** is the outer skin.
- The **mesocarp** is the fleshy layer.
- The **endocarp** is thin and hard, and contains the seeds.

## Fleshy Fruits and Dry Fruits

If the pericarp is soft and fleshy, as in the mango or tomato, the fruit is called a **fleshy fruit**. However, in some fleshy fruits such as mango, the endocarp is hard and encloses the seed.

If the pericarp is not fleshy but dry, as in the pea, bean or walnut, the fruit is called a **dry fruit**. Many of the food grains that we eat in our daily life, such as rice, wheat and maize, are dry fruits.

Fruits have three main functions.
- They protect seeds from harsh conditions.

## THE SEED

After fertilisation, the ovules in the ovary develop into seeds.

### The Parts of a Seed

Most seeds have three main parts — seed coat, cotyledon and embryo. Based on the number of cotyledons, seeds are of two types — **monocot seeds** and **dicot seeds**. Let us learn about the main parts of a seed.

### Structure of a dicot seed (bean)

A dicot seed has the following parts. (Fig. 1.30)

- The seed coat protects the seed from injury. The seed coat is made of two parts—an outer layer called the **testa** and an inner layer called the **tegmen**.
- The seed is attached to the fruit at an area called the **hilum**. The hilum usually has a different colour than the rest of the seed. (It is sometimes lighter.)

# [file name]: 1_16_01_plant_life.jpg

**Fig. 1.30 Parts of a dicot seed (bean)** (Labels: embryo, micropyle, cotyledon, seed coat)

- Just above the hilum is a small hole or pore called the **micropyle**. This is where the male gamete entered the ovule and the place where water will enter to allow germination to occur.
- Within the seed coat are the **cotyledons**, which store food for the embryo.
- Between the two cotyledons is the embryo. The embryo contains the **radicle** (which grows into the root) and the **plumule** (which grows into the shoot).

Some examples of dicot seeds are the seeds of bean, pea, black gram (**channa**), pumpkin, mango and papaya.

### Structure of a monocot seed (maize)

A monocot seed cannot be opened easily like the dicot seed can be. To study the structure of a monocot seed like maize, soak the seeds in water for a few hours and cut open the seed lengthwise with a sharp blade or knife.

A maize seed has two main sections.
- The broader section is called the **endosperm**. This stores food for the embryo.
- The inner layer is called the **aleurone layer**.
- The endosperm also has a thin layer called the **aleurone layer** surrounding it. The aleurone layer stores some proteins.
- The narrower section, which is the cotyledon, contains the embryo. The embryo has a radicle and a plumule.

In a maize grain, the seed coat is fused with the pericarp and forms a layer around the grain. The fruit and the seed cannot be separated.

Rice, wheat, maize, onion and garlic are examples of plants that produce monocot seeds.

## GERMINATION

Seeds are usually inactive or **dormant**, but have the potential to develop into full-grown plants. Seeds become active when they are exposed to certain conditions. The process by which a seed becomes active and starts to grow into a plant is called **germination**.

---

### Conditions Needed for Germination

Germination of seeds can occur only under some specific conditions. Let us carry out an experiment (Activity 1.5) to study the conditions required for germination.

From Activity 1.5, you can see that air, water and optimum or favourable temperature are the three conditions that are necessary for the germination of seeds. If one of these conditions is not available, the seed will not germinate.

Many chemical processes occur in the seeds during the process of germination. These chemical processes need the oxygen in air, water and a suitable temperature to take place.

Thus, if any of these conditions are absent or present in too large a quantity, germination will not occur. Too much water and too high a temperature will also stop germination from happening.
